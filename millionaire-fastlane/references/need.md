# Commandment of Need — Deep Dive

> "Ninety percent of all new businesses fail within five years, and I know why they fail. They fail because they fail the Commandment of Need." — [MF Ch. 30, p. 207]

The Commandment of Need is the ethical core of the Fastlane. The other four commandments are structural (control, entry, time, scale); Need is about *who the business is for*. DeMarco's position: businesses built on the founder's selfish "why" ("do what you love," "be my own boss," "make money") fail. Businesses that serve real market pain win.

## The selfish-premise audit

Money-chaser red flags [MF pp. 208–209]:
- "How can I make money starting a business?"
- "I'm an expert in [X] so I'll do that."
- "What business can I start with $200?"
- "I want to own a business."
- "I want to be my own boss."
- "I love [X], so I'll open a [X] store."

Every one of these begins with the founder, not the customer. None of them mention a problem being solved.

> "Money isn't attracted to selfish people. It is attracted to businesses that solve problems… Solve needs massively and money massively attracts." — [MF p. 210]

## The marketplace votes with money

> "If you sell 10 million anything, 10 million people have voted that your product will help them." — [MF p. 210]

The dollar-vote principle is how DeMarco resolves the "is this ethical" question — if customers give you money, they're telling you the product helps them. Revenue is validation. No revenue = no need served = no legitimate business.

## "Do what you love" is guru-speak

DeMarco dismantles "do what you love and the money will follow" as one of the most destructive platitudes in personal-finance literature [MF pp. 211–213]:

> "Beware of another guru-speak: 'Do what you love and the money will follow!' Bullshit. That is, unless you want to violate the Commandment of Need. 'Do what you love' is another mythical decree perpetrated by hypocritical gurus and so-called life coaches who are probably three clients away from broke." — [MF p. 211]

Why it fails:
1. **Most things people love are saturated.** American Idol auditions, Etsy shops, photographers, personal trainers — everyone "loves" these, which crowds the market and crushes margins [MF p. 213].
2. **Love is a feeling, not a motivator for hard work.** When the business demands 3 a.m. shifts cleaning bathrooms, love dies. Passion — a bigger *why* — survives.

**The two-condition test:** "For 'do what you love' to work, you need two things: 1) Your love must solve a need and 2) You must be exceptional at it." [MF p. 211]

## Passion ≠ love

> "Passion beats 'do what you love,' because passion fuels motivation for something greater than yourself and is generalized." — [MF p. 214]

DeMarco cites Mike Rowe's *Dirty Jobs* — bovine-manure cleaners, pigeon-droppings removers — owners who had "passionate whys and very deep bank accounts" [MF p. 214]. They didn't love scraping manure. They had a why (kids, a dream, a debt, a mission) big enough to pull them through work nobody would do out of love.

Applied: **ask the user what would pull them to pick up dog-shit at 3 a.m.** That's their passion. The love-of-the-craft answer ("I love coffee, so I'll open a coffee shop") is the death sentence.

## Leslie Walburn — the model case

Leslie Walburn wanted to run a dog-rescue shelter. Instead of opening the shelter (no business model, no scale, no need-solving), she started **an unrelated Fastlane business** to fund the shelter dream [MF p. 217]. Pattern: let passion set the destination; let need-service set the vehicle.

## The "I hate…" signal for open roads

The mirror of Need is the pain list. DeMarco's method for finding opportunity [MF Ch. 36]: listen for the "I hate…" / "I wish there was…" / "why doesn't someone…" phrases in your own life and others'. Every complaint is a potential need. His own Fastlane Forum started this way — an existing forum he liked got overrun by spammers; he opened a clean one [MF p. 200].

## What "solving need" actually looks like

DeMarco's canonical list of need-servers [MF pp. 210–211]. Make millions of people:
- Feel better.
- Solve a problem.
- Get educated.
- Look better.
- Feel secure.
- Feel a positive emotion.
- Satisfy an appetite.
- Have things made easier.
- Have dreams enhanced.

Hit any of these at scale and you are a Fastlaner.

## Violate-Need catalog

- Joe, the martial-arts expert, opening a studio out of passion — $21K/yr profit, closed in 10 months [MF pp. 208, 211].
- Hip-hop apparel store next to a retirement home in Chandler, AZ — closed in 18 months [MF p. 213].
- Car-stereo shop opened by a car-audio enthusiast — saturated market [MF p. 213].
- Personal training services — saturated [MF p. 213].
- The limo driver who came to hate driving — love died on contact with work [MF p. 212].
- Ashlyn Gardner's coffee shop — passion-opened, died from thin margins and owner exhaustion [MF pp. 240–241].
- Money-chasers who rode the mortgage boom and then pivoted to loan-modification when it crashed — never served a real need, just chased money [MF p. 210].

## Ethical boundary

DeMarco's core ethic: "surrender your own selfishness and address the selfishness of others." [MF p. 117] Consumers are selfish by definition — they care only what's in it for them. The producer's job is to stop caring what's in it for them and start caring what's in it for the customer. Wealth is the receipt, not the target.
