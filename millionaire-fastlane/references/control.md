# Commandment of Control — Deep Dive

> "Yes or no. You're either driving the Fastlane or you aren't. You're either in control over your financial plan or you aren't. There is no in between." — [MF Ch. 32, p. 224]

Control is the first commandment because violating it caps every other lever. A Fastlaner must own and drive the business — organization, product, pricing, revenue model, operations. Anything else is hitchhiking on someone else's vehicle.

## The three tiers of income

DeMarco stratifies control into three income brackets [MF p. 225]:

- **Good money: ~$20,000/month** — what hitchhikers earn (top affiliate, star distributor, elite franchisee).
- **Big money: ~$200,000/month** — what drivers earn (the one running the platform, selling the franchise, owning the MLM).
- **Legendary money: $1M+/month** — controllers of all five commandments.

DeMarco's own lived case: a top affiliate on his limo-lead platform pulled $20K/month while DeMarco, the driver, pulled $200K/month on the same program [MF p. 226].

## The driver/hitchhiker test

> "Drivers create MLM companies; they don't join them. Drivers sell franchises; they don't buy them. Drivers offer affiliate programs; they don't join them. Drivers run hedge funds; they don't invest in them. Drivers sell stock; they don't buy stock. Drivers offer drop-shipping; they don't use drop-shipping. Drivers offer employment; they don't get employed. Drivers accept rents and royalties; they don't pay rents and royalties. Drivers sell licenses; they don't buy them. Drivers sell IPO shares; they don't buy them." — [MF p. 225]

Control is **binary**. Every relationship in business has exactly one driver; the rest are hitchhikers whose income the driver can vaporize in a policy change.

## Violation signals

Run the control-audit on any business road:

- Can I change the product without asking? [MF p. 227]
- Can I change the price?
- Can I change the marketing?
- Can I change the compensation structure?
- Am I painting someone else's logo on my car, my body, my website?
- Does my income depend on a "centralized source" whose terms I don't vote on?

If any answer is no, you're not driving.

## Network marketing — the signature violation

DeMarco spends Ch. 32 dismantling MLM because it violates **both** Control AND Entry, and sometimes Need [MF p. 231]:

> "Network marketing is a hitchhiking strategy that disguises itself as an entrepreneurial activity." — [MF p. 229]

The MLM distributor:
- Can't change the comp plan.
- Can't change the product.
- Can't change the pricing.
- Can't prevent the parent company from altering any of the above — and when they do, the downline and residual income vaporize overnight. DeMarco cites a friend who "did well" until his MLM changed its product line and comp plan — income gone in months [MF p. 227].

> "The only people in the company who were living in houses on the Pacific Ocean with stables full of exotic cars were the founders and inner circle — not the distributors who signed up years later." — [MF pp. 229–230]

Brand investment rule: "When you blindly invest your life and time into someone else's brand, you become a part of their marketing plan. You become a swab of paint in their big picture." [MF p. 228] The illustrative case: a woman in a rusted Hyundai in DeMarco's gym parking lot, covered in acai-drink decals, advertising "Make a huge income from home!" [MF p. 228].

## Affiliate & drop-shipping

Both violate Control. The affiliate doesn't own the product, the customer relationship, the conversion funnel, or the pricing. The drop-shipper doesn't own inventory, supplier relationships, or fulfillment. Both can be terminated by policy change.

> "As a driver, I was the one making big money ($200,000/month), while he [the affiliate] settled for good money ($20,000/month)." — [MF p. 226]

## Stockholders & fund investors

Buying stock is hitchhiking. You vote proportional to shares (effectively zero), you have no operational control, and your return is entirely at the mercy of management and market conditions. Drivers *sell* stock (issue IPO shares, run the fund) — they don't buy them. This extends to mutual funds, hedge-fund investors, passive index holders — all hitchhikers.

## Franchisees

A franchisee buys the manual and pays royalties on revenue forever. They can't change the menu, the prices, the suppliers, the hours, the store design. When the franchisor restructures (new uniforms, new POS, new mandated remodel at franchisee expense), the franchisee writes the check. Drivers sell franchises; they don't buy them.

## AdSense publishers

Even this — building a content site monetized by Google AdSense — is hitchhiking. Google is the driver; the publisher's income depends entirely on Google's ad rates, approval status, and algorithm. Countless "AdSense millionaires" vaporized when Google changed its policies [MF p. 226].

## The fix

**Demand the driver's seat.** On any venture, locate the driver — the person who can change terms unilaterally — and become them, or don't play.

Applied to common paths:
- Don't join the MLM — found it.
- Don't buy the franchise — sell one.
- Don't become an affiliate — run the program.
- Don't be the employee — employ.
- Don't borrow — lend.

## Ethical boundary

The Paradox of Practice [MF Ch. 14, pp. 91–93]: never promote or sell a wealth strategy that isn't what made you rich. Most financial gurus teach the Slowlane (save, invest, compound) while themselves operating on the Fastlane (royalties, brand, asset sales). That asymmetry is predatory — the guru monetizes the audience's belief in a plan the guru personally doesn't use.

> "A Paradox of Practice exists when someone promotes a moneymaking strategy but that strategy is not what made him or her rich. In other words, they're not practitioners of their own advice. These people effectively teach one wealth equation (the Slowlane) while they get rich leveraging another (the Fastlane)." — [MF p. 91]
