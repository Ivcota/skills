# Commandment of Time — Deep Dive

> "A business attached to your time is a job." — [MF Ch. 34, p. 241]

The Commandment of Time is the test that separates a Fastlane business from a self-employed grind. If you must be physically present for the business to generate revenue, you've recreated a job — often with worse hours and no employer benefits.

## The four diagnostic questions

From [MF p. 239]:
1. **Can this business be automated and systematized to operate while I'm absent?**
2. **Are my margins thick enough to hire a manager (human-resource system) without killing profitability?**
3. **Can the operation benefit from a money-tree seedling (content, computer, software, distribution)?**
4. **How can I get this business to operate exclusive of my time?**

If the answer to all four is "no" or "not easily," you are staring at a job.

## The five money-tree seedlings

DeMarco's complete list, in his stated order with his explicit passivity grades [MF Ch. "To Break Time Is to Grow a Money Tree", pp. 130–134]. Mnemonic: **RCCDH**.

> "Each system inherently has a grade that rates its level of passivity. A higher grade means a greater potential for passivity, but not necessarily a greater income." [MF p. 130]

1. **Rental Systems** — Passivity Grade **A**. Real estate, equipment, self-storage, licensing — anything where an owned asset earns recurring income. DeMarco calls real estate "Fastlane 1.0 / Wealth 1.0." [MF p. 130]
2. **Computer/Software Systems** — Passivity Grade **A-**. Internet, apps, SaaS, software products. "Computers work 24/7 and they don't bitch." Implicit leverage: a website is accessible to millions, a house on Elm Street is accessible to a few. [MF pp. 131–132]
3. **Content Systems** — Passivity Grade **B+**. Books, music, video, courses, IP — information that replicates cheaply and rides other systems (Internet, distribution). [MF pp. 132–133]
4. **Distribution Systems** — Passivity Grade **B**. Chains, franchises, marketplaces, infomercials, wholesale-to-retail channels. Often hybrids with content or computer seedlings. [MF p. 133]
5. **Human Resource Systems** — Passivity Grade **C**. Agencies, service firms, employee-driven operations. "The most expensive and complicated to run. Humans are unpredictable, expensive, and difficult to control." DeMarco rejected adding HR to his Internet business because it would erode passivity. [MF p. 134]

## Ashlyn's coffee shop — the canonical failure

DeMarco's repeated cautionary tale [MF pp. 240–241]: Ashlyn Gardner opened a coffee shop she loved. She arrived at 4 a.m. to open, stayed until 8 p.m. to close. Margins were too thin to hire a general manager without wiping out her profit. After three years of servitude and no time for family, she closed it. The failure wasn't execution — it was **structure**. Coffee-shop-by-owner is a Commandment-of-Time violation from day one. A single-location service business with thin margins has no path to time-decoupling.

## The passivity grades

DeMarco grades each seedling A through C, where the grade is the *ceiling*, not a guarantee:

| Seedling | Grade | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Rental | A | Owned asset earns with no ongoing labor input |
| Computer/Software | A- | Code runs 24/7; near-zero marginal cost; needs occasional reinvention |
| Content | B+ | Replicates cheaply but needs distribution and refresh cycles |
| Distribution | B | Scales reach but adds operational complexity |
| Human Resource | C | Humans need management; managers need managers |

A business can hybridize seedlings (Amazon = distribution + computer + HR) and the realized passivity is the weighted average minus integration overhead.

## Two root causes of Time violations

[MF p. 241]:

1. **Inaccessible seeds — "the road started with a deficiency."** You chose a business type that structurally cannot be automated (most service businesses that require owner expertise per-transaction).
2. **Infertile soil — "the seeds won't grow."** The margins, operational complexity, or customer expectation prevent the seedling from taking root.

> "Money trees don't grow in the desert." — [MF p. 240]

## The 10-for-60 / 70-for-140 question

DeMarco's preferences-test [MF p. 240]:

> "Would you rather work 10 hours a week and earn $60,000, or work 70 hours a week for $140,000? I'd take the former over the latter every time."

The Fastlane goal is **disconnection of time from income** — even modest income uncoupled from time beats high income coupled to time.

## DeMarco's own examples

**Satisfies Time:**
- The limo-lead website (Ch. 18): ran as a computer system; DeMarco traveled and lived off it for months [MF p. 128–129].
- *The Millionaire Fastlane* itself (content system): ~1,000 hours to write, sold for years after [MF pp. 118, 132–133].
- His single-family rental home (rental system, 95% passive) [MF p. 130].
- His post-exit municipal-bond and foreign-currency yield portfolio — passive capital [MF pp. 137–138].

**Violates Time (rejected):**
- Phoenix airport valet-parking business — ran the numbers, killed it because the 24-employee human-resource system would destroy passivity [MF p. 134].

## Automation-grading any idea

When the user presents a business idea, score it:

| Automatable? | Who delivers value? | Passivity grade |
|---|---|---|
| Yes, fully (software) | Code | 95–100% |
| Yes, with humans | Trained staff | 50–80% |
| Partial | Owner + tools | 20–50% |
| No | Owner only | 0–20% |

Only the top two rows belong on a Fastlane road. The bottom two are jobs in founder costume.

## The "infertile soil" diagnostic

Even a money-tree seedling can fail if:
- Customer expects the owner specifically (celebrity brand).
- Margins won't support a manager at market rate.
- Regulatory or licensing requires the owner's personal presence/credentials.
- Delivery requires one-to-one attention (concierge model at low price).

Fix: **restructure the offering** until margins or product shape permit automation — raise price, productize the service, license the IP, or walk away from the road.
