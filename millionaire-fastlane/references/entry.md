# Commandment of Entry — Deep Dive

> "As entry barriers to any business road fall, or lessen, the effectiveness of that road declines while competition in that field subsequently strengthens." — [MF Ch. 31, p. 220]

## The mechanism

Low-barrier roads attract a herd. The herd creates:
- Oversupply → margins collapse.
- Noise → customer acquisition cost rises.
- Copycats → differentiation disappears.

A $200 MLM distributor kit, a "become an eBay seller in 10 minutes" pitch, a one-click blog signup — all share the same structural flaw. If you can enter in minutes, so can everyone else, and that's the problem.

> "If you could create an eBay business in 10 minutes, guess what? So could millions." — [MF p. 220]

## The "everyone" red flag

> "The best warning flag is 'everyone.' Everyone is a red flag that the Commandment of Entry has been violated." — [MF p. 222]

When media, neighbors, Uber drivers, and cocktail parties are all talking about the same money opportunity, the commandment is broken and the boom is about to bust.

DeMarco's boom roll call [MF pp. 221–222]:
- **Late 1990s:** tech stocks — "everyone" was a day trader.
- **Mid-2000s:** real estate — "everyone" was a house-flipper.
- **Post-2008:** loan modifications — ex-mortgage-brokers "everyone" became loan modifiers.

> "Dumb money — EVERYONE — always shows up at the end of a boom." — [MF p. 223]

The actionable rule: when you spot "everyone is doing it," **get out and stay out.**

## Event vs process

The diagnostic question: **Is entry an event or a process?**

**Event entry** — violates:
- Pay $200, get a distributor kit, you're in (MLM).
- Click "Start selling" on an e-commerce marketplace.
- Register a blog domain.
- Buy a stock.
- Sign up to day-trade.

**Process entry** — satisfies:
- Opening a bed-and-breakfast in Napa Valley: find property → finance → insure → license → permit → hire → 10+ more steps [MF p. 221].
- Starting an internet company in a niche with ~12 competitors (DeMarco's case): find market → build platform → acquire customers one at a time.
- Founding a hedge fund: regulatory approvals, capital raising, infrastructure.
- Launching a licensed medical device, a chartered bank, a patented invention.

Process barriers **filter out tourists** — the 90% who want the upside without the work — and protect your margins.

## Exceptionality as escape hatch

> "If you violate the Commandment of Entry, be prepared to be *exceptional*." — [MF p. 221]

Low-barrier roads (blogging, acting, music, poker, stock trading) can still produce Fastlane wealth — but only for those in the top 0.1% of execution. Most attempts fail.

> "Exceptionality is a long shot, much like an above-average high school athlete going pro." — [MF p. 221]

Translation for the skill: if the user insists on a violated-Entry path (streaming, music, writing), they must (a) accept lottery odds or (b) commit to exceptional execution over a 5–10 year horizon. Do not validate the idea otherwise.

## "Sell shovels, not gold"

DeMarco's gold-rush metaphor [MF p. 221]: when "everyone" is mining gold, the person who gets rich is selling shovels. Applied:

- Poker boom → online poker rooms (brokers) got rich; players mostly didn't.
- Crypto boom → exchanges got rich; retail holders largely didn't.
- Real estate boom → escrow/title companies got rich; flippers got wrecked.
- MLM boom → company founders got rich; distributors didn't.

The shovel-seller is always the driver (Commandment of Control) *and* the process-owner (Commandment of Entry). That overlap is not coincidence.

## Violation catalog

From Ch. 31 directly:
- Joining an MLM — $200 distributor kit [MF p. 220].
- Starting an eBay reseller business [MF p. 220].
- Internet blogging (as a wealth strategy) [MF p. 221].
- Playing professional poker [MF p. 221].
- Retail currency/stock/futures trading [MF p. 221].
- Buying houses during a housing boom [MF p. 222].
- Tech stocks late 1990s [MF p. 222].
- Loan-modification businesses post-2008 [MF p. 222].

## Satisfy-Entry catalog

- Founding the MLM (only the founder satisfies entry) [MF p. 221].
- Opening a B&B in a regulated, property-constrained market [MF p. 221].
- Starting an internet business in a niche with few competitors [MF p. 221].
- Being eBay in the early days, as founder/driver [MF p. 220].
- Anything with capital-intensity, regulatory-intensity, or skill-intensity moats.
