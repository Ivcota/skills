# The Three Interstates — Deep Dive

> "I call the most potent Fastlane roads 'The Three I's,' or 'The Three Interstates,' because they possess the fastest upper speed limits and meet, or can meet, all five Fastlane commandments." — [MF Ch. 35, p. 243]

The Three Interstates are the road categories most likely to score 5/5 on CENTS. Each is an umbrella covering dozens of business models. The most powerful Fastlanes combine all three.

## Interstate 1: The Internet [MF pp. 243–246]

> "The Internet has made more millionaires in the last decade than any other medium out there… The Internet is the shark of the Fastlane." [MF p. 244]

### Why the Internet wins on CENTS by default

Assuming a need-based premise [MF p. 244]:

- **Scale** — Worldwide audience from launch. The unit cap is global.
- **Time** — Systematizes via computer systems; runs while you sleep.
- **Control** — End-to-end ownership of platform, brand, pricing.
- **Entry** — Barriers still high enough to keep out "everyone" (technical skill, capital for ads, distribution savvy).
- **Need** — On you. Internet alone doesn't satisfy Need; only the *right* Internet business does.

### The seven Internet business-model categories [MF pp. 244–246]

| # | Model | What it is | Examples DeMarco gives |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Subscription** | Recurring fee for access to data, info, software | RealtyTrac, LoopNet; the uptime-monitoring service he calculated at ~$1M MRR |
| 2 | **Content** | Free content + advertising to reach eyeballs | Online news, niche blogs, the Fastlane Forum itself |
| 3 | **Lead Generation** | Aggregate fragmented industries; sell leads | Limos.com (DeMarco's own); plastic surgeons; not airlines (too consolidated) |
| 4 | **Social Networks** | Aggregate communities by tribe | Facebook, MySpace, LinkedIn |
| 5 | **Brokerage** | Match buyers + sellers; earn per transaction | PayPal, Elance, CarsDirect, Travelocity |
| 6 | **Advertising** | Match buyers + sellers; earn on ad fees instead | His limo-listing site; eBay (mixed); Google/Yahoo (mixed) |
| 7 | **E-commerce** | Sell goods/services/info online | Amazon, CSN Stores; small retailers expanded worldwide; e-books |

**Asset value bonus:** Internet businesses are valued not just on net profit but on traffic metrics. Many sites sell for billions with no profit. "Traffic, or visitors to a Web site, also has a boundless upside scale." [MF p. 246]

## Interstate 2: Innovation [MF pp. 246–248]

> "Innovation involves two acts: 1) Manufacture and 2) Distribution." [MF p. 246]

### What "Innovation" actually means

Not necessarily inventing the lightbulb. Often it's *taking something old and stale and making it better, or repackaging the unconventional as conventional* [MF p. 247]. The skull-shaped vodka bottle. The Snuggie (40M units sold of a blanket-with-arms concept that had existed for years).

### Categories of innovation products [MF p. 247]

- **Food** — beer, barbecue sauce, cookies, secret recipes
- **Household** — robotic vacuums, tools, hangers
- **Health & vitality** — vitamins, herbs, energy drinks/bars, "male enhancement formulas"
- **Information** — books, magazines, subscription newsletters
- **Personal** — clothing, purses, shoes, gloves
- **Automotive** — accessories, add-ons, stick-ons

### Manufacture is half the battle; distribution is the other half

> "A great product is worthless if it doesn't get into the hands of people, and that requires distribution." [MF p. 247]

Distribution channels DeMarco names:
- **Infomercial** — sell via mass media
- **Retail** — sell to distributors and wholesalers (Walmart, Target supply chain)
- **Direct marketing** — print media, postal mail, Internet
- **Network marketing** — distributor armies (you create the MLM, you don't join one)

The book itself is DeMarco's worked example: he manufactured (wrote, edited, published) and now distributes (Amazon, book wholesalers, the Internet). "Writing a book is not a business; selling the book is." [MF p. 247]

### Authoring as a Fastlane

> "Have you ever wondered why people sell get-rich-quick books and yet the content is just regurgitated blather from 30 prior books? The authors know that authoring is a potent Fastlane." [MF p. 247]

Books satisfy CENTS structurally — content system (Time), worldwide habitat (Scale), author owns (Control), publishing process (Entry), and *if* the book serves a real need (Need).

## Interstate 3: Intentional Iteration (II) [MF pp. 248–249]

> "Iteration is: 'the means or act of repeating a process, usually with the aim of approaching a desired goal or target or result.'" [MF p. 248]

### The honest caveat

> "Intentional iteration is a potent Fastlane but it offers the greatest challenge because it really doesn't satisfy all five commandments, but four. The process of intentional iteration is the act of satisfying the final commandment, scale." [MF p. 248]

II is the *workaround* for businesses that score 4/5 on CENTS but fail Scale because their unit habitat is local. You repeat the unit until reach is achieved.

### Worked examples

| Singles-based unit | Iterated to home run |
|---|---|
| One single-family rental | 50 single-family rentals |
| One sandwich shop ("bought a job") | 300-unit franchise system |
| One candy kiosk | 200 kiosks across 50 malls |
| One coffee shop (Ashlyn's failure) | A coffee chain with managers and brand |
| One dental practice | A multi-location DSO |

### The two iteration mechanisms

DeMarco names two paths to scaling a singles unit [MF p. 248]:

1. **Human resource systems** — hire trained staff to operate replicated units
2. **Repeated successes** — chain locations, franchise the brand, license the system

> "The Fastlane franchising premise is to build a local business defined by systematic processes, then franchise the concept nationally or worldwide. The iterator's goal is to replicate and sell a concept, a brand, and a system and remove himself from operations." [MF p. 248]

### The iteration test

> "A popular thread at my forum is titled 'Is a candy kiosk Fastlane?' A forum user wanted to know if having automated candy kiosks in the mall constitutes a Fastlane plan. As a standalone, no. But with II? Fastlane baby!" [MF p. 248]

Any business that asks "is this Fastlane?" and the honest answer is "only if I run 200 of them" — that's an Intentional Iteration play. The decision becomes: do you have the operational capacity, capital, and brand to actually iterate? Most don't. Those who do build empires.

## Combining the Interstates

The most powerful Fastlanes layer multiple Interstates:

- **SaaS** = Internet + Innovation (the software is the innovation; the Internet is the distribution)
- **A book + a course + a forum** = Innovation + Internet + (potentially) II via course-as-product
- **Franchised e-commerce brand** = Internet + II
- **A productized service** = Innovation + II + (often) Internet

> "Each interstate road is an umbrella for dozens of other roads. Put all three together and you have hundreds of roads available for your travel." [MF p. 243]

## Chapter summary [MF p. 249]

- The best Fastlanes satisfy all five Commandments: Control, Entry, Need, Time, and Scale.
- Assuming a need-based premise, the **Internet is the fastest interstate**, because it overwhelmingly satisfies all Commandments.
- **Innovation** can be any variety of open roads: authoring, inventing, or services.
- Inventing success needs coupling with distribution.
- A singles-based business is scaled to a home-run business by **intentional iteration**. With iteration, scale is conquered.
