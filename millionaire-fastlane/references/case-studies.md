# Case Studies — Worked Examples from *The Millionaire Fastlane*

Every example below appears in the book. Dollar figures and outcomes are DeMarco's own stated numbers; no invented values. Composite cases (DeMarco's teaching constructs) are flagged.

## MJ DeMarco's own businesses

- **Limo-dispatch / lead-generation website** (late 1990s) — spotted an unmet need while driving limos (no central directory for out-of-town bookings), built a web directory, pivoted to selling leads at ~$4 per conversion. Scaled to worldwide reach via Internet habitat. Illustrates the full Fastlane equation and Potent Fastlane #1 (Internet — Lead Generation). [MF pp. 13–16, 121–122, 245]
- **Two sales of the same company** — sold ~$1.2M in 2000 (kept <$300K after taxes and bad stock investments); bought it back at $250K firesale; grew and sold again in 2007 for millions. Illustrates asset value + liquidation events. [MF pp. 15–17, 126]
- **Phoenix airport valet-parking** (evaluated and rejected) — ran the numbers, killed it because 24-employee HR system would destroy passivity. Illustrates the Commandment of Time check. [MF p. 134]
- **Writing *The Millionaire Fastlane*** — ~1,000 hours; content system that survives time; Amazon and distributors as distribution systems. "Writing a book is not a business; selling the book is." [MF pp. 118, 132–133, 247]
- **Single-family rental home** — tenant pays monthly; 95% passive. Rental-system seedling. [MF p. 130]
- **Fastlane Forum** — opened when an existing entrepreneur forum DeMarco liked was overrun by spammers. Illustrates the "I hate…" signal for finding open roads. [MF pp. 200–201]
- **Failed early ventures** — vitamins, jewelry, turnkey mail-order kits, long-distance MLM. All failed. Infomercial-marketer's dream / money-chaser pattern. [MF pp. 9–10, 198]
- **Corvette purchase after first sale** — bought to "look rich"; lost ~$900K total (taxes + bad stock options + liquid asset erosion). Lifestyle-servitude trap. [MF p. 15]
- **Post-exit passive portfolio** — municipal bonds, treasuries, foreign-currency yields. Applied compound interest to preserve wealth, not build it. [MF pp. 137–138, 141]

## Web / software / content (Fastlane exemplars)

- **Nicholas (iPhone game developer)** — taught himself iOS on free sites while working full-time at Sun Micro and raising a 1-year-old. "$600,000 in a single month with a single iPhone game." [MF pp. 131–132]
- **J.K. Rowling** — divorced English teacher turned content mogul. Sold 30M+ Harry Potter books in 35 languages. [MF p. 132]
- **Dan Brown** — sold 80M copies of *The DaVinci Code* in 51 languages. [MF p. 132]
- **Bill Gates (Microsoft) / Larry Ellison (Oracle)** — software replication margins, worldwide habitat. [MF p. 131]
- **Mark Zuckerberg (Facebook)** — pivoted from college-only network to mass-market; "the world steered the plan." Ch. 28 Redline commitment; Ch. 39 business-plan-is-useless. [MF pp. 196, 271]
- **Pierre Omidyar (eBay)** — started as hobbyist flash sale; execution turned it into a brokerage platform. [MF p. 267]
- **Brin & Page (Google)** — entered a crowded search field (Yahoo, AltaVista, Snap) and out-executed. "Skip the big idea and go for the big execution." [MF p. 251]
- **J. Darius Bikoff (Glacéau Vitamin Water)** — founded 1996; Coca-Cola offered $4.1B 11 years later. [MF p. 23]
- **Sheila Hinton (composite)** — 24-year-old computer tech scaled virus-cleaning business into 27 states, $2.9M profit, sold for $24M. Illustrates Asset Value = Net Profit × Industry Multiplier. [MF p. 123]
- **John Twitnuts (composite, cautionary)** — turned down a $640M offer for pre-revenue social site; fad faded; fire-sold for $2.5M. Liquidation-event timing. [MF pp. 126–127]
- **RealtyTrac, LoopNet** — subscription-based Internet models. [MF p. 244]
- **PayPal, Elance, CarsDirect, Travelocity** — brokerage platforms earning per-transaction fees. [MF p. 245]

## Retail / consumer / inventions

- **Spanx-style undergarment founder** — 20M units of body-shaping undergarments. Innovation interstate. [MF p. 112]
- **Snuggie** — repackaged an existing product into infomercial mass-market; 40M sold. Distribution mastery. [MF pp. 247, 252]
- **Apple vs. PC** — exploited PC viruses/spyware/update-nags to build differentiated brand. Brand as defense to commoditization. [MF p. 295]
- **Coke vs. Pepsi** — loyalty belongs to brand, not product. [MF p. 295]
- **FedEx, M&M's, Domino's USPs** — "When it absolutely positively has to be there overnight" / "melts in your mouth, not in your hand" / "30 minutes or less or it's free." USP anchors brand. [MF p. 296]
- **Wal-Mart (Sam Walton)** — entered decades-old department-store space and won via execution. [MF p. 251]
- **Starbucks (Howard Schultz)** — coffee existed 1,000 years; added brand/ambiance/emotion. [MF pp. 133, 251]
- **Dogfish Head (Sam Calagione)** — started 1995 with 10-gallon home brew kit. Old product, better execution. [MF p. 252]
- **1-800-GOT-JUNK (Brian Scudamore) / Waste Management (Wayne Huizenga)** — "garbage" — oldest need on earth — became Fortune 500 and national franchise. [MF p. 252]

## Real estate / rental

- **DeMarco's single-family rental home** — 95% passive, tenant pays monthly. [MF p. 130]
- **Self-storage with automated kiosks** (unnamed Fastlane Forum member) — hybrid rental + computer system; 85% passive. Adding HR would drop passivity. [MF p. 134]

## MLM / network marketing (negative examples)

## Franchises

- **Jillian** (composite) — Wall Street adviser bought a deli franchise; 13-year career; liquidated half her 401K; 7-day workweeks; margins and royalties made hiring a manager impossible; closed in 2 years and returned to a 9-5. Franchise as "business masquerading as a job." [MF pp. 129–130]
- **Friend considering one coffee franchise** — DeMarco advised against; a single franchise has no Effection access; would need 20+ to scale. [MF pp. 235–236]
- **Starbucks / Dairy Queen / McDonald's (Ray Kroc) / Subway** — chaining/franchising as a distribution system WHEN YOU OWN IT. [MF pp. 133, 251]
- **Candy-kiosk Fastlane Forum question** — "Is a candy kiosk Fastlane?" One kiosk = no. 200 kiosks in 50 malls = yes, via Intentional Iteration. [MF p. 248]

- **Andy, bank-collections agent** — hates job M–F; survives for weekend boating. "Do what you hate for do what you love." [MF p. 212]
- **Pauline, Jose, Janice, Gary** — online knitting / car-stereo / gallery sculpture / personal training. All derivatives of "do what you love"; saturated markets, thin margins, contaminated passion. [MF pp. 212–213]
- **Leslie Walburn** (Fastlane Forum reader, **positive**) — passion for animals / no-kill shelter. Started an *unrelated* Fastlane business to fund the dog-rescue dream. Passion fuels the road; the road need not be the dream. [MF pp. 216–217]

## Other illustrative cases

- **Lonnie Scruggs** — real-estate investor; two jobs → realized more education was the answer; self-taught to financial freedom. Real-world education. [MF p. 190]
- **Randy Pausch (Last Lecture)** — "brick walls filter those who don't want it badly enough." Redline commitment. [MF pp. 196–197]
- **Chuma & Azur** (Egyptian pyramid parable) — Azur lifts stones for 40+ years (job = manual labor); Chuma builds the machine (business system), 3 years to build, 5 years to reap. Fastlane = system vs. Slowlane = labor. [MF pp. 112–115]
- **AARP 2002 survey** — 69% expected to work past retirement age. Slowlane failure rate. [MF p. 99]
- **Harrison Group survey of 3,000 pentamillionaires** — almost all got rich via big lump sum after years; 80% started own business or worked for fast-growth small company. Asset value beats 10% paycheck saving. [MF pp. 122–123]
- **Gary the limo-client** (late-1990s passenger) — semi-retired 20-something who sold an admin-office company for millions; lived off municipals and treasuries. Savers become lenders. [MF p. 136]
- **The Rio vs. The Venetian hotels** (DeMarco's Vegas stay) — fancy Venetian had terrible staff; dated Rio had great staff. Architecture can't overcome poor human interaction. [MF pp. 288–289]
- **Liquid Web hosting** — resolved DeMarco's support ticket in 10–20 min; turned him into an evangelist. SUCS — Superior Unexpected Customer Service. [MF pp. 279–280]
- **Warren Buffett, early 2009** — bought while "everyone" was selling. Lone contrarian when the Rule of Everyone signals a top/bottom. [MF p. 223]
