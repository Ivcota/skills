# Zero to One — Case Studies Reference

Consolidated cases from Peter Thiel's *Zero to One* (2014), organized by the SKILL.md framework sections. Every case carries a direct quote and an L#### line citation into `source.txt` (format: `Z21 L####`).

---

## 1. The Contrarian / 0→1 examples

### PayPal — Contrarian founding
**Thiel's use:** The running 0→1 case — building a globally unique payments product while the dot-com herd ran in circles.
> "When I was running PayPal in late 1999, I was scared out of my wits—not because I didn't believe in our company, but because everyone else in the Valley seemed to believe in anything at all."
— Z21 L329-377

**Lesson:** Contrarian conviction is what makes a 0→1 bet look crazy to the crowd and obvious to the founder.

## 2. Monopoly vs Competition

### U.S. Airlines — The commodity baseline
**Thiel's use:** Opening contrast to set up monopoly vs. competition. Massive value created, almost none captured.
> "U.S. airline companies serve millions of passengers and create hundreds of billions of dollars of value each year. But in 2012, when the average airfare each way was $178, the airlines made only 37 cents per passenger trip."
— Z21 L447-451

**Lesson:** Creating value is not enough — if you can't capture it, you're running a charity for your customers.

### Google — The canonical monopoly
**Thiel's use:** The paired half of the airline contrast. Same economy, radically different economics.
> "Google brought in $50 billion in 2012 (versus $160 billion for the airlines), but it kept 21% of those revenues as profits — more than 100 times the airline industry's profit margin that year."
— Z21 L451-455

**Lesson:** Profit margins, not revenue, tell you whether you've built a monopoly or a treadmill.

### Google — Monopoly lies
**Thiel's use:** How monopolists hide. Frame the market wide enough and any monopoly looks like a fair competitor.
> "Think about how Google talks about its business. It certainly doesn't claim to be a monopoly. But is it one? Well, it depends: a monopoly in what?"
— Z21 L513-536

**Lesson:** Watch the denominator. 68% of search, 3.4% of global ads, 0.24% of global tech — same company, three different stories. A monopoly that has to lie about itself is still a monopoly.

### British-food-restaurant lie — Competitor's inverse
**Thiel's use:** The mirror image of monopoly-lies — small players exaggerate uniqueness to feel safe from competition.
> "Think about how Google talks about its business. It certainly doesn't claim to be a monopoly. But is it one? Well, it depends: a monopoly in what?"
— Z21 L513-536

**Lesson:** Non-monopolists narrow the market in their heads; monopolists widen it. Both lies are told to avoid scrutiny.

## 3. Rivalry & destructive competition

### PayPal vs X.com — The feature war that nearly ended both
**Thiel's use:** Rivalry as existential threat. Two strong teams burning themselves down over mirror-image products.
> "In February 2000, Elon and I were more scared about the rapidly inflating tech bubble than we were about each other... in early March we met on neutral ground... and negotiated a 50-50 merger."
— Z21 L846-862

**Lesson:** When you and your rival are locked into feature-for-feature combat, merging beats winning.

### Oracle vs Siebel vs Informix — Rivalry as theater
**Thiel's use:** Extended Shakespearean set piece showing how personal rivalry turns into operational distraction.
> "Ellison sent truckloads of ice cream sandwiches to Siebel's headquarters to try to convince Siebel employees to jump ship. The copy on the wrappers? 'Summer is near. Oracle is here.'"
— Z21 L816-845

**Lesson:** Rivalry makes you mimic your enemy instead of serve your customer. Informix's CEO ended up in federal prison; the distraction was complete.

### Google vs Microsoft — Romeo & Juliet of tech
**Thiel's use:** While the two titans fought OS vs OS, search vs search, browser vs browser, the real winner walked past them.
> "Windows vs. Chrome OS, Bing vs. Google Search, Explorer vs. Chrome... Apple came along and overtook them all. In January 2013, Apple's market capitalization was $500 billion, while Google and Microsoft combined were worth $467 billion."
— Z21 L767-783

**Lesson:** The price of obsessing over your nearest competitor is that a third player redefines the game entirely.

## 4. Four Monopoly Characteristics

### PayPal — Proprietary technology (10x)
**Thiel's use:** Textbook 10x improvement in user experience.
> "PayPal, for instance, made buying and selling on eBay at least 10 times better. Instead of mailing a check that would take 7 to 10 days to arrive, PayPal let buyers pay as soon as an auction ended."
— Z21 L979-983

**Lesson:** A 10x improvement is the minimum dosage for a proprietary-tech moat — anything less, the incumbent absorbs you.

### Apple iPad — Design as 10x
**Thiel's use:** Proves 10x need not be raw spec — design can vault a category from "unusable to useful."
> "Then Apple released the iPad. Design improvements are hard to measure, but it seems clear that Apple improved on anything that had come before by at least an order of magnitude: tablets went from unusable to useful."
— Z21 L993-1000

**Lesson:** Integrated design is a measurable moat even when the axes are qualitative.

### Amazon — 10x catalog breadth
**Thiel's use:** Scale of selection as the original 10x wedge.
> "Amazon made its first 10x improvement in a particularly visible way: they offered at least 10 times as many books as any other bookstore... Bezos's founding vision was to dominate all of online retail, but he very deliberately started with books."
— Z21 L984-1121

**Lesson:** Pick a dimension the incumbent can't 10x back on, then let the catalog compound.

### Facebook — Network effects
**Thiel's use:** Canonical network-effect product. Harvard-only was a feature, not a limitation.
> "Facebook started with just Harvard students—Mark Zuckerberg's first product was designed to get all his classmates signed up, not to attract all people of Earth."
— Z21 L1016-1020

**Lesson:** Network effects compound only after density; chase a narrow group hard before broadening.

### Twitter — Economies of scale
**Thiel's use:** Product built so each added user costs essentially nothing.
> "Twitter already has more than 250 million users today. It doesn't need to add too many customized features in order to acquire more, and there's no inherent reason why it should ever stop growing."
— Z21 L1035-1037

**Lesson:** Design for scale from the start; bespoke servicing is the tax a monopoly refuses to pay.

### Apple — Brand on substance
**Thiel's use:** Strongest tech brand, but propped up by proprietary tech and network effects underneath.
> "Today's strongest tech brand is Apple: the attractive looks and carefully chosen materials of products like the iPhone and MacBook..."
— Z21 L1040-1061

**Lesson:** Brand alone is hollow (see late-period Yahoo); brand on substance is the fourth pillar of monopoly.

## 5. Start Small & Monopolize / Last Mover

### PayPal PalmPilot — Negative example
**Thiel's use:** Concrete "start small" lesson by showing the failure mode first — beaming money to nobody.
> "Our first product let people beam money to each other via PalmPilots... Nobody needed our product, so we had no customers. With that lesson learned, we set our sights on eBay auctions..."
— Z21 L1084-1095

**Lesson:** "Everyone, everywhere" is a nowhere. Find the dense small market.

### PayPal / eBay PowerSellers — The right niche
**Thiel's use:** The pivot into a 20,000-person market with high money velocity.
> "We needed a smaller niche market segment with a higher velocity of money—a segment we found in eBay 'PowerSellers'... There were 20,000 of them."
— Z21 L2794-2805

**Lesson:** Niche quality = size × density × frequency of use — not size alone.

### Amazon — Sequenced expansion
**Thiel's use:** After books, the deliberate walk outward: CDs, videos, software, then general retail.
> "Amazon made its first 10x improvement in a particularly visible way: they offered at least 10 times as many books as any other bookstore... Bezos's founding vision was to dominate all of online retail, but he very deliberately started with books."
— Z21 L984-1121

**Lesson:** Plan the whole empire, but take the first territory first.

### eBay — Beanie Baby beachhead
**Thiel's use:** Auction dynamics worked perfectly for obsessive collectors before extending to general buyers.
> "When it launched its auction marketplace in 1995, it didn't need the whole world to adopt it at once; the product worked well for intense interest groups, like Beanie Baby obsessives."
— Z21 L1122-1136

**Lesson:** Start where the product mechanic is magic, not where the market is biggest.

### PayPal — Discounted cash flow
**Thiel's use:** Quantitative justification for the last-mover valuation argument.
> "When I projected our future cash flows, I found that 75% of the company's present value would come from profits generated in 2011 and beyond."
— Z21 L913-919

**Lesson:** Real tech value lives a decade out. If you can't survive long enough to collect it, the valuation is fiction.

### Twitter vs New York Times — Last-mover math
**Thiel's use:** Stark present-value contrast validating last-mover logic.
> "When Twitter went public in 2013, it was valued at $24 billion—more than 12 times the Times's market capitalization—even though the Times earned $133 million in 2012 while Twitter lost money."
— Z21 L885-895

**Lesson:** Markets price durability, not this quarter's profit. Being last > being first, if last means lasting.

## 6. Definite Optimism

### Facebook / Yahoo $1B offer — Definite founder move
**Thiel's use:** Zuckerberg's 10-minute rejection is the crown jewel of definite-founder behavior.
> "Mark Zuckerberg walked into the board meeting and announced: 'Okay, guys, this is just a formality, it shouldn't take more than 10 minutes. We're obviously not going to sell here.'"
— Z21 L1615-1621

**Lesson:** A definite plan makes any indefinite acquirer's offer look small, because you know what you're giving up.

### Apple / Jobs — Definite multi-year planning
**Thiel's use:** Jobs as designer-of-business, with the iPod as opening move of a post-PC plan.
> "The greatest thing Jobs designed was his business. Apple imagined and executed definite multi-year plans to create new products and distribute them effectively."
— Z21 L1596-1609

**Lesson:** MVP culture is indefinite optimism in disguise. The greatest companies design the business itself, years ahead.

### Buffett / Bezos / Gates — Lottery-ticket rebuttal (by their own words)
**Thiel's use:** Lineup of successful people attributing success to luck — Thiel treats this as indefinite-optimist humility theater.
> "Warren Buffett has famously claimed to be a 'member of the lucky sperm club' and a winner of the 'ovarian lottery.' Jeff Bezos attributes Amazon's success to an 'incredible planetary alignment'..."
— Z21 L1197-1207

**Lesson:** Don't believe the luck narrative even from people who built definite plans. It's indefinite optimism wearing modesty.

### Jobs / Dorsey / Musk — Serial founders rebut luck
**Thiel's use:** Serial multi-billion-dollar founders are statistical evidence against the lottery-ticket theory.
> "A few, like Steve Jobs, Jack Dorsey, and Elon Musk, have created several multibillion-dollar companies. If success were mostly a matter of luck, these kinds of serial entrepreneurs probably wouldn't exist."
— Z21 L1207-1211

**Lesson:** If luck dominated, repeat winners would be impossible. They exist because design does.

## 7. Power Law

### Founders Fund 2005 — Facebook > everything else combined
**Thiel's use:** Empirical shape of VC returns from his own book. One bet dominates the fund.
> "Facebook, the best investment in our 2005 fund, returned more than all the others combined. Palantir, the second-best investment, is set to return more than the sum of every other investment aside from Facebook."
— Z21 L1708-1711

**Lesson:** Distribution of outcomes is not normal. Concentrate on the few bets that could return the fund.

### Andreessen Horowitz / Instagram — 312x isn't enough
**Thiel's use:** Home-run math: a $250K→$78M return still only 5% of what A16Z needed.
> "Andreessen Horowitz invested $250,000 in Instagram in 2010. When Facebook bought Instagram just two years later for $1 billion, Andreessen netted $78 million—a 312x return in less than two years."
— Z21 L1721-1728

**Lesson:** A $1.5B fund needs 19 Instagrams at that check size just to break even. Either concentrate or lose to the power law.

## 8. Secrets

### Hewlett-Packard — From secret-seeking to gossip
**Thiel's use:** Extended cautionary tale. The arc of a company that stopped believing in secrets.
> "The sad decline of Hewlett-Packard provides a cautionary tale... Having abandoned the search for technological secrets, HP obsessed over gossip. As a result, by late 2012 HP was worth just $23 billion."
— Z21 L1989-2020

**Lesson:** 1991-2000 invention era: $9B → $135B. 2000s gossip era: back to $23B. Secrets compound; politics rots.

### Airbnb / Uber / Lyft — Spare-capacity secrets
**Thiel's use:** Evidence secrets still exist — hidden value sitting on couches and in private cars.
> "The sad decline of Hewlett-Packard provides a cautionary tale... Having abandoned the search for technological secrets, HP obsessed over gossip. As a result, by late 2012 HP was worth just $23 billion."
— Z21 L1989-2020

**Lesson:** Look for value trapped under dormant conventions — the best secrets often hide in plain social sight.

### Nutrition — Unstandardized field
**Thiel's use:** Nutrition as an example of an entire domain where the current consensus is probably wrong.
> "The sad decline of Hewlett-Packard provides a cautionary tale... Having abandoned the search for technological secrets, HP obsessed over gossip. As a result, by late 2012 HP was worth just $23 billion."
— Z21 L1989-2020

**Lesson:** Pick a field no one has fully professionalized. The secret isn't hidden — it's unsorted.

## 9. Foundations & Mafia

### PayPal Mafia — Culture as compounding asset
**Thiel's use:** The paradigm case that relationships and shared culture outlive v1.
> "Since then, Elon Musk has founded SpaceX and co-founded Tesla Motors; Reid Hoffman co-founded LinkedIn; Steve Chen, Chad Hurley, and Jawed Karim together founded YouTube... Today all seven of those companies are worth more than $1 billion each."
— Z21 L2391-2402

**Lesson:** Pick co-founders you'd want to start your next company with; the mafia matters more than the liquidity event.

### General Motors — Misalignment by ownership
**Thiel's use:** Classic Foundations example of ownership/possession misalignment in a big incumbent.
> "The CEO of a huge company like General Motors, for example, will own some of the company's stock, but only a trivial portion of the total. Therefore he's incentivized to reward himself through the power of possession rather than the value of ownership."
— Z21 L2244-2254

**Lesson:** Without real ownership, executives optimize for possession — cost cuts, stock buybacks, optics — not long-term value.

### Microsoft / Bill Gates — Cost of losing the founder
**Thiel's use:** The DOJ antitrust case stripped Gates from the company's critical path and tipped Microsoft into stagnation.
> "Microsoft's Windows claimed a 90% share of the market for operating systems in 2000... Gates's enemies had already deprived his company of the full engagement of its founder, and Microsoft entered an era of relative stagnation."
— Z21 L3667-3687

**Lesson:** Founders are foundational. Removing them — by lawsuit, board coup, or fatigue — is a silent capital destruction event.

### Apple / Steve Jobs — Return of the founder
**Thiel's use:** The inverse of Microsoft: Jobs returns in 1997 when Apple is near zero, drives the iPod/iPhone/iPad era.
> "That year Michael Dell famously said of Apple, 'What would I do? I'd shut it down and give the money back to the shareholders.' Instead Jobs introduced the iPod (2001), the iPhone (2007), and the iPad (2010)..."
— Z21 L3697-3714

**Lesson:** A returning founder with a definite plan can resurrect a company the market has written off.

## 10. Distribution

### SpaceX — Complex sales
**Thiel's use:** Musk as "sales grandmaster" — hauling a startup rocket into billion-dollar NASA contracts against a 500k-employee legacy lobby.
> "Within just a few years of launching his rocket startup, Elon Musk persuaded NASA to sign billion-dollar contracts to replace the decommissioned space shuttle."
— Z21 L2655-2665

**Lesson:** For 7-figure+ deals, the founder is the sales team. No one else has the authority to close.

### Palantir — Complex sales without salespeople
**Thiel's use:** Inverse of the conventional enterprise motion: no sales org, CEO on the road 25 days a month.
> "Palantir, the data analytics company I co-founded with my law school classmate Alex Karp, doesn't employ anyone separately tasked with selling its product... Our deal sizes range from $1 million to $100 million."
— Z21 L2666-2672; L2969-2980

**Lesson:** At the top of the deal-size range, selling is leadership. Outsourcing it is the first sign you won't close.

### Box — Personal sales at SMB scale
**Thiel's use:** Illustration of "start inside the account." Stanford Sleep Clinic → Stanford researchers → Stanford-wide → Stanford Hospital.
> "In 2009, Blake sold a small Box account to the Stanford Sleep Clinic... If it had started off by trying to sell the president of the university on an enterprise-wide solution, Box would have sold nothing."
— Z21 L2689-2702

**Lesson:** In personal sales, the president isn't the entry point. The sleep-clinic researcher is.

### ZocDoc — Distribution as product
**Thiel's use:** The salesforce isn't just a channel — every new doctor makes the product more valuable to consumers.
> "ZocDoc needs lots of salespeople—so many that they have an internal recruiting team to do nothing but hire more. But making personal sales to doctors doesn't just bring in revenue; by adding doctors to the network, salespeople make the product more valuable to consumers."
— Z21 L2703-2714

**Lesson:** In two-sided networks, sales reps are product managers. Hire them as such.

### PayPal — Viral growth economics
**Thiel's use:** Numbers behind the canonical viral-growth story. Paid signup plus paid referral, compounding on itself.
> "By directly paying people to sign up and then paying them more to refer friends, we achieved extraordinary growth. This strategy cost us $20 per customer, but it also led to 7% daily growth."
— Z21 L2782-2790

**Lesson:** Viral growth works when CAC is bounded and the product itself is the delivery vehicle. $20 CAC with doubling every 10 days is a bargain.

## 11. Seven Questions (Cleantech vs Tesla)

### Cleantech bubble — Negative worked example
**Thiel's use:** The whole sector used as a multi-question failure case. Solyndra is the famous grave; most companies died similarly.
> "Instead of a healthier planet, we got a massive cleantech bubble. Solyndra is the most famous green ghost, but most cleantech companies met similarly disastrous ends."
— Z21 L3095-3108

**Lesson:** Of 2007's top 150 solar manufacturers, 40 were dead by 2013. A sector can fail all seven questions at once.

### MiaSolé — Monopoly-question failure
**Thiel's use:** CEO self-identified as "one of several" — yet also predicted being largest in the world within a year. The contradiction screamed commodity.
> "Pearce predicted that MiaSolé would become 'the largest producer of thin-film solar cells in the world' within a year's time. That didn't happen... MiaSolé, which was acquired in 2013 for hundreds of millions of dollars less than its investors had put into the company."
— Z21 L3198-3209

**Lesson:** If the CEO describes the field in terms of peers, stop. You're looking at a commodity business with a dream deck.

### Cleantech CEOs — The suit rule
**Thiel's use:** The People Question made into a hiring/screening heuristic.
> "So we instituted a blanket rule: pass on any company whose founders dressed up for pitch meetings... never invest in a tech CEO that wears a suit."
— Z21 L3238-3247

**Lesson:** The suit is a tell — the founder is selling the story, not the technology. Real tech CEOs wear T-shirts because the product has to speak first.

### Tesla — 7-for-7 worked example
**Thiel's use:** Ch. 13's positive answer, question by question. Technology, Timing, Monopoly, Team, Distribution, Durability, Secret — all mapped.
> "Tesla is one of the few cleantech companies started last decade to be thriving today. They rode the social buzz of cleantech better than anyone, but they got the big stuff right."
— Z21 L3362-3440

**Lesson:** In the same sector and the same era as MiaSolé, Tesla passed all seven: Daimler/Mercedes/Toyota/GM studied their tech; they took the 2010 DOE loan before Solyndra politicized it; they started with a high-end Roadster submarket; Musk is engineer + salesman; they own their distribution; the brand is a head start; their secret was that cleantech was a social phenomenon as much as a technology one.
