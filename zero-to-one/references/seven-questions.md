# Man + Machine and the Seven Questions

Source: Peter Thiel, *Zero to One* (2014), Chapter 12 ("Man and Machine") and Chapter 13 ("Seeing Green"). This is the climax of the book's scoring apparatus: Ch. 12 sets the design principle for what kind of product to build, and Ch. 13 delivers the seven-question pass/fail checklist that every business plan must survive.

---

## Part A — Man + Machine (Ch. 12)

### 1. Core claim

The premise that better computers will necessarily replace human workers is wrong. "Computers are complements for humans, not substitutes. The most valuable businesses of coming decades will be built by entrepreneurs who seek to empower people rather than try to make them obsolete." (L2870–2873)

This reframes the entire AI/automation conversation. Luddites and strong-AI futurists share the same (wrong) premise — that computers substitute for humans. Thiel rejects both (L2867–2870).

### 2. Categorical difference between humans and computers

Humans and machines are not more or less powerful than each other along a single axis — they are categorically different (L2919–2921).

- Humans have **intentionality**: we form plans and make decisions in complicated situations (L2908–2910).
- Computers excel at **efficient data processing** but struggle with basic judgments that are simple for any human (L2910–2912).
- A Google supercomputer with 16,000 CPUs, after scanning 10 million thumbnails, learned to identify a cat with 75% accuracy — a task any four-year-old does flawlessly. Meanwhile, a cheap laptop beats the smartest mathematicians at some tasks (L2915–2920).

The asymmetry is the opportunity: gains from working *with* computers are higher than gains from trade with other people, because we don't compete with computers for resources (L2922–2925).

### 3. Technology as escape from competition

Humans compete for jobs and resources; computers compete for neither (L2881–2882). Computers don't yearn for luxurious foods or beachfront villas in Cap Ferrat — they want only a nominal amount of electricity, which they're not even smart enough to want (L2926–2930). "Properly understood, technology is the one way for us to escape competition in a globalizing world." (L2932–2934)

Ch. 12 is therefore the bridge between Thiel's anti-competition thesis (Ch. 3) and his product-design advice: building hybrid man-machine systems is the concrete way to escape competition. Globalization means substitution (more humans competing for the same resources and jobs). Technology means complementarity (more leverage without a new rival for oil or real estate). Any business that builds a pure substitution play puts itself back inside the globalization fight it was trying to escape.

### 4. Worked examples of hybrid systems

**PayPal's "Igor" fraud engine (L2950–2964).** PayPal was losing $10M/month to credit-card fraud. Pure automation failed because fraudsters adapted. Pure human review was too slow. The solution was hybrid: software flagged the most suspicious transactions through a well-designed UI, and human operators made the final call. PayPal turned its first quarterly profit in Q1 2002 (vs. a $29.3M loss a year earlier). The FBI adopted the system.

**Palantir (L2969–2999).** Thiel co-founded Palantir in 2004 on the same thesis: neither spies alone (CIA approach) nor computers alone (NSA approach) can make us safe. Palantir software flags suspicious activity for trained analysts. Analysts using Palantir have predicted IED placements in Afghanistan, prosecuted insider-trading cases, taken down the largest child-pornography ring, and saved banks and the government hundreds of millions in fraud losses (L2993–2999).

**LinkedIn for recruiters (L3013–3025).** LinkedIn didn't try to replace recruiters. Recruiting is part detective work and part sales — "effectively replacing all those functions with a computer would be impossible." Instead LinkedIn transformed how recruiters work. Today 97%+ of recruiters use it. "If LinkedIn had tried to simply replace recruiters with technology, they wouldn't have a business today." (L3023–3025)

### 5. Big data is usually dumb data

"Today's companies have an insatiable appetite for data, mistakenly believing that more data always creates more value. But big data is usually dumb data." (L3045–3047) Computers find patterns; they cannot compare patterns across sources or interpret complex behaviors. Actionable insights come from a human analyst (L3048–3052).

**The design question that follows from Ch. 12 (L3057–3060):**

> "The most valuable companies in the future won't ask what problems can be solved with computers alone. Instead, they'll ask: **how can computers help humans solve hard problems?**"

### 6. Ethical boundary from Ch. 12

- Do **not** build for substitution. Design to empower, not to replace. Substitution products compete with the humans they displace; complementary products generate new value neither side could produce alone.
- Do **not** equate more data with more value. Insight is a human output; data is an input. Teams that hoard data without a clear human workflow around it are producing dumb data at scale (L3045–3052).
- Do **not** chase strong-AI doom or utopia framing as an excuse to ignore near-term design choices. Replacement by computers is a worry for the 22nd century; indefinite fears shouldn't stop us from making definite plans today (L3076–3085).
- The ideological trap (L3027–3034): CS training pushes engineers toward replacement because that's what publishes. Counter it explicitly in product reviews — the question is "how can computers help humans solve hard problems?" not "can we automate this person away?"

## Part B — The Seven Questions (Ch. 13, "Seeing Green")

### 1. The cleantech setup

At the start of the 21st century everyone agreed cleantech was the next big thing. Investors poured more than **$50 billion** into thousands of cleantech companies in the 2000s (L3096–3098). The result: a massive bubble. More than **40 solar manufacturers** went out of business or filed for bankruptcy in **2012 alone** (L3100–3103). Solyndra is the famous green ghost; most companies met similar ends.

Thiel's diagnosis (L3108–3110):

> "Most cleantech companies crashed because they neglected one or more of the seven questions that every business must answer."

### 2. The decision rule

L3134–3140:

> "Whatever your industry, any great business plan must address every one of them. If you don't have good answers to these questions, you'll run into lots of 'bad luck' and your business will fail. If you nail all seven, you'll master fortune and succeed. Even getting five or six correct might work. But the striking thing about the cleantech bubble was that people were starting companies with **zero good answers** — and that meant hoping for a miracle."

Use as binary pass/fail. Score 7/7 is mastery. 5–6 is survivable. 0–4 is hoping for a miracle.

### 3. The seven questions

| # | Question | Verbatim (L3112–L3133) | Rubric quote | Rubric line |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Engineering** | "Can you create breakthrough technology instead of incremental improvements?" | "A great technology company should have proprietary technology an order of magnitude better than its nearest substitute." | L3146–3147 |
| 2 | **Timing** | "Is now the right time to start your particular business?" | "Entering a slow-moving market can be a good strategy, but only if you have a definite and realistic plan to take it over." | L3184–3185 |
| 3 | **Monopoly** | "Are you starting with a big share of a small market?" | "If you can't monopolize a unique solution for a small market, you'll be stuck with vicious competition." | L3205–3207 |
| 4 | **People** | "Do you have the right team?" | "Never invest in a tech CEO that wears a suit... The best sales is hidden." | L3244–3247 |
| 5 | **Distribution** | "Do you have a way to not just create but deliver your product?" | "Selling and delivering a product is at least as important as the product itself." | L3251–3253 |
| 6 | **Durability** | "Will your market position be defensible 10 and 20 years into the future?" | "Every entrepreneur should plan to be the last mover in her particular market. That starts with asking yourself: what will the world look like 10 and 20 years from now." | L3282–3284 |
| 7 | **Secret** | "Have you identified a unique opportunity that others don't see?" | "Great companies have secrets: specific reasons for success that other people don't see." | L3336–3337 |

### 4. Question-by-question detail

**Engineering (L3145–3164).** Incremental wins often end up meaning no improvement at all for the end user. A 20% lab improvement is wiped out by real-world friction and customer skepticism. Only 10x wins offer "transparent superiority." Solyndra's cylindrical solar cells were ~1/π as efficient as flat ones — a radically inferior starting point they could never recover from (L3149–3154).

**Timing (L3165–3186).** SpectraWatt's CEO compared solar to microprocessors "in the late 1970s" (L3168–3170). Wrong analogy. Bell Labs' first solar cell was 1954; efficiency gains were slow and linear. The slow-market rule: enter only with a definite, realistic plan to take it over. Cleantech had none.

**Monopoly (L3187–3231).** John Doerr's "Internet-sized markets are in the billions; energy markets are in the trillions" (L3190–3191) was the genre's core fallacy: trillion-dollar markets mean ruthless, bloody competition. **MiaSolé** (L3198–3209) is the canonical counter-example: CEO Dave Pearce told Congress in 2006 that his company was one of several "very strong" thin-film solar startups — then minutes later predicted MiaSolé would be "the largest producer of thin-film solar cells in the world" within a year. It was acquired in 2013 for hundreds of millions less than invested. The market-size fallacy works in both directions: cleantech founders rhetorically shrank their market to seem differentiated, then turned around and asked to be valued on the global energy market (L3210–3229).

**People (L3232–3248).** Energy problems are engineering problems, so the CEOs should have been nerds. Instead they were salesman-executives in suits. Founders Fund instituted a blanket rule: pass on any company whose founders dressed up for pitch meetings (L3241–3242). "Never invest in a tech CEO that wears a suit" (L3244). The tell: a CEO who looks like a salesman is probably bad at sales and worse at tech (L3245–3247). The deeper principle, from Ch. 11: the best sales is hidden.

**Distribution (L3249–3280).** Better Place raised $800M (2007–2012) and sold only ~1,000 cars before bankruptcy. Customers had to get approval, prove proximity to a swap station, promise predictable routes, subscribe to fuel, and learn battery-swap behavior. "Better Place thought its technology spoke for itself, so they didn't bother to market it clearly." Selling and delivering is at least as important as the product.

**Durability (L3281–3319).** "Every entrepreneur should plan to be the last mover in her particular market." Rephrase-test for any market: **"What will stop China from wiping out my business?"** (L3304–3306). Cleantech also failed to anticipate fracking: shale gas rose from 1.7% of US natural gas in 2000 to 34% in 2013, with gas prices down 70% since 2008 — devastating most renewable business models (L3311–3319).

**Secret (L3320–3337).** Every cleantech company justified itself with **conventional truths** about the need for a cleaner world. An overwhelming *social* need does not imply a *business* opportunity. Bullish solar quotes from Bush, Bill Gross, Solaria's CEO — all conventional, all bankrupt. Great companies have secrets; cleantech companies had consensus.

### 5. Cleantech's losing scorecard

Cleantech companies failed with "zero good answers" across the seven (L3108–3110, L3139). Any single mistake is enough to doom a company (L3142–3144). The reason so many plans passed investor diligence despite being 0/7 was the bubble's indefinite optimism: investors bet on the general idea of green energy instead of specific business plans (L3447–3450). When the scoring rule is "the cause is important," every pitch passes. When the scoring rule is "answer all seven," almost none do.

The typical cleantech pitch looked like this on the scorecard:

- Engineering: 2x at best, often worse than incumbent (Solyndra's 1/π efficiency).
- Timing: appeal to a trend ("solar is the new microprocessor") with no inflection.
- Monopoly: "trillion-dollar market" framing — i.e., a red ocean.
- People: salesman-CEOs in suits optimizing for subsidies, not product.
- Distribution: "if we build it they'll come" (Better Place).
- Durability: no answer to China or fracking.
- Secret: conventional truth ("the world needs clean energy").

Zero passes. Hoping for a miracle.

### 6. Tesla: 7 for 7 (L3362–L3437)

| # | Question | Tesla's answer | Line |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Engineering | Advanced batteries plus an integrated end-to-end stack. Daimler uses Tesla battery packs, Mercedes uses a Tesla powertrain, Toyota uses a Tesla motor, GM created a task force to track Tesla. Model S rated higher than any car Consumer Reports ever reviewed. | L3367–3377 |
| 2 | Timing | Secured a $465M DOE loan in January 2010 — ~18 months before Solyndra imploded and politicized subsidies. A half-billion-dollar subsidy was "unthinkable in the mid-2000s" and "unthinkable today." One moment to play it; Tesla played it. | L3379–3390 |
| 3 | Monopoly | Started with a tiny dominatable submarket: high-end electric sports cars. Only ~3,000 Roadsters sold, but at $109K each. Used that beachhead to fund R&D for the Model S, then owned luxury electric sedans (20K+ in 2013). | L3392–3400 |
| 4 | People | Elon Musk is the consummate engineer-and-salesman. Staff framed as "Special Forces" vs. regular army. | L3402–3407 |
| 5 | Distribution | Owns the entire distribution chain — sells and services cars in its own stores. Higher up-front cost, but control over experience, brand, and long-run margin. Cuts out dealers. | L3409–3417 |
| 6 | Durability | Head start + moving faster than anyone else — lead set to widen. A coveted brand in a high-trust purchase category. Founder still in charge. | L3419–3425 |
| 7 | Secret | Cleantech was a social/fashion phenomenon, not primarily an environmental one. Rich people wanted to *look* green. So Tesla built cars that made drivers look cool, period — not cool by association. DiCaprio ditched his Prius for a Roadster. | L3427–3437 |

Tesla's success "proves that there was nothing inherently wrong with cleantech" (L3439–3440). The problem was the scorecard.

### 7. Ethical boundaries from Ch. 13

- **Market-size honesty.** Don't shrink-then-expand your market (L3225–3229). Name the real submarket you can dominate *and* the real total you'll be valued against — consistently.
- **Real secret, not conventional truth.** Pitches whose only rationale is "everybody knows this market is huge" fail the Secret question by construction (L3335–3337).
- **Social-entrepreneurship trap.** "'Do well by doing good.' Usually they end up doing neither." (L3346–3347) The ambiguity in "socially good" — good *for* society vs. merely *seen* as good — is the trap. Whatever gets applause from all audiences can only be conventional (L3349–3352). Progress is held back by the **sameness** of corporations and nonprofits, not their difference (L3353–3357).

## Part C — Applying both together

### Product-build heuristic

Build hybrid man + machine products (Ch. 12) that pass all seven questions (Ch. 13). The Ch. 12 design question — "How can computers help humans solve hard problems?" — is a direct on-ramp to a strong Engineering (10x) and Secret (under-seen opportunity) answer, because substitution products compete on axes everyone else is already optimizing.

### The Seven Questions as a book-wide recap

Ch. 13 is not a new framework; it's the scorecard that aggregates earlier chapters:

| Question | Recaps which chapter | Core lens |
|---|---|---|
| Engineering | Ch. 5 (10x proprietary technology) | Order-of-magnitude advantage |
| Timing | Ch. 6 (definite optimism) | Why now, with a definite plan |
| Monopoly | Ch. 5 (start small and monopolize) | Big share of small market |
| People | Ch. 9–10 (founders, mafia, distribution) | Hidden-sales tell; T-shirts and jeans |
| Distribution | Ch. 11 (you are the salesperson) | Channel is part of the product |
| Durability | Ch. 5 (last mover advantage) | 10–20 year defensibility |
| Secret | Ch. 8 (secrets) | Non-consensus, specific reason |

If a plan fails here, the failure traces back to an earlier chapter. Fix the upstream chapter; the scorecard answer follows.

### Seven-Questions scorecard template (for pitch review)

```
Company: _______________________  Reviewer: _______  Date: _______

 1. Engineering — 10x proprietary tech vs. nearest substitute?          [ PASS / FAIL ]
    Evidence / dimension / magnitude: ______________________________

 2. Timing — Why now, with a definite, realistic plan?                  [ PASS / FAIL ]
    Inflection / market motion / plan: _____________________________

 3. Monopoly — Big share of a small, real market?                       [ PASS / FAIL ]
    Named submarket / current share / path to dominance: ____________

 4. People — Technologist founder(s); sales hidden; no suits?           [ PASS / FAIL ]
    Founder background / sales motion / suit-test: __________________

 5. Distribution — Identified channel that actually delivers?           [ PASS / FAIL ]
    Single primary channel / CAC / delivery path: ___________________

 6. Durability — Defensible in 10–20 years; last mover?                 [ PASS / FAIL ]
    What stops a fast-follower (e.g. China) from wiping this out?: __

 7. Secret — Specific, non-consensus reason for success?                [ PASS / FAIL ]
    The secret (one sentence, not a slogan): _______________________

 Score: __/7     Verdict:  7/7 master fortune · 5–6 survivable · ≤4 miracle
```
