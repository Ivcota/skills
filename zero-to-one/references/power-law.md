# The Power Law — Follow the Money

Deep-dive reference for Chapter 7 of Peter Thiel's *Zero to One* (2014).

## 1. Core concept

> "We don't live in a normal world; we live under a power law." (L1667–1670)

A normal distribution expects most outcomes to cluster around a mean, with a few modest winners and a few modest losers balancing each other out. A power-law distribution is the opposite: a tiny number of outcomes are so enormous that they dwarf everything else combined. The entire chapter is Thiel's argument that careers, portfolios, markets, and distribution channels all obey this law — and that pretending otherwise is the single most common strategic mistake in both business and life.

Thiel frames it as a secret hiding in plain sight (L1766–1767): "Power law distributions are so big that they hide in plain sight." The reason: in the early stages, future winners and future flops look almost identical. Variance is low until it isn't. By the time the gap is obvious, the bet is already locked in.

## 2. The Power Law in Venture Capital

### The biggest secret

> "The biggest secret in venture capital is that the best investment in a successful fund equals or outperforms the entire rest of the fund combined." (L1712–1714)

This isn't an anecdote. It's the structural truth of the asset class. A fund's return isn't the average of its companies — it's essentially the single best company, plus noise.

### Founders Fund 2005 — worked example

Thiel's own data (L1708–1711):
- **Facebook** — the best investment in Founders Fund's 2005 fund — returned more than all the other investments in that fund combined.
- **Palantir** — the second-best investment — is set to return more than the sum of every other investment aside from Facebook.

Read that twice. Two companies, in one fund, each individually outperform the rest of the portfolio. That is what a power-law distribution actually looks like in the wild.

### The two rules of VC

From these facts, Thiel derives two rules (L1715–1720):

1. **Only invest in companies that have the potential to return the value of the entire fund.** Most possible investments — even most *good* ones — fail this test and must be rejected. (L1715–1718)
2. **Because rule 1 is so restrictive, there can be no other rules.** (L1719–1720) No sector mandates, no stage rules, no check-size limits. If a company can return the fund, take the swing regardless of what else it violates.

These rules are the operational consequence of taking the power law seriously.

## 3. The A16Z / Instagram counter-example — worked math

The counter-example that *looks* like a spectacular win but actually proves the rule (L1721–1728):

- Andreessen Horowitz invested **$250,000** in Instagram in 2010.
- Two years later, Facebook bought Instagram for $1B.
- A16Z's stake netted **$78M** — a **312x return in under two years.**

By every intuitive measure, this is a career-defining home run. And yet:

- A16Z had a **$1.5B fund.**
- $1.5B ÷ $78M per Instagram = **~19 Instagrams needed to break even.**

One of the Valley's best firms had one of the decade's best outcomes, and it wasn't even close to returning the fund. That's why Thiel says (paraphrasing L1726–1728) VCs must put *a lot more money* into any company worth funding — a $250K check is structurally incapable of returning a billion-dollar fund, even on a 312x.

**Takeaway:** Even a famous win doesn't let you diversify. The power law punishes small checks and scattered bets regardless of hit rate.

## 4. Against spray-and-pray

> "But this 'spray and pray' approach usually produces an entire portfolio of flops, with no hits at all." (L1696–1701)

The intuitive move — assume normal distribution, diversify across many bets, hope winners counterbalance losers — is wrong in two directions:

1. **Returns aren't normally distributed**, so "counterbalancing" is a fiction. The winners don't offset the losers; they *swamp* the entire rest of the fund by orders of magnitude.
2. **Diversification as a strategy repels the very companies it claims to hedge for.** If you're spreading attention across many bets, you will miss the rare company that could have returned the fund — because finding it requires single-minded pursuit, not dabbling. (L1700–1701)

The spray-and-pray portfolio doesn't just underperform a concentrated portfolio. It typically produces **zero hits** — all flops, because the discipline to identify a potential fund-returner was never applied in the first place.

## 5. The Power Law in Careers

Life is not a portfolio (L1794–1795). An individual can't run dozens of careers simultaneously and hope one works out. The same math that governs VC funds governs the career of every person choosing what to work on.

Thiel's concrete claims:

- **0.01% of Google beats 100% of a flop.** (L1818–1821) If you fully fund your own venture you can own 100% of the equity — but 100% of a failed company is 100% of nothing. 0.01% of Google was worth more than $35M at time of writing.
- **Differences *between* companies dwarf differences in roles *within* companies.** (L1816–1817) Title, equity percentage, and seniority matter far less than whether the company itself is on an exponential curve. Picking the right rocket ship dominates picking the right seat.
- **Focus relentlessly on something you're good at that will be valuable.** (L1807–1810) The sequence matters: first think hard about whether the work will be valuable *in the future*, then pour your effort into being excellent at it.

Corollary Thiel draws explicitly: you should *not* necessarily start your own company even if you're extraordinarily talented. (L1811–1815) Joining the best growing company is often the higher-expected-value bet.

## 6. The singular principle

The operational punchline of the chapter (L1822–1831):

> "The most important things are singular: One market will probably be better than all others... One distribution strategy usually dominates all others... in a power law world, you can't afford not to think hard about where your actions will fall on the curve."

Thiel's three "ones":

- **One market** will probably be better than all the others you could target. (ties to Ch. 5)
- **One distribution strategy** usually dominates all the others you could try. (ties to Ch. 11)
- **Some moments matter far more than others** — time and decision-making themselves follow a power law. (ties to Ch. 9)

The principle is not "pick one thing and ignore everything else." It's: **assume that one thing will end up mattering 100x more than the alternatives, and bet accordingly.** The alternative — treating options as roughly equal and hedging across them — denies the underlying distribution and virtually guarantees you miss the dominant one.

## 7. Why the power law hides in plain sight

Two reasons Thiel gives for why even professional VCs miss it:

1. **Early-stage variance is low** (L1766–1767 and the preceding passage). Ten companies with monopoly potential look similar in year one. Even in year three, the difference between exponential and linear growth is unclear. The power law only becomes legible after ~10 years, by which point the portfolio splits not into "winners and losers" but into **one dominant investment and everything else.**
2. **Daily experience hides it.** Investors spend most of their time on early-stage or problematic companies — by definition the undifferentiated middle — and rarely experience the power-law gap viscerally.

## 8. Why everyone struggles to see it — the school system riff

Thiel's anti-education riff (L1795–1815) is not a throwaway — it's his explanation for why the mistake is endemic:

- American education teaches normal-distribution thinking by construction.
- Every high school course period is 45 minutes, regardless of subject.
- Every student proceeds at roughly the same pace.
- College "excellence" is packaged in hundred-page alphabetized catalogs that reassure students "it doesn't matter what you do, as long as you do it well."
- Model students hedge by assembling "a suite of exotic and minor skills" — the human equivalent of a spray-and-pray portfolio.

Thiel's direct refutation (L1806–1807): **"That is completely false. It does matter what you do."** The school system trains people to optimize for a distribution that doesn't exist in reality.

## 9. Operational applications

The power law is not a thesis — it's a decision filter. Apply it wherever you're allocating finite attention or capital:

- **Career choice.** Join the company with the best power-law trajectory. Don't spread bets across multiple jobs, side projects, or "optionality" plays. A small slice of a rocket ship beats a large slice of a flop (L1818–1821).
- **Investment.** Concentrate. Five to seven companies per fund, each with fund-returning potential (L1708–1720). Avoid diversification-as-strategy; apply rule 1 ruthlessly.
- **Distribution.** Nail one channel. Do not run five half-heartedly. (L1825–1827, ties to Ch. 11.) Whatever channel is working best will likely dominate all the others *combined* — find it and pour resources into it.
- **Prioritization.** Before executing, identify the one market, one channel, one decision, or one hire that will dominate the outcome — and over-invest in it. Assume singularity, not pluralism.
- **Founding vs. joining.** Given the power-law gap between companies, hesitate before starting your own. The expected value of a senior role at the right rocket ship often beats 100% of a venture you fully fund yourself (L1811–1821).

## 10. Diagnostic: when are you violating the power law?

Use these as self-checks. Each symptom is a sign you're implicitly assuming a normal distribution when the underlying reality is a power law.

- **"I'm hedging my bets across X, Y, and Z."** Translation: you have refused to identify which one will dominate. In a power-law world, two of those three are going to round to zero — and carrying them dilutes attention from the one that won't.
- **"We're running five distribution experiments in parallel."** Likely failure mode: half-hearted execution of the one channel that would actually have worked (L1825–1827, ties to Ch. 11).
- **"I'm taking this job because the title is better even though the company is worse."** Differences between companies dwarf differences in roles (L1816–1817). The title is optimizing inside the wrong distribution.
- **"Even if this fails, I'll have learned a lot."** A legitimate framing for early experiments, but a dangerous framing for multi-year career bets. 100% of a flop is still 100% of nothing (L1818–1821).
- **"Our fund is staying disciplined — we only invest in companies that could 10x."** Not enough. Rule 1 is *fund-returners*, not 10x'ers (L1715–1718). A 10x on a small check in a large fund cannot close the math (see the A16Z / Instagram example in §3).
- **"I'll do a little of this and a little of that until I find what sticks."** This is the individual-career version of spray-and-pray (L1696–1701). Typical outcome: a portfolio of flops with no hit.

## 11. The discipline this requires

Thiel's closing (L1828–1831): "You can't trust a world that denies the power law to accurately frame your decisions for you, so what's most important is rarely obvious. It might even be secret."

Acting on the power law means:

- Rejecting most "reasonable" options — including many that look good — because they can't return the fund / make the career / dominate the market.
- Tolerating that the most important choice is usually non-obvious and unpopular. The crowd's framing is built on normal-distribution logic; it will actively mislead you.
- Committing fully once you've identified the dominant bet. Hedging after selection reintroduces the very diversification problem you were trying to escape.
