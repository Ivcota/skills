# Distribution / Sales Is Hidden

Source: Peter Thiel, *Zero to One* (2014), Chapter 11, "If You Build It, Will They Come?"

## Core concept

The Field of Dreams conceit — build it and they will come — is the most expensive lie in Silicon Valley. "Customers will not come just because you build it. You have to make that happen, and it's harder than it looks" (L2557–2558).

Thiel's strongest claim about distribution is the one engineers find hardest to accept: **superior sales and distribution by itself can create a monopoly, even with no product differentiation. The converse is not true** (L2632–2633). No matter how strong your product, you still need a strong distribution plan.

Two numbers set the outer bounds of what distribution can look like (L2637–2640):

- **CLV** (Customer Lifetime Value): total net profit over the customer relationship
- **CAC** (Customer Acquisition Cost): average spend to acquire one customer
- **CLV must exceed CAC.** The higher the price of the product, the more you can — and must — spend to make the sale.

## Nerds vs. salesmen — the size of the industry engineers ignore

Thiel opens the chapter with numbers because engineers underestimate how big sales actually is (L2560–2564):

- U.S. advertising industry: **$150 billion/year, 600,000+ employees**
- U.S. sales industry: **$450 billion/year, 3.2 million Americans working in sales**

"Seasoned executives will suspect the number is low, but engineers may sigh in bewilderment. What could that many salespeople possibly be doing?" (L2563–2564).

The answer is: the whole economy runs on persuasion, and engineers have been trained not to see it.

### Advertising works on you too

"In Silicon Valley, nerds are skeptical of advertising, marketing, and sales because they seem superficial and irrational. But advertising matters because it works. It works on nerds, and it works on you" (L2565–2567). Thiel's sharpest jab: "You may think that you're an exception; that your preferences are authentic, and advertising only works on other people… Anyone who can't acknowledge its likely effect on himself is doubly deceived" (L2567–2572).

Advertising doesn't exist to make you buy now — it "exists to embed subtle impressions that will drive sales later" (L2570).

## Sales is hidden

"Like acting, sales works best when hidden" (L2599). The evidence is in the job titles:

- People who sell advertising are called **"account executives"** (L2602)
- People who sell customers work in **"business development"** (L2603)
- People who sell companies are **"investment bankers"** (L2603)
- People who sell themselves are called **"politicians"** (L2604)

"There's a reason for these redescriptions: none of us wants to be reminded when we're being sold" (L2604). Sales grandmasters exist; you've encountered them; you didn't notice because "their art is hidden in plain sight" (L2594).

## Why engineers miss distribution

Engineers are trained on transparency — code works or it fails, and surface appearances don't matter (L2577–2580). Sales looks to them like "an orchestrated campaign to change surface appearances without changing the underlying reality" which "strikes engineers as trivial if not fundamentally dishonest" (L2580–2582).

When engineers see "salespeople laughing on the phone with a customer or going to two-hour lunches, they suspect that no real work is being done" (L2583–2585). Thiel's correction: "What nerds miss is that it takes hard work to make sales look easy" (L2587–2588).

The result is a self-delusion Thiel names directly:

> "The engineer's grail is a product great enough that 'it sells itself.' But anyone who would actually say this about a real product must be lying: either he's delusional (lying to himself) or he's selling something (and thereby contradicting himself)." (L2619–2622)

"It's better to think of distribution as something essential to the design of your product. If you've invented something new but you haven't invented an effective way to sell it, you have a bad business — no matter how good the product" (L2628–2631).

## The sales ladder by deal size

Distribution methods sit on a continuum set by average selling price (ASP). The ladder below is from L2641–2790.

| Channel | ASP range | Who sells | Cycle | Canonical example | Key mechanic |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Complex sales** | $1M+ (seven figures) | CEO, no formal salesforce | Months–years; 1 deal/year or two | SpaceX: Musk personally closed NASA billion-dollar shuttle replacement (L2655–2665). Palantir: CEO Alex Karp spends **25 days a month on the road** meeting clients; deals range $1M–$100M; "buyers want to talk to the CEO, not the VP of Sales" (L2666–2672). | Relationships and political inertia; 50–100% YoY for a decade is the success signal (L2673–2680). Start small; build reference customers before bigger deals (L2680–2685). |
| **Personal sales** | $10K–$100K | Modest sales team, individual relationships | Weeks–months | Box in 2008: third salesperson (Blake) sold a small account to the Stanford Sleep Clinic in 2009; that seed grew into Stanford Hospital running on Box and a Stanford-branded Box account for every student and faculty member (L2689–2699). | **"If it had started by trying to sell the president of the university on an enterprise-wide solution, Box would have sold nothing"** (L2699–2701). Land narrow, expand from inside. |
| **Distribution doldrums** | $1K–$10K | Dead zone — nobody economically | N/A | Hypothetical $1,000 convenience-store inventory tool: no TV channel only store owners watch; ads in *Convenience Store News* won't close; sending a rep to every prospect is too expensive (L2715–2728). | Thiel: "This is why so many small and medium-sized businesses don't use tools that bigger firms take for granted. It's not that small business proprietors are unusually backward or that good tools don't exist: **distribution is the hidden bottleneck**" (L2729–2734). |

## Distribution-as-product (ZocDoc)

ZocDoc is the case where distribution and product merge (L2703–2714). It charges doctors a few hundred dollars a month, average deal size a few thousand dollars, so it needs lots of salespeople — enough to run an internal recruiting team "to do nothing but hire more" (L2707–2709). But:

> "Making personal sales to doctors doesn't just bring in revenue; by adding doctors to the network, salespeople make the product more valuable to consumers (and more consumer users increases its appeal to doctors)." (L2709–2712)

Each sale is both revenue and a product improvement. This only works when the offering has network-effect economics.

## The power law of distribution

Distribution follows its own power law (L2810–2816):

> "Most businesses get zero distribution channels to work: poor sales rather than bad product is the most common cause of failure. If you can get just one distribution channel to work, you have a great business. If you try for several but don't nail one, you're finished." (L2813–2816)

The "kitchen sink approach — employ a few salespeople, place some magazine ads, and try to add some kind of viral functionality to the product as an afterthought — doesn't work" (L2811–2813).

## If you don't see salespeople, you're the salesperson

The chapter's closing line, and the clearest test for founders who insist they've "avoided sales":

> "Look around. If you don't see any salespeople, you're the salesperson." (L2841–2842)

## Everybody sells

"Your company needs to sell more than its product. You must also sell your company to employees and investors" (L2818–2819). Two versions of the "it sells itself" lie show up in the org:

- **HR version:** "This company is so good that people will be clamoring to join it." (L2820–2821)
- **Fundraising version:** "This company is so great that investors will be banging down our door to invest." (L2822–2823)

Clamor is real but "rarely happens without calculated recruiting and pitching beneath the surface" (L2823–2824). Press and PR matter even for viral-distribution products — because "any prospective employee worth hiring will do his own diligence; what he finds or doesn't find when he googles you will be critical to the success of your company" (L2833–2835).

The rule generalizes from customers to every non-customer audience: employees, investors, press, regulators. "Everybody has a product to sell — no matter whether you're an employee, a founder, or an investor. It's true even if your company consists of just you and your computer" (L2838–2841).

## The suit rule (bridges from Ch. 13)

Thiel's empirical hack for detecting bad tech CEOs, from the cleantech crash (L3244–3247):

> "Never invest in a tech CEO that wears a suit… The best sales is hidden. There's nothing wrong with a CEO who can sell, but if he actually looks like a salesman, he's probably bad at sales and worse at tech."

Founders Fund passed on any company whose founders dressed up for pitch meetings (L3241–3244). Sales grandmasters don't look like salesmen. The sartorial cue is a proxy for the deeper principle — good sales is hidden, polished sales is obvious, and obvious is bad.

## Diagnosing which rung you're on

A quick decision tree derived from the ladder:

- Average deal **> $1M** → complex sales. Founder/CEO must own it personally. If the founder won't spend ~25 days/month on the road, the business won't work at this price point (Palantir benchmark, L2669–2672).
- Average deal **$10K–$100K** → personal sales. Build a team, pick a narrow wedge, land small, expand from inside. Do not pitch the CEO of the customer first (Box lesson, L2699–2701).
- Average deal **$1K–$10K** → distribution doldrums. Before shipping, change the price or the product — not the marketing plan (L2715–2734).
- Low-ticket, mass appeal, **no viral loop** → marketing/advertising. Only viable when CAC/CLV math makes every other channel uneconomical (L2744).
- Low-ticket, **invite lives inside core use** → viral. Target the highest-velocity niche first, not the biggest market (PayPal → eBay PowerSellers, not Western Union emigrants, L2794–2808).

Most founders miscategorize because they anchor on what they want the ASP to be rather than what the customer will actually pay.

## Operational checks

Use these before committing to a go-to-market plan.

**1. What's your ASP?**
The ladder above is deterministic. A $3,000 product cannot be CEO-sold profitably and cannot be advertised profitably — you're in the doldrums (L2715–2734) and need to change the price, the product, or both before shipping. The ASP determines the channel; the channel does not determine the ASP.

**2. Name the one channel you're betting on, and say why.**
"We'll do content + PLG + outbound + events + paid social" is the kitchen sink. Thiel says kitchen sink equals failure: "If you try for several but don't nail one, you're finished" (L2815–2816). Pick one. Commit. The others are optional only after the first one works.

**3. Is distribution the hidden bottleneck for this product?**
If so, no amount of product polish will save it. "If you've invented something new but you haven't invented an effective way to sell it, you have a bad business — no matter how good the product" (L2628–2631).

**4. Does the founder know the CAC/CLV numbers cold?**
Not a range, not "we're working on it" — the actual numbers. CLV must exceed CAC (L2637–2640). At PayPal that meant $20 CAC against money-transfer fees over the customer lifetime (L2784–2790). If the founder can't quote both numbers, distribution isn't really being managed.

**5. If you don't see salespeople in the org, who is the salesperson?**
If the answer is "nobody, the product sells itself," you have either a delusional founder or a selling founder pretending not to sell (L2619–2622, L2841–2842). Neither is a good sign.

**6. Does the CEO wear a suit to pitches?**
Red flag per L3244–3247. Polished sales is bad sales. Good sales is hidden.

**7. For complex-sales businesses: will the CEO commit to ~25 days/month on the road?**
This is the Palantir benchmark (L2669). If not, the business can't sustain a $1M+ ASP — regardless of what the product can do.

**8. For personal-sales businesses: what's the narrowest wedge you can land?**
Not the enterprise deal. The Stanford Sleep Clinic, not the Stanford president (L2689–2701). Name the specific first team, specific first use case, specific first champion.
