# Secrets — Deep Dive

> *"every great business is built around a secret that's hidden from the outside. A great company is a conspiracy to change the world; when you share your secret, the recipient becomes a fellow conspirator."*
> — source.txt L2124–2127

Chapter 8 is the ongoing-practice version of the contrarian question. Where the contrarian question is a one-shot filter, Secrets is the habit: the discipline of continuing to look for important truths that are hidden but discoverable, and of building companies as closed groups organized around them.

---

## 1. Core concept — every great business is built on a secret

Thiel's thesis statement closes the chapter and doubles as the acceptance criterion for any pitch: a company that can't name its secret is indistinguishable from one that doesn't have one. *"every great business is built around a secret that's hidden from the outside. A great company is a conspiracy to change the world; when you share your secret, the recipient becomes a fellow conspirator."* (L2124–2127).

The two halves matter separately:

- **"Built around a secret"** — the strategic claim: the reason the business works is a specific truth the rest of the market has not seen.
- **"Conspiracy to change the world"** — the organizational claim: a company is the social structure that lets a small group act on that truth together, without broadcasting it.

Treat it as an acceptance criterion. For any startup pitch, force an explicit answer to *what's the secret this company is built around?* Generic rationales ("large market," "strong team") mean the founder hasn't found the secret yet.

---

## 2. Three-zone map of knowledge

Thiel divides claims about the world into three zones:

| Zone | Description | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Easy truths** | Conventional knowledge — taught in school, agreed upon. "won't give you an edge. It's not a secret." | L1840 |
| **Secrets** | *"something important and unknown, something hard to do but doable"* | L1860–1861 |
| **Impossibilities / mysteries** | Problems that may resist solution entirely (string theory's untestability). *"You can achieve difficult things, but you can't achieve the impossible."* | L1849–1857 |

The governing statement: *"Every correct answer is necessarily a secret: something important and unknown, something hard to do but doable"* (L1860–1861). Work in the middle zone. Easy truths are already priced in; impossibilities eat your life. *"If there are many secrets left in the world, there are probably many world-changing companies yet to be started"* (L1862–1864).

The map is also a diagnostic. Most failed startups are either attacking easy truths (me-too products built on consensus theses) or attacking mysteries (cold fusion with no specific wedge). The middle zone is smaller than it looks, and staying in it requires active work.

## 3. Belief in secrets is an effective truth

Andrew Wiles proved Fermat's Last Theorem in 1995 after a nine-year solo effort that he kept secret until 1993 — 358 years after other mathematicians had failed (L2022–2030). The proof required brilliance, but it required belief first: *"He needed brilliance to succeed, but he also needed a faith in secrets. If you think something hard is impossible, you'll never even start trying to achieve it. Belief in secrets is an effective truth."* (L2031–2034).

The operative clause is the middle one: disbelief is self-sealing. You don't fail to find the secret because it's not there; you fail because you never opened the search. Fermat's Last Theorem was exactly as findable in 1986 as it was in 1994 — the only variable was whether anyone was looking.

This is why Secrets is a prerequisite for the contrarian question: *"Contrarian thinking doesn't make any sense unless the world still has secrets left to give up"* (L1844–1846). The chapter order is load-bearing. You can't meaningfully answer "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" if you don't first believe such truths exist.

## 4. The four social forces that killed secret-hunting

Thiel names four trends that *"have conspired to root out belief in secrets"* (L1926–1927). Each is a cultural antibody against the search — and each is a diagnostic for a person or team that has stopped looking.

**Incrementalism.** *"From an early age, we are taught that the right way to do things is to proceed one very small step at a time, day by day, grade by grade."* (L1927–1929). The A-student pipeline trains grade-by-grade effort and punishes off-curriculum work. Academia's publication-counting is the adult form: *"academics usually chase large numbers of trivial publications instead of new frontiers"* (L1934–1935). If your planning unit is "one quarter out," you are not hunting secrets.

**Risk aversion.** *"People are scared of secrets because they are scared of being wrong… The prospect of being lonely but right—dedicating your life to something that no one else believes in—is already hard. The prospect of being lonely and wrong can be unbearable."* (L1937–1942). A secret is unvetted by construction. Institutional incentives reward being conventionally wrong over being unconventionally right.

**Complacency.** *"Social elites have the most freedom and ability to explore new thinking, but they seem to believe in secrets the least. Why search for a new secret if you can comfortably collect rents on everything that has already been done?"* (L1943–1946). The elite schools' implicit welcome — *"You got into this elite institution. Your worries are over. You're set for life."* (L1948–1950) — makes rent-collection the enemy of discovery. The people best positioned to search are the people most insulated from needing to.

**Flatness.** *"As globalization advances, people perceive the world as one homogeneous, highly competitive marketplace: the world is 'flat.' Given that assumption, anyone who might have had the ambition to look for a secret will first ask himself: if it were possible to discover something new, wouldn't someone from the faceless global talent pool of smarter and more creative people have found it already?"* (L1951–1958). It's efficient-markets thinking applied to ideas: if the secret were true, someone would already believe it. Consequence: *"today, you can't start a cult"* (L1960–1961).

## 5. The two kinds of secrets

Every candidate secret falls into one of two buckets (L2063–2068):

- **Secrets of nature** — undiscovered physical and scientific truths. Found by studying *"some undiscovered aspect of the physical world"* (L2064–2065).
- **Secrets about people** — *"things that people don't know about themselves or things they hide because they don't want others to know"* (L2066–2068).

Thiel's brainstorming prompt pairs them: *"So when thinking about what kind of company to build, there are two distinct questions to ask: What secrets is nature not telling you? What secrets are people not telling you?"* (L2068–2070).

Use both axes. A team that only asks the first question writes research proposals; a team that only asks the second writes marketing decks. Companies that compound hit both — PayPal solved a nature-side problem (fraud detection at scale) by exploiting a people-side truth (eBay PowerSellers had a velocity of money no one else was pricing).

## 6. Where to look

*"The best place to look for secrets is where no one else is looking."* (L2095). Thiel's heuristic is institutional: look for fields that matter but haven't been standardized. *"Most people think only in terms of what they've been taught; schooling itself aims to impart conventional wisdom. So you might ask: are there any fields that matter but haven't been standardized and institutionalized?"* (L2095–2099).

Two axes, four quadrants:

|  | Standardized | Unstandardized |
|---|---|---|
| **Matters** | Physics — set in its ways; no edge (L2099–2100) | **Sweet spot** — e.g. nutrition |
| **Doesn't matter** | — | Astrology (L2100–2101) |

Thiel's worked example: *"Nutrition matters for everybody, but you can't major in it at Harvard. Most top scientists go into other fields. Most of the big studies were done 30 or 40 years ago, and most are seriously flawed."* (L2101–2105) — *"exactly the kind of field that could yield secrets"* (L2108–2109). The heuristic generalizes: what matters to everyone, has credentialed gatekeepers nowhere, and hasn't been seriously restudied in decades?

## 7. Open but unsuspected secrets

Some secrets are hidden not by obscurity but by familiarity — they sit in plain sight under an assumption nobody has bothered to check. *"Great companies can be built on open but unsuspected secrets about how the world works."* (L2047–2048).

Airbnb, Lyft, and Uber all harnessed spare capacity — unused rooms, unused cars — that was visible to everyone and priced by no one (L2049–2055). *"only by believing in and looking for secrets could you see beyond the convention to an opportunity hidden in plain sight"* (L2056–2058). The retrospective simplicity of these bets ("it's just a website that matches rooms") is itself evidence for the secret thesis: *"If insights that look so elementary in retrospect can support important and valuable businesses, there must remain many great companies still to start."* (L2060–2062).

## 8. A conspiracy to change the world

Once you have a secret, you face a distribution problem. Tell everyone and you get Faust's crucified-and-burned outcome (L2115–2119). Tell no one and you get Wiles in solitary for nine years. Thiel's resolution: *"there's always a golden mean between telling nobody and telling everybody—and that's a company."* (L2122–2124). A company is the correctly-sized social unit for acting on a secret — small enough to keep it, large enough to execute it.

This reframes the two hardest founder activities. **Hiring** and **fundraising** are not transactions; they are recruitment of fellow conspirators. *"when you share your secret, the recipient becomes a fellow conspirator."* (L2126–2127). Diligence runs both ways: the candidate/investor is evaluating whether they believe the secret, and the founder is evaluating whether the candidate is someone the secret can be safely shared with.

## 9. HP cautionary tale

Hewlett-Packard is Thiel's worked example of a company that stopped believing in secrets and collapsed (L1989–2020).

**Invention decade (1991–2000):** From $9B in 1990 to $135B by mid-2000 (L1989–1996).

| Year | Product | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 1991 | DeskJet 500C | World's first affordable color printer |
| 1993 | OmniBook | One of the first "superportable" laptops |
| 1994 | OfficeJet | World's first all-in-one printer/fax/copier |

**Pivot to brand-and-services (1999–2005):** 1999 "Invent" branding campaign *while* actual invention stopped (L1997–1999). 2001 HP Services, *"a glorified consulting and support shop"* (L2000). 2002 Compaq merger, *"presumably because it didn't know what else to do"* (L2001–2002). By 2005, market cap halved to $70B (L2003–2004).

**Board dysfunction (L2005–2014):** Two factions. Perkins (an engineer) argued the board should identify promising new technologies and have HP build them. Dunn (chairwoman, banker by trade) argued *"charting a plan for future technology was beyond the board's competence"* — a night-watchman governance role. Perkins lost. Dunn then arranged illegal wiretaps to identify a leaker; the backlash disgraced the board (L2015–2018).

**Verdict:** *"Having abandoned the search for technological secrets, HP obsessed over gossip. As a result, by late 2012 HP was worth just $23 billion."* (L2018–2020) — not much more than it was worth in 1990, adjusting for inflation.

**The pattern to screen for:** brand-the-search replaces the search; services and mergers replace products; governance theater replaces technology bets; internal intrigue fills the space where secrets used to live.

## 10. Secrets about people are underappreciated

*"Secrets about people are relatively underappreciated."* (L2081). Thiel's explanation is mechanical: nature-secrets require specialized training, so they come with credentialed gatekeepers; people-secrets require only the willingness to ask forbidden questions. *"Maybe that's because you don't need a dozen years of higher education to ask the questions that uncover them: What are people not allowed to talk about? What is forbidden or taboo?"* (L2081–2084).

The monopoly secret itself is a people-secret in disguise. You could derive *"competition and capitalism are opposites"* quantitatively from profit data — the natural route — or you could ask the shorter question: *"what are people running companies not allowed to say?"* (L2090–2091). You'd notice monopolists downplay their dominance to avoid scrutiny while competitive firms exaggerate their uniqueness (L2091–2094). Both routes arrive at the same truth; the people-route is faster and cheaper for founders without PhDs.

## 11. Operational prompts

Each is a direct operationalization of a claim in the chapter.

- **Pair-brainstorm on the two axes.** Run a 30-minute session with one collaborator using nothing but Thiel's prompt pair: *"What secrets is nature not telling you? What secrets are people not telling you?"* (L2068–2070). Require at least three candidates per axis. The pairing itself is the tool — most teams run only the nature track.

- **List three underinstitutionalized fields you've spent time in.** Per L2098–2099, these are fields that *"matter but haven't been standardized and institutionalized"* — the nutrition test (L2101–2109). Your edge isn't domain knowledge; it's the fact that no conventional curriculum organizes what you already know. What can't be majored in at Harvard but that you've lived inside?

- **The industry-taboo question.** Adapt the contrarian question to your sector: *"What important truth do people in my industry refuse to say out loud?"* This is the people-secret question (L2081–2084) localized to the room you work in. Answers already said publicly this year don't count.

- **Pitch diligence — what's the secret?** Require an explicit answer to *"what valuable company is nobody building?"* (L1859–1860). Reject pitches whose rationale collapses to *"everyone knows this market is huge"* — by construction, a consensus thesis cannot be a secret. The bar: *"specific reasons for success that other people don't see"* (L3336–3337).

- **HP-pattern screen for mature companies.** On any incumbent, check the HP sequence (L1997–2020): has invention been replaced by a branding campaign *about* invention? Have products been replaced by services and mergers? Has the board drifted from technology bets to governance theater? Three-of-three is late-stage HP.
