# The Contrarian Question — Deep Dive

> *"What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"*
> — source.txt L103–104

The opening move of *Zero to One*. Thiel asks it of every job candidate and frames the entire book around it. This file is the full rubric, script, and worked examples for applying the question to hiring, diligence, ideation, and self-examination.

---

## 1. Core concept

The contrarian question is a filter for **conviction vs. consensus**. Thiel argues it sounds easy because it's straightforward, but it's "very hard to answer. It's intellectually difficult because the knowledge that everyone is taught in school is by definition agreed upon. And it's psychologically difficult because anyone trying to answer must say something she knows to be unpopular" (source.txt L105–109). The rarity it probes is not IQ — it's nerve: *"Brilliant thinking is rare, but courage is in even shorter supply than genius"* (source.txt L109–110).

A "good" answer has a specific shape. Thiel: *"A good answer takes the following form: 'Most people believe in x, but the truth is the opposite of x.'"* (source.txt L120–121). That shape is non-optional — it forces the respondent past topical opinions into genuine non-consensus belief. If the answer doesn't fit the template, it isn't an answer to this question, it's an answer to some other one.

Good answers also point forward. *"Most answers to the contrarian question are different ways of seeing the present; good answers are as close as we can come to looking into the future"* (source.txt L131–133). The question is diagnostic not of taste but of foresight — it surfaces the specific future the respondent believes in and is willing to build toward.

---

## 2. The 0→1 / 1→n distinction

The contrarian question is inseparable from Thiel's progress taxonomy.

> *"Horizontal or extensive progress means copying things that work—going from 1 to n. Horizontal progress is easy to imagine because we already know what it looks like. Vertical or intensive progress means doing new things—going from 0 to 1. Vertical progress is harder to imagine because it requires doing something nobody else has ever done."*
> — source.txt L136–140

> *"At the macro level, the single word for horizontal progress is globalization… The single word for vertical, 0 to 1 progress is technology."*
> — source.txt L144, L152

Only non-consensus belief about the future can point to 0→1 opportunities. Consensus, by definition, is already being copied somewhere — that's 1→n. The contrarian question is the method; 0→1 is the goal.

---

## 3. Grading rubric

Thiel's own bad answers (source.txt L113–120):

| Answer | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| *"Our educational system is broken and urgently needs to be fixed."* | Might be true, but "many people already agree with" it (L118–119). Popular truth. |
| *"America is exceptional."* | Same failure — popular truth. |
| *"There is no God."* | "Simply takes one side in a familiar debate" (L119–120). Pre-made position, not a belief discovered by the respondent. |

### Scorecard

Grade every answer on four axes:

1. **Shape.** Does it fit *"Most people believe x, but the truth is the opposite of x"* (L120–121)? If not, push back and make them restate it.
2. **Unpopularity.** Is the belief unpopular *in their peer group*? A VC saying "software is eating the world" to a room of VCs is consensus. Unpopularity must be local, not global.
3. **Future-orientation.** Is it a claim about the future, or a re-description of the present (L131–133)? Present-tense reframes fail.
4. **Courage tax.** Did saying it cost them something? If the answer is safe to hold in public, it's probably not the real answer.

Pass requires all four. Three-of-four is a soft pass with a follow-up. Two-of-four is a fail.

## 4. The business version

> *"The business version of our contrarian question is: what valuable company is nobody building? This question is harder than it looks, because your company could create a lot of value without becoming very valuable itself. Creating value is not enough—you also need to capture some of the value you create."*
> — source.txt L442–446

The personal contrarian question translates into startup strategy by adding a value-capture constraint. It's not enough to name a future no one believes in — you have to name a **company** that would exist in that future and would **capture** the value it creates. Thiel's immediate example: U.S. airlines created hundreds of billions of dollars of value in 2012 and made 37¢ per passenger trip; Google created less value but captured far more (source.txt L447–455). "Valuable company nobody is building" is the contrarian question with a P&L attached.

This is where the contrarian question lands in the business frame and hands off to the Monopoly vs. Competition section (Ch. 3) — the business version only has a right answer if the company being imagined can escape competition.

## 5. Interview / diligence script

A five-step application of the question — use in founder interviews, investor diligence calls, hiring, or self-interview.

**Step 1 — Ask the open form.**
Verbatim: *"What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"* (source.txt L103–104). Do not pre-explain the shape. The first answer is almost always bad; that's useful signal.

**Step 2 — Force the shape.**
If the answer doesn't fit *"Most people believe x, but the truth is the opposite of x"* (source.txt L120–121), say so and ask them to restate. Watch whether they can. Many respondents cannot compress their view into the opposition form because they don't actually have one.

**Step 3 — Probe present vs. future.**
Ask: "Is this a claim about how the world already is, or how it will be?" Good answers "are as close as we can come to looking into the future" (source.txt L131–133). If it's a present-tense reframe ("education is broken"), push: *what specifically will be different in 10 years if you're right?*

**Step 4 — Grade courage.**
Ask: "Who in your immediate peer group would disagree with this, and what would they say?" If they can't name the opposition, the belief isn't unpopular where they live. Remember: *"courage is in even shorter supply than genius"* (source.txt L109–110). You are testing for the willingness to be wrong in public.

**Step 5 — Move to the business version.**
Only after the personal answer passes, ask: *"What valuable company is nobody building?"* (source.txt L442–443). Press on value *capture*, not just value *creation*. The airline test: could this company earn Google-like margins, or is it an airline — lots of value created, none captured?

**Anti-pattern to watch for in Step 1–2:** performative contrarianism. Thiel's own warning: *"the most contrarian thing of all is not to oppose the crowd but to think for yourself"* (source.txt L437–439). If the answer reads as reflex opposition — picked because it's unpopular rather than because it's believed — fail it. You're grading conviction, not rebelliousness.

## 6. Worked examples

### Thiel's own answer (pass)

> *"My own answer to the contrarian question is that most people think the future of the world will be defined by globalization, but the truth is that technology matters more."*
> — source.txt L174–176

Check it against the rubric:
- **Shape:** Fits exactly. "Most people think X (globalization defines the future); truth is opposite (technology matters more)."
- **Unpopularity:** Against the consensus of the post-1971 era — *"Since 1971, we have seen rapid globalization along with limited technological development, mostly confined to IT"* (source.txt L166–167).
- **Future-orientation:** A claim about the next century, not a reframe of the present.
- **Courage tax:** Directly contradicts development economics orthodoxy ("developed vs. developing nations" framing, source.txt L170–173).

The supporting argument is that globalization without new technology is environmentally unsustainable (source.txt L177–183) — note how the answer forces a specific prediction.

### Contrast — the three bad answers

See section 3. Each fails a different axis: "education is broken" fails unpopularity; "America is exceptional" fails unpopularity; "there is no God" fails shape (it's a side in an existing debate, not a belief discovered by working through the question).

### The business-version translation

The 1999 bubble provides a worked failure case. By late 1999, consensus held that *"companies exist to make money, not to lose it"* was irrelevant — *"no loss was too big to be described as an investment in an even bigger, brighter future. The conventional wisdom of the 'New Economy' accepted page views as a more authoritative, forward-looking financial metric than something as pedestrian as profit"* (source.txt L233–238). The post-crash contrarian move was to rediscover profit. The business version of the question would have surfaced this in 1999 — what valuable company is nobody building? One that captures value, not one that accumulates page views.

### The preface's formula-rejection

The preface opens with a form of the contrarian answer applied to founder-pattern-matching: *"Every moment in business happens only once. The next Bill Gates will not build an operating system. The next Larry Page or Sergey Brin won't make a search engine. And the next Mark Zuckerberg won't create a social network. If you are copying these guys, you aren't learning from them"* (source.txt L53–56). Useful as a preface to Step 1 when the respondent is about to pitch "Uber for X" or "the Facebook of Y" — the question rules out copying by construction.

## 7. Common failure modes (from the mistakes file)

Four mistakes from `notes/job-5-mistakes.md` sit directly downstream of a failed contrarian-question answer.

**Incremental-advances dogma.** *"Grand visions inflated the bubble, so they should not be indulged.… Small, incremental steps are the only safe path forward"* (source.txt L390–393). Fix: *"It is better to risk boldness than triviality"* (source.txt L417). Incrementalism is the procedural form of never answering the contrarian question at all — if you can't name a future belief, you default to tweaking the present.

**Improve-on-the-competition dogma.** *"Don't try to create a new market prematurely.… you should build your company by improving on recognizable products already offered by successful competitors"* (source.txt L402–405). This is 1→n framed as virtue. A company built this way has no answer to *"what valuable company is nobody building?"* — by construction, someone is already building it.

**Disruption worship.** *"If your company can be summed up by its opposition to already existing firms, it can't be completely new and it's probably not going to become a monopoly"* (source.txt L1159–1161). Disruption posturing is contrarian theater — opposition to incumbents as identity — without the underlying belief about the future. It answers "who are we against?" instead of "what do we believe?"

**Branding without substance.** *"Beginning with brand rather than substance is dangerous.… No technology company can be built on branding alone"* (source.txt L1062, L1073). A brand-first company is signaling a contrarian position it hasn't actually earned — logo and copy pretending to be conviction.

All four are ways of producing a bad answer to the contrarian question without noticing.

## 8. Adjacent concepts

- **Secrets (Ch. 8).** The contrarian question, translated into ongoing practice. *"Every correct answer is necessarily a secret: something important and unknown, something hard to do but doable"* (source.txt L1860–1861). Where the contrarian question is a one-shot filter, Secrets is the habit of continuing to look. *"The best place to look for secrets is where no one else is looking"* (source.txt L2095). Pair the two: the contrarian question produces the first secret; Secrets-thinking finds the next.

- **Definite Optimism (Ch. 6).** The mindset that gives the contrarian answer teeth. *"A business with a good definite plan will always be underrated in a world where people see the future as random"* (source.txt L1620–1621). Without definite optimism, a contrarian answer is just a contrarian *opinion* — no plan, no commitment. Indefinite optimism is why most people never get past Step 3 of the interview script: they haven't committed to any specific future.

- **Monopoly vs. Competition (Ch. 3).** Where the business version of the question lands. *"All happy companies are different: each one earns a monopoly by solving a unique problem. All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition"* (source.txt L683–686). The contrarian question surfaces candidate 0→1 opportunities; Ch. 3 is the test of whether those opportunities can be monopolized and therefore captured.
