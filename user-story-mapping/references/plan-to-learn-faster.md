## The Discovery Sequence

Patton describes a four-phase discovery process that precedes story writing: *(howtoes.blog/2024/03/26/)*

1. **Frame the Idea** — Clarify why this product/feature is worth building, for whom, and within what business constraints. Establish success criteria: what outcome would make this a win?

2. **Understand Customers** — Develop personas, map current-state user journeys, surface pain points and workarounds. This is the as-is map phase.

3. **Envision Solutions** — Create future-state story maps with UI sketches and prototypes. This is the to-be map phase — explicitly a design fiction, not a commitment.

4. **Minimize and Plan** — Ruthlessly cut the to-be map to the minimum needed to validate the core hypothesis. Identify what to build first to learn fastest.

---

## As-Is vs. To-Be Mapping

> "The future is a fiction" with no evidence until a release validates it. *(Patton, mindtheproduct.com)*

**As-is map** — Maps current user behavior, including pain points, workarounds, frequency, and duration. Built from user research, not assumptions. Shows what users actually do, not what the product team thinks they do.

**To-be map** — The hypothesized future state. Built in the Envision phase using UI sketches and narrative walkthroughs. Every task on a to-be map is an assumption until it is validated by user behavior.

Showing stakeholders the as-is map before the to-be map grounds the conversation in evidence and prevents "why are we building this?" questions during reviews.

## Minimum Viable Product Experiment (MVPe)

An MVPe is a targeted experiment to validate a specific risky assumption before committing to build the full feature. It is distinct from an MVP (which is a shippable product) — an MVPe is the smallest test of the riskiest hypothesis.

> "Failing to learn is frequently the biggest failure." *(Patton, howtoes.blog/2024/03/26/)*

**MVPe design steps:**
1. Identify the riskiest card on the to-be map — the assumption that, if wrong, would invalidate the most work
2. Define what it would mean for that assumption to be true
3. Design the smallest test (prototype, landing page, wizard-of-oz, etc.) that would give evidence
4. Run the test before writing any implementation stories

## The Now and Later Model

Patton uses the "Now and Later" distinction to separate current-state mapping (now) from future-state design (later): *(Patton, mindtheproduct.com)*

- **Now map (as-is):** What users do today — documented with pain points, workarounds, frequency
- **Later map (to-be):** What we want users to do after the release — built as a design hypothesis

Teams that skip the Now map and jump to the Later map build solutions for problems they have not verified exist.

## Running a Discovery Sprint

A practical four-session structure, consistent with Patton's discovery process: *(howtoes.blog/2024/03/26/)*

**Session 1 — Frame (half day):**
- State the opportunity: what problem, for whom, and what outcome does the business need?
- Define who is in scope (users) and who is out of scope
- List the top 5 assumptions the team is making

**Session 2 — Understand (1-2 days):**
- Interview users about their current behavior (as-is)
- Build the as-is story map on the wall as you learn
- Mark pain points, workarounds, and high-frequency activities
- Do not discuss solutions yet

**Session 3 — Envision (half day to 1 day):**
- Build the to-be story map in a small group
- Sketch UI for the highest-priority tasks
- Label every card on the to-be map with its key assumption

**Session 4 — Minimize (half day):**
- Identify the riskiest assumption in the to-be map
- Design the smallest MVPe that would give evidence on that assumption
- Define what "confirmed" and "disconfirmed" look like before running the test
