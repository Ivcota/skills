# Systems Strategy — Reference

Deep-dive on Chapter 18 of *The E-Myth Revisited* — the seventh and culminating Business Development strategy. Every quote below is pulled verbatim from the extraction notes and full-text transcript; nothing is paraphrased from model memory.

---

## What a system is

Gerber opens the chapter by finally defining a word he has used throughout the book:

> "A system is a set of things, actions, ideas, and information that interact with each other, and in so doing, alter other systems."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8010–8011

He extends the definition to everything:

> "In short, everything is a system. The universe, the world, San Francisco Bay, the office I'm sitting in, the word processor I'm using, the cup of coffee I'm drinking, the relationship you and I are having—they're all systems."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8014–8017

The implication: the question is never whether your business has a system, but whether the systems it has are conscious, integrated, and serving the Primary Aim.

## The three kinds: Hard, Soft, Information

> "There are three kinds of systems in your business: Hard Systems, Soft Systems, and Information Systems."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8027–8028

**Hard Systems:**
> "Hard Systems are inanimate, unliving things. My computer is a Hard System, as are the colors in this office's reception area."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8029–8030

**Soft Systems:**
> "Soft Systems are either animate—living—or ideas. You are a Soft System; so is the script for Hamlet."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8032–8033

**Information Systems:**
> "Information Systems are those that provide us with information about the interaction between the other two. Inventory control, cash flow forecasting, and sales activity summary reports are all Information Systems."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8035–8038

The job of the Business Development Program is to innovate, quantify, orchestrate, and integrate all three.

## The Lucite-collar story

At E-Myth Worldwide the office standards insisted on white boards, blue markers, and white walls — all simultaneously. Erasing the boards at the end of meetings repeatedly sent blue ink onto the white walls. Memos, meetings, policies, Cleaning Teams, Wall Tours, and Board Spot Checks all failed. Then someone saw it as a conflict that needed a *system* answer, not a *people* answer. They installed a clear Lucite collar extending four inches out from each edge of every board.

> "And all because of a four-inch Lucite collar! A Hard System for producing a human and totally integrated result. A system solution to a typically people-intensive problem. Without anyone having to pay attention to it... After all, that's the purpose of a system—to free you to do the things you want to do."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8143–8152

The story is Gerber's purest illustration of a Hard System. The people-intensive approach (campaigns, signage, discipline) cost time every week and produced nothing. A single inanimate object produced the result automatically, forever, and freed the owner.

## 20% of salespeople / 80% of sales

Gerber picks up the most quoted cliché in sales and names its actual mechanism:

> "Everyone in business has heard the old saw: 80 percent of our sales are produced by 20 percent of our people. Unfortunately, few seem to know what the 20 percent are doing that the 80 percent aren't. Well, let me tell you. The 20 percent are using a system, and the 80 percent aren't."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8160–8168

The system is a Soft System — a scripted, fully orchestrated interaction between salesperson and customer:

> "A selling system is a Soft System."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8170

Its six primary steps:
1. Identification of the specific Benchmarks—or consumer decision points—in your selling process.
2. The literal scripting of the words that will get you to each one successfully (yes, written down like the script for a play!).
3. The creation of the various materials to be used with each script.
4. The memorization of each Benchmark's script.
5. The delivery of each script by your salespeople in identical fashion.
6. Leaving your people to communicate more effectively, by articulating, watching, listening, hearing, acknowledging, understanding, and engaging each and every prospect.
— Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8178–8191

Gerber reports a career-development company producing a 300% revenue increase in one year with untrained staff, an ad agency a 500% increase in two years, and a health spa a 40% increase in two months — all from handing novices the same Soft System.

## Selling is opening, not closing

The Power Point Selling Process has three Benchmarks: the Appointment Presentation, the Needs Analysis Presentation, and the Solutions Presentation. The reversal of conventional sales wisdom sits inside the second:

> "Most salespeople think that selling is 'closing.' It isn't. Selling is opening. That's what the Needs Analysis Presentation does. It opens up the prospective customer to a deeper experience of his frustration and to the opportunities available to him by going through the questioning process with you."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8397–8401

By the time the third Benchmark arrives, the sale is already made — the Solutions Presentation is only there to satisfy the conscious mind:

> "The Solutions Presentation simply provides the rational armament for the emotional commitment."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8419–8420

This mirrors the marketing principle from Ch. 17 — that buying decisions are made unconsciously and emotionally, and the conscious mind goes back out into the world to assemble rational justification after the fact.

## Information as glue

Every Benchmark in the Soft System can be counted (calls made, prospects reached, appointments scheduled, appointments held, Needs Analyses completed, Solutions Presentations completed, solutions sold, average dollar value). That is the Information System. It tells you the conversion rate between any two Benchmarks, which salesperson needs help at which step, who is on the system verbatim and who is off it, and the cost of making one sale.

> "If your Systems Strategy is the glue that holds your Franchise Prototype together, then information is the glue that holds your Systems Strategy together. It tells you when and why you need to change. Without it, you might as well put on a blindfold, have someone turn you around three times, and set out with a dart in your hand, waiting for a signal from the heavens to throw it."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8540–8548

Information is the feedback loop that lets the Prototype evolve. Without it, every change is guesswork.

## Conflict + Will = Resolution

The Lucite-collar story is also where Gerber states his equation for how systems are born:

> "Conflict without will creates frustration. An engine turning, but going nowhere. / Conflict with will creates resolution, a movement beyond the dilemma."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8121–8125

Two standards collide (clean walls vs. white boards + blue ink). That collision is the essential condition for innovation. What turns conflict into a system is *will* — the refusal to rest until the conflict is resolved systemically, not by another memo.

---

## Total interdependence

The chapter closes the Business Development Program by collapsing the seven strategies into one thing:

> "your Primary Aim and your Strategic Objective and your Organizational Strategy and your Management Strategy and your People Strategy and your Marketing Strategy and your Systems Strategy—all of them are totally interdependent, rather than independent of one another. That the success of your Business Development Program totally depends on your appreciation of that integration. And that your Prototype is that integration."
> — Ch. 18, full-text.txt:8576–8583

You cannot fix one strategy in isolation. A change to the Marketing Strategy changes what the Soft Systems must deliver, which changes the Information Systems you need to measure them, which changes the Position Contracts and therefore the Organization Chart. The Prototype *is* the integration.

## Ethical boundary

The Technician's objection to all of this is that systems dehumanize. Gerber rejects that outcome directly:

> "Are you beginning to understand that systematizing your business need not be a dehumanizing experience, but quite the opposite?... That hiring people, developing people, and keeping people requires a strategy built on an understanding of people completely foreign to most businesses?... That without an idea worth pursuing, there can be no People Strategy at all?"
> — Ch. 16, full-text.txt:7151–7164

The Lucite collar is the model: it removes a people-intensive chore that no human wanted to do and frees people for the work they were actually hired for. Systems replace rote work, not human judgment. The ethical line: systematize to free people, never to strip them of agency, dignity, or the "idea worth pursuing" that made them join in the first place.

## Three-systems audit template

For an existing small business, walk each function through three passes, grounded in Gerber's own examples:

**Pass 1 — Hard Systems (things).**
- What physical objects, layouts, colors, or tools are currently failing to produce an integrated result?
- Where are you trying to solve a recurring people-intensive problem with memos, meetings, signs, or policies? (The Lucite-collar test: if you've held more than one meeting about the same recurring breakdown, a Hard System is probably the real answer.)
- What conflict between two standards is quietly producing frustration? Name both standards explicitly.

**Pass 2 — Soft Systems (people and ideas).**
- For each customer-facing interaction — sales call, intake, delivery — is there a written script, memorized and delivered identically by every person every time?
- Have you named the Benchmarks (decision points) of your selling process? (Appointment → Needs Analysis → Solutions is Gerber's example; yours will differ in content, not in structure.)
- Does the Needs Analysis *open* the customer to their frustration, or does your process try to *close* them?

**Pass 3 — Information Systems (data about the other two).**
- For each Benchmark in your Soft System, can you produce the count (how many attempted, how many completed) and the conversion rate to the next Benchmark?
- Do you know the cost of making one sale, derived Benchmark-by-Benchmark?
- What single number would tell you *when and why* to change the Soft System? If no number exists, you are the blindfolded dart-thrower.

**Integration check.**
- Trace one customer journey from promise (Marketing) to sale (Soft System) to delivery (Operations) to measurement (Information). Is each handoff visible and accounted for, or does the chain break?
- Would a new hire on their first day, with only the documented systems, produce the same customer experience as your best veteran? If not, the systems are in heads, not in the Prototype.
