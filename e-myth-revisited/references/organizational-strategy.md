## Why Organization Chart first

Gerber opens Chapter 14 with a blunt, load-bearing claim: the Organization Chart isn't a bureaucratic formality — it's the single most leveraged move a small business can make.

> "the organizational development reflected in the Organization Chart can have a more profound impact on a small company than any other single Business Development step."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:5610–5612

The reason most small companies don't get this leverage is that they organize the wrong way:

> "Most companies organize around personalities rather than around functions. / That is, around people rather than accountabilities or responsibilities. / The result is almost always chaos."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:5616–5620

The absence of a chart pushes the business onto a rickety foundation:

> "without an Organization Chart, everything hinges on luck and good feelings, on the personalities of the people and the goodwill they share. / Unfortunately, personalities, good feelings, goodwill, and luck aren't the only ingredients of a successful organization; alone, they are the recipe for chaos and disaster."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:5750–5756

The chapter's epigraph from Theodore Levitt frames the stakes: "An organization is therefore a structured institution. If it is not structured, it is a mob. Mobs do not get things done, they destroy things." (full-text.txt:5590–5593).

## Jack and Murray's example

Gerber runs the same company twice to make the point. First, as a cautionary tale: Widget Makers as a pure personality-driven partnership.

Jack and Murray start by "sharing the work" — "taking turns, taking turns" (full-text.txt:5646–5647). They hire Jerry (a nephew), then Herb (Jack's wife's brother). Everyone does everything:

> "Everybody's opening up, answering the telephone, going out for sandwiches, making deposits—taking turns, taking turns, taking turns."
> — full-text.txt:5690–5691

Then the collapse. Widgets come back defective. The books look funny. Tools go missing. Nails appear in the screw boxes. Tempers mount. And no one can resolve any of it because:

> "If everybody's doing everything, then who's accountable for anything? / If Jack and Murray are partners, who's in charge?"
> — full-text.txt:5728–5730

Gerber then rewinds ("Let's start Widget Makers all over again," full-text.txt:5761) and walks through the version that works. Jack and Murray sit at the kitchen table and:

1. Each write a **Primary Aim** at the top of a sheet (full-text.txt:5790–5796).
2. Draw a line across the page with **SHAREHOLDERS** above and **EMPLOYEES** below — "they have agreed with each other that is to be their role outside of the business" (full-text.txt:5803–5809).
3. Do the research to complete a **Strategic Objective** (demographic work, a questionnaire, 150 Needs Analysis calls — full-text.txt:5814–5832).
4. Only *then* draw the Organization Chart.

The chart they draw is the chart for the finished business, with every named position and reporting line. The punchline Gerber drives home is the absurdity that makes the method work:

> "Jack and Murray sit back and look at the completed Organization Chart of Widget Makers, Inc., and smile. It sure looks like a big company. The only problem is that Jack and Murray's names will have to fill all the boxes! They're the only two employees."
> — full-text.txt:5883–5886

That "problem" is the point. The chart describes the finished business — the faces will change, the work won't:

> "Jack and Murray realize that there's no difference between the Widget Makers of today and the Widget Makers of tomorrow; the work is the same; only the faces will change."
> — full-text.txt:5894–5896

## The Position Contract

Once the chart exists, every box gets a **Position Contract**. Gerber is deliberate that this is not a job description.

> "A Position Contract (as we call it at E-Myth Worldwide) is a summary of the results to be achieved by each position in the company, the work the occupant of that position is accountable for, a list of standards by which the results are to be evaluated, and a line for the signature of the person who agrees to fulfill those accountabilities."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:5901–5905

The distinction from a job description is explicit:

> "Jack and Murray know that a Position Contract is not a job description. / It is a contract, rather than just a description, between the company and an employee, a summary of the rules of the company's game."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:5907–5911

And the word "accountability" is given a literal meaning that the signature line enforces:

> "Accountability literally means 'stand up and be counted.'"
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:5916

> "Therefore, the Position Contract is the document that identifies who's to stand up and what they're being counted on to produce."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:5918–5919

The contract has five components: summary of results, the work, standards for evaluation, accountabilities, and a signature. The signature is the commitment device; it makes the role real whether the occupant is a new hire or the owner temporarily filling in.

## Signing yourself into every box

After writing Position Contracts, Jack and Murray — "as shareholders" — proceed to "the most critical task of their new association: naming the people to put in the boxes" (full-text.txt:5926–5929). Because there are only two of them, every box gets Jack's or Murray's signature.

The COO slot forces a choice — "There can't be two Chief Operating Officers of a company. Someone's got to be accountable. Someone's got to be in charge" (full-text.txt:5942–5943). Jack takes COO, then signs as VP/Operations and VP/Finance. Murray takes VP/Marketing, Sales Manager, and Advertising/Research Manager. They notice Jack has eight boxes to Murray's three and rebalance: Murray picks up Accounts Receivable, Accounts Payable, and Service Manager — six jobs each (full-text.txt:6007–6016).

The telling detail: they hadn't done any actual work yet.

> "Not a bit of work had been performed on the job, and yet the two of them were able to conceive of the company, the work that needs to be done, the standards by which they would hold each position accountable, and which position is accountable to which position and specifically for what."
> — full-text.txt:6023–6027

> "In creating their Organization Chart, Jack and Murray had also generated the blueprint for their Franchise Prototype."
> — full-text.txt:6040–6041

## Prototype from the bottom up

Having drawn the chart for the finished business and signed into every box, Jack and Murray begin the prototyping process — but deliberately not at the top.

> "Having created a picture of the business as it will look when it's finally done, Jack and Murray start the prototyping process. / But at the bottom of the organization, not at the top. / They start working on the business where they start working in the business. / In the position of Salesperson and Production Person and Accounts Receivable Clerk. / Not as the owners or partners or shareholders. Not as the COO or the VP/Marketing. / But as employees, at the very bottom of the organization. Doing Tactical Work, not Strategic Work."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:6045–6060

Gerber draws a sharp line between the two modes of work:

> "Tactical Work is the work all technicians do. / Strategic Work is the work their managers do."
> — full-text.txt:6062–6064

And he names the chart as the vehicle that makes the transition from one to the other possible:

> "If Jack and Murray's business is going to thrive, they have to find other people to do the Tactical Work so as to free Jack and Murray to do the Strategic Work. / The Organization Chart is the means through which that critical transition can be made."
> — full-text.txt:6066–6071

The mechanism: while Murray works *in* the Salesperson position, he simultaneously works *on* it as VP/Marketing — applying Innovation, Quantification, and Orchestration. He tests clothing colors and styles, tests scripts, tests words. When something works, it goes into the Widget Makers Sales Operations Manual (full-text.txt:6109–6127).

## Replace yourself with a system

Only *after* the Sales Operations Manual is complete does Murray hire — and specifically not a veteran:

> "But not for someone with sales experience. / Not a Master Technician. But a novice. A beginner. An Apprentice. / Someone eager to learn how to do it right."
> — full-text.txt:6132–6136

The ad: "COME AND SEE OUR TURN-KEY OPERATION. COME AND SEE OUR MONEY MACHINE. NO EXPERIENCE NECESSARY. JUST AN OPEN MIND AND A WILLINGNESS TO LEARN." (full-text.txt:6146–6149).

When the new hire takes over the system, the moment is load-bearing:

> "At that moment, at that exact instant, Murray moves up to the position of Sales Manager and begins the process of Business Development all over again. / Because at that moment, Murray has taken the most important step in freeing himself from the Tactical Work of his business. Murray has replaced himself with a system that works in the hands of a person who wants to work it. / And now Murray's job becomes managing the system rather than doing the work. / Murray is now engaged in Strategic Work."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:6171–6183

Jack does the same for each of his Tactical positions. Both are "working on the business while working in it, and all according to plan" (full-text.txt:6188–6189).

## The flow: Primary Aim → Strategic Objective → Org Chart

Gerber closes the chapter by naming the causal chain that the whole exercise depends on:

> "That your Organization Chart flows down from your Strategic Objective, which in turn flows down from your Primary Aim. / That each is the cause of the one preceding it, and each, therefore, plays a part in the fulfillment of the one before it. / A logic is established, an integrated whole."
> — Ch. 14, full-text.txt:6196–6202

Without the chart, "confusion, discord, and conflict become the order of the day"; with it, "the direction, purpose, and style of the business are balanced, interacting purposefully and progressing with intention and integrity toward a cohesive and sensible whole" (full-text.txt:6206–6211).

## Worked structure

The only worked structure Gerber gives in Chapter 14 is Widget Makers, Inc. Here it is, verbatim from full-text.txt:5855–5882, with the Position Contract components each box would need (results / work / standards / accountabilities / signature) implied by Gerber's own definition at 5901–5905.

- **President / Chief Operating Officer (COO)** — "accountable for the overall achievement of the Strategic Objective and reporting to the SHAREHOLDERS who include, on an equal basis, Jack and Murray."
- **Vice-President / Marketing** — "accountable for finding customers and finding new ways to provide customers with the satisfactions they derive from widgets, at lower cost, and with greater ease, and reporting to the COO."
- **Vice-President / Operations** — "accountable for keeping customers by delivering to them what is promised by Marketing, and for discovering new ways of assembling widgets, at lower cost, and with greater efficiency so as to provide the customer with better service, reporting to the COO."
- **Vice-President / Finance** — "accountable for supporting both Marketing and Operations in the fulfillment of their accountabilities by achieving the company's profitability standards, and by securing capital whenever it's needed, and at the best rates, also reporting to the COO."
- **Reporting to VP/Marketing:** Sales Manager, Advertising/Research Manager.
- **Reporting to VP/Operations:** Production Manager, Service Manager, Facilities Manager.
- **Reporting to VP/Finance:** Accounts Receivable Manager, Accounts Payable Manager.

Every box needs a Position Contract stating the results the position must produce, the specific work accountabilities (e.g., for Sales Manager, the work codified in the Sales Operations Manual — scripts for incoming calls, outgoing calls, meeting the customer at the door, responses to inquiries, order entry, returns, new product requests, inventory; full-text.txt:6123–6127), the standards by which results are measured (drawn from the Strategic Objective's standards — money, demographics, psychographics, date of completion), the reporting line, and the signature.

Sarah's bakery (All About Pies) is referenced at the end of the chapter — Sarah paraphrases the method back: "I need to create an Organization Chart for All About Pies as it will look when it's done, seven years from now, rather than the way it is now?" (full-text.txt:6227–6229). Gerber does not draw her chart in Ch. 14, so no invented structure appears here.
