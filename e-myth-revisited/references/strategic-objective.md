# Strategic Objective — Reference

Deep-dive on Chapter 13 of *The E-Myth Revisited*. Every claim below is anchored to a direct quote with a line number from `extraction-notes/full-text.txt` (primary), cross-referenced with `extraction-notes/02-frameworks.md`, `extraction-notes/03-copy-patterns.md`, and `extraction-notes/04-case-studies.md`.

## What it is

The Strategic Objective is not a business plan — it is a list of standards that translate your Primary Aim into measurable shape for the business.

Gerber defines it directly: "Your Strategic Objective is a very clear statement of what your business has to ultimately do for you to achieve your Primary Aim" (full-text.txt:5047-5048).

It is "the vision of the finished product that is and will be your business" (full-text.txt:5050-5051), and its job is to keep the business a means, not an end: "your business is a means rather than an end, a vehicle to enrich your life rather than one that drains the life you have" (full-text.txt:5053-5054).

Critically, it lives downstream of your Life Plan, not your Business Plan:

> "Your Strategic Objective is not a business plan. It is a product of your Life Plan, as well as your Business Strategy and Plan. Your Life Plan shapes your life, and the business that is to serve it." (full-text.txt:5056-5058)

Where a Business Strategy and Plan "provide the structure within which your business is intended to operate over time to fulfill your Life Plan" (full-text.txt:5058-5060), the Strategic Objective reduces all of that to "a set of simple and clearly stated standards" (full-text.txt:5070-5071).

Gerber summarizes the purpose in four short lines:

> "Your Strategic Objective is just such a list of standards. / It is a tool for measuring your progress toward a specific end. / It is designed for implementation, not for rationalization. / It is a template for your business, to make certain that the time you invest in it produces exactly what you want from it." (full-text.txt:5074-5081)

## Money as the first standard

"The first standard of your Strategic Objective is money. Gross revenues. How big is your vision? How big will your company be when it's finally done? Will it be a $300,000 company? A million-dollar company? A $500-million company?" (full-text.txt:5088-5091).

Gerber presses: "If you don't know the answer, how can you possibly know whether your business can help you realize your Primary Aim?" (full-text.txt:5093-5094).

Gross revenues alone are not enough — "you also have to know what your gross profits are going to be, your pretax profits, your after-tax profits" (full-text.txt:5096-5098).

He anticipates the obvious objection (you can't really know future sales) and dismisses it:

> "The answer is, you can't! But it doesn't matter. At the beginning of your business, any standards are better than no standards. Creating money standards is not just strategically necessary for your business; it is strategically necessary for your life, for the realization of your Primary Aim." (full-text.txt:5104-5108)

Then he reframes the money question from income to assets:

> "How much money do I need to live the way I wish? Not in income but in assets. In other words, how much money do you need in order to be independent of work, to be free?" (full-text.txt:5114-5117)

This reframing leads to Gerber's most provocative claim about building a business at all:

> "In fact, there is ultimately only one reason to create a business of your own, and that is to sell it! To do it, to finish it, and then to get paid for it! Just like Ray Kroc did, to create your Franchise Prototype, to turn-key your business, to create a business that really works, and then sell it." (full-text.txt:5119-5125)

The money standard therefore has three nested parts:

1. **Gross revenues at completion** — "How big will your company be when it's finally done?" (full-text.txt:5089-5090)
2. **Profits** — gross, pretax, and after-tax (full-text.txt:5096-5098)
3. **Sale price and date** — "Ten times earnings? Twenty times earnings?… When do you want it? Three years from now? Five years?" (full-text.txt:5126-5129)

The logic is that a sellable business — "a little money machine. An absolutely predictable little business that does what it promises to do every single time" (full-text.txt:5137-5139) — is the only kind that can free you.

## The Opportunity Worth Pursuing test

Once the money standards are set, the business must have "a realistic chance of achieving those standards, of producing that return on your investment" (full-text.txt:5149-5151). That check is the second standard: Opportunity Worth Pursuing.

> "An Opportunity Worth Pursuing is a business that can fulfill the financial standards you've created for your Primary Aim and your Strategic Objective." (full-text.txt:5159-5161)

If the business can reasonably fulfill them, pursue it. If not, walk away:

> "If it is unreasonable to assume that it can, then no matter how exciting, interesting, or appealing the business is, forget it. Walk away from it. It will consume too much of your precious time and prevent you from finding a true Opportunity Worth Pursuing." (full-text.txt:5166-5169)

The diagnostic is a single question:

> "Does the business I have in mind alleviate a frustration experienced by a large enough group of consumers to make it worth my while?" (full-text.txt:5172-5174)

This collapses a two-part test into one operational filter:

- **Financial fit** — can it hit the Primary-Aim-driven numbers?
- **Market fit** — is the frustration real, and is the frustrated crowd large enough?

"This standard fulfills two primary requirements of your Strategic Objective. It tells you what kind of business you're creating while it defines who your customer will be. It tells you what you need to sell and to whom" (full-text.txt:5176-5179).

## Commodity vs. Product

Asked what kind of business they're in, most owners name the commodity. Gerber rejects this reflex:

> "Ask anyone what kind of business they're in and they'll instinctively respond with the name of the commodity they sell. 'We're in the computer business.' Or, 'We're in the hot tub business.' Always the commodity, never the product." (full-text.txt:5183-5186)

His distinction:

> "The commodity is the thing your customer actually walks out with in his hand. / The product is what your customer feels as he walks out of your business. / What he feels about your business, not what he feels about the commodity." (full-text.txt:5190-5196)

The canonical illustration is Charles Revson of Revlon:

> "In the factory Revlon manufactures cosmetics, but in the store Revlon sells hope." (full-text.txt:5202-5203)

The commodity is cosmetics; the product is hope (full-text.txt:5205). Gerber then walks through a Chanel TV commercial — "Not a word about perfume. That's the commodity. The commercial is selling the product—fantasy" (full-text.txt:5232-5233) — to make the same move in a second medium.

Why this belongs inside the Strategic Objective (not the Marketing Strategy): the product — the feeling — is what the business is fundamentally committing to deliver. You cannot set standards for delivery if you have not named what you are delivering.

> "The truth is, nobody's interested in the commodity. / People buy feelings." (full-text.txt:5241-5243)

And the trend is intensifying: "as the world becomes more and more complex, and the commodities more varied, the feelings we want become more urgent, less rational, more unconscious" (full-text.txt:5245-5247).

So the Strategic Objective must name the feeling explicitly:

> "What's your product? What feeling will your customer walk away with? Peace of mind? Order? Power? Love? What is he really buying when he buys from you?" (full-text.txt:5237-5239)

## Demographics + Psychographics standards

If people buy feelings, then you need to know whose feelings — and why. Gerber splits this into two sciences, each one a standard in the Objective.

**Demographics.** "Every business has a Central Demographic Model. That is, a most probable customer. And that customer has a whole set of characteristics through which you can define him—age, sex, income, family status, education, profession, and so forth" (full-text.txt:5257-5260).

Gerber's one-line definition:

> "Demographics is the science of marketplace reality. It tells you who your customer is." (full-text.txt:5262-5263)

**Psychographics.** The Central Demographic Model customer "buys for very particular reasons, none of which are rational or even explicable! Yet he buys, or doesn't. The motivations that propel him in either direction constitute your Central Psychographic Model" (full-text.txt:5265-5270).

Gerber's companion definition:

> "Psychographics is the science of perceived marketplace reality. It tells you why your customer buys." (full-text.txt:5272-5273)

Each becomes a standard because the Opportunity Worth Pursuing test depends on both:

> "when you ask, 'Is this business an Opportunity Worth Pursuing?' the only way to tell is to determine how many selling opportunities you have (your customers' demographics) and how successfully you can satisfy the emotional or perceived needs lurking there (your customers' psychographics)." (full-text.txt:5275-5279)

Demographics tells you whether the pool is large enough. Psychographics tells you whether you can actually move it. Together they close the loop that started with "a frustration experienced by a large enough group of consumers" (full-text.txt:5172-5174).

## Other standards in the Objective

Gerber explicitly declines to cap the list: "There is no specific number of standards in your Strategic Objective. There are only specific questions that need to be answered" (full-text.txt:5283-5284).

The enumerated Ch. 13 questions are:

> "When is your Prototype going to be completed? In two years? Three? Ten? / Where are you going to be in business? Locally? Regionally? Nationally? Internationally? / How are you going to be in business? Retail? Wholesale? A combination of the two? / What standards are you going to insist upon regarding reporting, cleanliness, clothing, management, hiring, firing, training, and so forth?" (full-text.txt:5286-5294)

So the Objective spans:

- **Completion date** — two, three, or ten years to a finished Prototype
- **Geographic scope** — local, regional, national, or international footprint
- **Delivery mode** — retail, wholesale, or a combination
- **Operational standards** — reporting, cleanliness, clothing, management, hiring, firing, training, "and so forth"

Gerber's summary: "the standards you create for your business will shape both your business and the experience you have of your business" (full-text.txt:5295-5297). The list is open-ended by design, because the Strategic Objective is less a template than a commitment to keep naming standards until the business has a concrete finished shape.

## Sarah's Objective — worked example

When Sarah articulates her Strategic Objective at the start of her working session with Gerber, she does it in seven-year terms, using every layer of the framework at once.

**Completion date and geography.** "I see my business about seven years from now… There are four locations: the one I have now and three more" (full-text.txt:5473-5474) — naming the three adjoining communities. Seven years to a finished Prototype; four locations across four adjacent towns.

**Money standards.** "I see the shops producing annual sales of $450,000 each, for a total of $1,800,000 a year. I'm not totally certain what the net profit will be, but I've decided that it should be about 15 percent, or $67,500 for each shop, for a total of $270,000 for the four" (full-text.txt:5483-5486).

That is $1.8M in gross revenues and $270K in profit — a 15% margin up from her current 11% (full-text.txt:5487-5488).

**Sale price.** "That means if I sell the business in seven years, given a realistic price/earnings ratio, I think it's called, I should be able to sell the business for more than $1 million" (full-text.txt:5490-5492).

**Primary-Aim logic.** "One million dollars in seven years—that's my dream… Because, first, I won't ever need any more money than that to do everything I have ever wanted, and second, because it's really a great round number to shoot for. It sort of makes everything I need to do really concrete" (full-text.txt:5492-5497).

Note the move: the $1M number is derived from her life needs, not market benchmarks — a textbook application of "How much money do I need to live the way I wish? Not in income but in assets" (full-text.txt:5114-5117).

**Product (the feeling).** Grounded in her aunt's kitchen: "I want the business, All About Pies, to be all about caring, not about pies" (full-text.txt:5523-5524).

This later becomes the "It" of her business: "The 'It' of your business, Sarah, is Caring" (full-text.txt:7353).

Sarah's Objective shows every standard firing together: commodity (pies) vs. product (caring), demographics (four named communities), money (gross, margin, sale price), completion date (7 years), delivery mode (retail shops), and geography (adjacent towns). See `04-case-studies.md` (Case 1) for full context.

## How tension becomes energy

The standards are not decorative — they are the engine.

> "the standards of your Strategic Objective create the tension that draws the future model of your business and the way it actually appears today closer to one another." (full-text.txt:5299-5301)

And generalizing beyond one business:

> "standards create the energy by which the best companies, and the most effective people, produce results." (full-text.txt:5303-5304)

This is why Gerber insists any standards beat no standards at the outset (full-text.txt:5104-5105). The gap between a concrete future and a concrete present is what pulls work forward; without numbers, dates, geography, and a named feeling, there is no gap to close, so there is no energy.

The Strategic Objective manufactures that tension on purpose. Sarah's $1.8M / 7-year / 4-shop / "Caring" picture is not a forecast — it is a tension generator. The further it sits from her current one-shop, 11%-margin reality, the more energy it releases to drive disciplined execution.
