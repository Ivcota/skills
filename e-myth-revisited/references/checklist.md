# E-Myth Checklist — Reference

End-to-end worksheet for applying *The E-Myth Revisited* to an existing small business. Each step cites the chapter it comes from. Work through the parts in order — mindset first, then the seven strategies, then the first step. Revisit the anti-pattern self-audit whenever the business feels stuck.

## Part 1 — Mindset reset

- [ ] I accept the typical E/M/T imbalance (10/20/70) and the Fatal Assumption — that understanding the technical work of a business is *not* the same as understanding a business that does that technical work (Ch. 2)
- [ ] I commit to working ON the business, not IN it — treating the business as a product that must work without me (Ch. 6–7)
- [ ] I'm designing the business as the Franchise Prototype — the pre-production model for 5,000 perfect replicates, where the 5,000th unit runs as smoothly as the first (Ch. 8–9)
- [ ] I adopt Innovation, Quantification, Orchestration as continuous practice — the backbone of every extraordinary business (Ch. 10)
- [ ] I have stopped conflating the business with myself — I see the business as a separate organism whose sole function is to find and keep customers (Ch. 3, Ch. 9)

## Part 2 — The seven strategies

### Primary Aim (Ch. 12)

- [ ] Written answers to: What do I value most? What kind of life do I want? What do I want my life to look like, to feel like? Who do I wish to be?
- [ ] Written funeral-tape eulogy — the script I want played for the mourners
- [ ] 2-year / 10-year / 20-year vision written
- [ ] Money needed (in *assets*, not income) to be free of work stated, with a by-when date
- [ ] Aim is written down so the business can "know" it — I know it first, in words, on paper

### Strategic Objective (Ch. 13)

- [ ] Gross revenue target and completion date for the finished Prototype
- [ ] Profit target and sellable-multiple target stated (the business exists ultimately to be sold)
- [ ] Opportunity Worth Pursuing test passed — the business alleviates a frustration experienced by a large enough group of consumers
- [ ] Commodity (what the customer walks out with) distinguished from product (what the customer *feels* as they walk out)
- [ ] Demographic profile of the Central Demographic Model written — *who* buys
- [ ] Psychographic profile written — *why* they buy
- [ ] Standards written for: location, delivery mode (retail/wholesale/etc.), cleanliness, clothing, reporting, hiring, firing, training
- [ ] Every standard tested against the Primary Aim — "What will serve my Primary Aim?"

### Organizational Strategy (Ch. 14)

- [ ] Organization Chart for the *finished* business drawn — built from the Strategic Objective, not from today's headcount
- [ ] Chart organizes around functions and accountabilities, not personalities
- [ ] My own name written into every seat I currently occupy
- [ ] Position Contract written and signed for each role — summarizing results, work, standards, and accountability
- [ ] Plan in place to prototype each position bottom-up, starting where I currently work *in* the business, so I can replace myself with a system

### Management Strategy (Ch. 15)

- [ ] The "It" of my business defined in one word — the core purpose-word that expresses what I believe in and why people buy from me
- [ ] "It" expressed concretely in every touchpoint (how we answer the phone, take the money, ship the order)
- [ ] One color-coded checklist written for each critical task (Operations Manual = "a series of checklists")
- [ ] Hierarchy of Systems drafted: How We Do It Here / How We Recruit, Hire, Train People to Do It Here / How We Manage It Here / How We Change It Here
- [ ] Management System is designed into the Prototype itself — it produces the marketing result automatically, not through elite managers
- [ ] I am delegating tasks but not accountability — I set the standard, hired managers execute to it

### People Strategy (Ch. 16)

- [ ] The idea of the business — the "game" I'm running — is clearly articulated
- [ ] The game comes first; what people do comes second (Rule 1)
- [ ] It is a game I would play myself (Rule 2)
- [ ] The game's strategy (its ethic) is written and treated as sacrosanct; tactics can change, the strategy cannot
- [ ] Scripted hiring sequence in place: group presentation of the idea → individual interview → structured first-day training
- [ ] The game is communicated *at the outset* of every hire — never after
- [ ] The game is real, not contrived — I would not be embarrassed to have employees hold me to it

### Marketing Strategy (Ch. 17)

- [ ] I have forgotten what *I* want and started with the customer
- [ ] Free-pie / equivalent questionnaire deployed to gather demographic + psychographic data from actual customers
- [ ] Central Demographic Model's unconscious expectations mapped to concrete Prototype elements (colors, shapes, words, sounds)
- [ ] I'm finding a *perceived* need and filling it — not the need I assumed existed
- [ ] Promise made at the door matches what Operations can deliver — before the customer leaves
- [ ] Lead Generation / Lead Conversion / Client Fulfillment designed as one continuous process, not three departments
- [ ] Primary marketing objective is getting existing customers to come back

### Systems Strategy (Ch. 18)

- [ ] Hard Systems (inanimate things — the Lucite collar, the layout, the signage) inventoried
- [ ] Soft Systems (scripts, Benchmarks, selling sequences) written and orchestrated
- [ ] Information Systems in place to tell me what's happening in the Hard and Soft systems — and to flag when to change them
- [ ] Selling treated as *opening* (Needs Analysis → Solutions Presentation), not closing
- [ ] All three system types integrated so the Prototype runs as one orchestrated unit
- [ ] "Conflict + Will = Resolution" — I am applying will to the conflicts the information surfaces, not just tolerating frustration

## Part 3 — Taking the first step (Afterword)

- [ ] I have stepped back and looked at my business through E-Myth eyes
- [ ] I have analyzed what the business is today
- [ ] I have decided what it must look like when finished
- [ ] I have named the gap — and accepted that the gap is always created by the absence of systems
- [ ] I have done something *today* to close the gap — because not until I do something will I understand it

## Anti-pattern self-audit

Score each honestly: Y = I'm doing this / N = I'm not. Any Y is an alarm bell pointing back to a specific chapter.

- [ ] **Fatal Assumption** — Am I still equating my craft skill with business skill? (Ch. 2)
- [ ] **Entrepreneurial Seizure** — Did I start this to escape a job, and am I now doing a dozen jobs I don't know how to do? (Ch. 2)
- [ ] **Tactical view** — Am I jumping in to do the work I see, instead of doing the strategic work that would grow the business? (Ch. 3)
- [ ] **Hiring as relief** — Am I hiring to escape work I hate (e.g., "hand the books to Harry") instead of hiring to build the system? (Ch. 4)
- [ ] **Management by Abdication** — Am I handing work off and disappearing, then snatching it back in frustration? (Ch. 4, Ch. 11)
- [ ] **Dependence on extraordinary people** — Am I counting on star hires to make the business work, instead of designing it so ordinary people produce extraordinary results? (Ch. 11)
- [ ] **Management by Luck** — Am I drifting from crisis to crash program with no Business Development Process? (Ch. 1)
- [ ] **Business = me** — Does the business depend on my personality, style, presence — so customers are buying *my* ability, not the business's? (Ch. 3, Ch. 9)
- [ ] **Built around the commodity** — Is the business designed to satisfy the Technician who created it, rather than the customer it serves? (Ch. 7)
- [ ] **Going for Broke** — Am I running on luck, speed, and technical brilliance, with demand outrunning my capacity to produce consistent results? (Ch. 4)

Every "Yes" above is a cue to reread the cited chapter and return to the relevant Part 2 section until the answer is "No."
