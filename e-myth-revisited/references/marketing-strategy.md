# Marketing Strategy — Reference

Ch. 17 of *The E-Myth Revisited*. Marketing is not a department; it is the lens through which every element of the Prototype is engineered — because the customer's unconscious mind is the only judge that matters.

## Forget what you want

Ch. 17 opens by disqualifying the owner's preferences entirely. The Marketing Strategy is not about the business; it is about the customer, who is probably not the person the owner imagines.

> "Your Marketing Strategy starts, ends, lives, and dies with your customer."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7404–7405

> "So in the development of your Marketing Strategy, it is absolutely imperative that you forget about your dreams, forget about your visions, forget about your interests, forget about what you want—forget about everything but your customer! When it comes to marketing, what you want is unimportant. It's what your customer wants that matters. And what your customer wants is probably significantly different from what you think he wants."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7407–7417

## Buying decisions are made unconsciously

Gerber's model of the buyer is two minds in sequence. The Conscious Mind is a sensor — an "antenna" that takes in all the sensory data around it: the colors, shapes, sounds, and smells of the store, the office, or the restaurant; and from the salesperson, how he stands or sits, the color of his hair, how his hair is combed, the expression on his face, the crease in his slacks, whether his shoes are shined, and whether the laces are tied (full-text.txt:7428–7446). "Nothing escapes the sensor."

But the sensor does not decide. The Unconscious Mind does.

> "It's like a vast, dark, underground sea in which a multitude of exotic creatures swim about, single and in schools, silently seeking out food, each with entirely different needs and tastes. Those creatures are your customer's expectations… every creature in that sea—every one of those expectations—is a product of your customer's life! Of his reactions, perceptions, attitudes, associations, beliefs, opinions, inferences, conclusions."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7484–7502

The Conscious Mind feeds the Unconscious Mind sensory input; the Unconscious Mind tastes it, and says yes or no — instantly, at the moment of first taste (7513–7519). Only *after* that silent verdict does the Conscious Mind go back out and build a rational case for what the customer had already decided emotionally.

> "the Unconscious Mind sends its answer up to the Conscious Mind, which then goes back out into the world to assemble the rational armament it needs to support its already determined emotional commitment. And that's how buying decisions are made. Irrationally! If anyone cared to do it, it could probably be proved that no one yet has ever made a rational decision to buy anything!"
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7530–7541

The practical evidence Gerber cites for the speed of this verdict: in a television commercial the sale is made or lost in the first three or four seconds; in a print ad 75% of buying decisions are made at the headline alone; in a sales presentation the sale is made or lost in the first three minutes (7521–7528). When a customer says "I want to think about it," he is not thinking. He has already done all the thinking he is going to do. Either he is emotionally incapable of saying no, or the "food" his Unconscious Mind craves was never served (7543–7561).

## Demographics and psychographics

The two pillars. Gerber defines them verbatim — do not paraphrase these; they are a matched pair.

> "Demographics and psychographics are the two essential pillars supporting a successful marketing program. If you know who your customer is—demographics—you can then determine why he buys—psychographics. And having done so, you can then begin to construct a Prototype to satisfy his unconscious needs, but scientifically rather than arbitrarily."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7577–7584

> "demographics is the science of marketplace reality. It tells you who buys. Psychographics is the science of perceived marketplace reality. It tells you why certain demographic types buy for one reason while other demographic types buy for another."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7586–7591

Each demographic model has a specific set of perceptions identifiable in advance. Women of a given age, education, family size, and geography buy for specific psychographic reasons; a different demographic group's reasons will be predictably different (full-text.txt:7702–7713).

## Central Demographic Model

The Central Demographic Model is the single ideal-customer profile that every perceivable element of the Prototype is then engineered to match — color, shape, word, sound, smell, the crease in the salesperson's slacks. IBM Blue is Gerber's exemplar: the specific shade of blue was chosen because it has "extraordinarily high appeal and preference to IBM's Central Demographic Model" — "True Blue," the color IBM's consumer knows it can depend on.

> "Because that shade of blue has an extraordinarily high appeal and preference to IBM's Central Demographic Model. They see that shade of blue, and it's love at first sight!"
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7601–7604

The navy-suit vs. orange-suit thought experiment makes the point harder: the same man in a deep navy suit reads as solid, reliable, trustworthy; the same man in a two-piece orange suit with white lizard cowboy boots is "out of business." The difference is not in the man — it is in your Unconscious Mind, perceived instantly, without thought (full-text.txt:7641–7675).

Operationally, this means: once you know your Central Demographic Model, every choice in the Prototype (stationery, logo, signs, scripts, uniforms, music, packaging) becomes a scientific question. Gerber contrasts this with how small-business owners normally decide: "Stationery designed by the local quick-printer with a logo thrown in. Colors picked by their wives. Signs designed by the local sign guy whose experience is in painting signs, not in determining what colors and shapes are psychographically correct" (full-text.txt:7871–7875). He calls this default state "willful disinformation" — deciding what you want to do "without any information at all, without any interest in what's true, and then simply [doing] it" (7868–7871).

The corrective is not to "go over the scientific deep end" but to become "a student of the art of business and the science of business" — sensitive to the same psychographic questions McDonald's, Federal Express, Disney, Walmart, Pepsico, and American Express spend enormous amounts of money to answer (7876–7885). "In a small business you simply can't afford to spend the money they do. But you can afford to spend the time, the thought, the attention, on the same questions they ask" (7887–7889).

## Perceived need, not need

Reality, for marketing purposes, does not exist outside of the customer's perception. The common dictum is wrong by one word.

> "Reality only exists in someone's perceptions, attitudes, beliefs, conclusions—whatever you wish to call those positions of the mind from which all expectations arise—and nowhere else. So the famous dictum that says, 'Find a need and fill it,' is inaccurate. It should say, 'Find a perceived need and fill it.' Because if your customer doesn't perceive he needs something, he doesn't, even if he actually does."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7681–7690

A real need the customer cannot perceive is, for marketing purposes, not a need. This is why the job is not to educate the customer into rational agreement — it is to feed the Unconscious Mind the sensory input it already expects.

Gerber draws the direct consequence: "And if you know his demographics, you can understand what those perceptions are, and then figure out what you must do to satisfy them and the expectations they produce. You can know your customer's psychographic reality" (full-text.txt:7697–7701). Each demographic model has a specific, pre-identifiable set of perceptions; a different demographic group — different age, marital status, education, geography — will hold different unconsciously-held reasons, and those differences predetermine what each group buys (7702–7713). Until the owner takes this seriously, the Prototype is "a crap shoot" (7715–7721).

## The questionnaire-for-pie tactic

When Sarah (All About Pies) asks *how* to determine her demographics and psychographics, Gerber's answer is concrete, cheap, and immediate: ask your existing customers, and bribe them with a pie.

> "Who are my customers, specifically? What is their Demographic Profile? How do you answer that question? You ask them! You ask each and every one of them, by having them complete a questionnaire in return for a free pie! The free pie is the price you pay for that information. The answers you get will prove to be a bonanza!"
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7819–7829

The same questionnaire collects psychographic signal: what colors, shapes, and words customers prefer; what brands of perfume, automobile, clothing, jewelry, and food they buy. Match those brands to the ads and commercials that sold them, and you discover — from companies already succeeding with your customer — what messages your Prototype should be sending (full-text.txt:7831–7841). The insight here is subtle: you do not have to commission original psychographic research. Brands your customers already buy have already paid for it, and their ads are the output.

The addresses on the questionnaires then define your **Trading Zone**. Gerber's procedure is literal: take the addresses from the questionnaires, identify them on a map, draw a line around them, and that is your first-pass Trading Zone. Then buy a list of demographically correct prospects living inside that zone (full-text.txt:7843–7853). The effect is compounding — your existing customers become the sampling instrument that locates the next wave of customers who resemble them.

## Lead Generation → Lead Conversion → Client Fulfillment as one process

Gerber's most operationally loaded claim in Ch. 17: marketing is not the marketing department. The entire business process — from the promise that attracts the customer to the delivery that sends him home satisfied — *is* the marketing process. Every position in the company asks marketing questions, because every position shapes what the business is "in the mind of our customers."

> "if by marketing we mean, 'What must our business be in the mind of our customers in order for them to choose us over everyone else?' And so, seen from the appropriate perspective, the entire business process by which your company does what it does is a marketing process. It starts with the promise you make to attract them to your door. It continues with the sale you make once they get there. And it ends with the delivery of the promise before they leave your door. In some companies that process is called Lead Generation, Lead Conversion, Client Fulfillment. In your business, Sarah, it's called Marketing, Sales, and Operations."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7906–7923

The COO is the driver who connects the three parts — where Hierarchy (the Organization Chart) and Process (Lead Gen → Conversion → Fulfillment) meet. "It is there, at that point in the middle, that your business comes together… It is at that point, the point I have called the Power Point in an earlier book, that a business truly becomes alive" (full-text.txt:7963–7970). The static and dynamic nature of every great business meets there: "This is how we do it here," repeated "over and over, and still over again" — continuous improvement, "electrifying, ecstatic, alive" (7972–7978).

The consequence for the owner: there is no function or position within the company that is free of asking marketing questions. The owner starts by asking them. The COO continues asking them. The VP/Marketing is accountable for asking them. Every role shapes what the business is in the mind of the customer, so every role is a marketing role (7897–7908).

## Promise and delivery as one ethical unit

Marketing, Sales, and Operations are three accountabilities but one purpose. The promise is worthless unless Operations delivers it; the delivery is worthless unless Marketing made the right promise in the first place. Gerber binds the three functions into a single ethical unit.

> "while the VP/Marketing and the VP/Operations and the VP/Finance each have their own specific accountabilities, they share one common purpose—to make a promise their customer wants to hear, and to deliver on that promise better than anyone else on the block!"
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7948–7951

> "committed to keeping a promise no competitor would dare to make."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7985–7986

The ethical consequence: you cannot promote harder than you can deliver, and you cannot operate without knowing what promise Operations is on the hook to keep. Marketing copy that outruns operational capacity is not "aggressive marketing" — it is a broken promise in slow motion.

Gerber's endgame for the whole strategy is a single line that reads as both a marketing goal and a moral commitment: "To deliver the promise no one else in your industry dares to make!" (full-text.txt:7982). That is the test. If a competitor can make the same promise without fear, the promise is not yet yours. If you make it but cannot deliver it, it is not a promise at all — it is an advertisement for your eventual failure. The promise and the delivery live or die together.

## The unforgiving age

Before the Primary Aim section, Ch. 17 plants a warning that frames why all of this matters now. Gerber calls the present moment the "unforgiving age" — a marketplace in which the customer is deluged by so many products and promises that he is "swamped in confusion and indecision."

> "We have entered the 'unforgiving age.' An age in which countless small businesses will either accept the challenge of an information-glutted society or be destroyed by it. An age in which your customer is deluged by so many products and promises that he becomes swamped in confusion and indecision. The challenge of our age is to learn our customer's language. And then to speak that language clearly and well so that your voice can be heard above the din. Because if your customer doesn't hear you, he'll pass you by."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7754–7766

The argument sharpens for small businesses specifically: "Your business is far more fragile than a big business. So if anything, you must take marketing more seriously than a big business does" (7747–7750). The big companies have budgets to absorb psychographic mistakes; the small business does not. So the discipline of demographics + psychographics + Central Demographic Model is not a luxury of scale — it is a survival requirement at small scale.

## The Primary Aim of every business = customers coming back

Every extraordinary business — McDonald's, Federal Express, Disney, Walmart — knows one arithmetic fact, and Gerber plants it at the end of Ch. 17 as the verdict on the whole strategy.

> "it is getting them to come back for more that is the Primary Aim of every business. Because what McDonald's knows, and what Federal Express knows, and what Disney knows—indeed, what every extraordinary business knows—is that the customer you've got is one hell of a lot less expensive to sell to than the customer you don't have yet."
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7933–7939

> "And that's why the business process of Lead Generation, Lead Conversion, and Client Fulfillment is so critical to the growth of your business. And that's what marketing is. The whole process. Not just a part of it but the entire thing. And it never stops!"
> — Ch. 17, full-text.txt:7941–7946

The marketing KPI that matters is not leads. It is returning customers — because every other number is a more expensive way to buy the same revenue.
