# Management Strategy — Reference

Deep dive on Ch. 15 of *The E-Myth Revisited* ("Your Management Strategy"), with the Ch. 15/16 transition that introduces the Hierarchy of Systems and the "It" exercise. All quotes cite line numbers in `extraction-notes/full-text.txt`.

---

## The wrong answer: hire amazing managers

Gerber opens the chapter by pre-empting the reader's instinct — the belief that the management problem will be solved by recruiting elite, credentialed managers. He rejects this flatly, before offering any alternative.

> "You may think that the successful implementation of a management strategy is dependent on finding amazingly competent managers—people with finely honed 'people skills,' with degrees from management schools, with highly sophisticated techniques for dealing with and developing their people.
> It isn't.
> You don't need such people.
> Nor can you afford them.
> In fact, they will be the bane of your existence.
> What you need, instead, is a Management System."
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6345–6359

Later, when Sarah asks what is wrong with hiring experienced managers, Gerber answers at the level of standards rather than skills: hired managers will manage by *someone else's* standards, which is why professional-manager shopping is a structural dead end.

> "Because they will manage by the standards they have been taught to manage by in somebody else's business. Not by your standards."
> — Ch. 15/16 transition, full-text.txt:7289–7290

## The System *is* the Management Strategy

The central thesis is a reversal: the System is not a thing managers *use* — the System *is* the strategy, and managers exist to operate it. This removes most management decisions from the human layer and pushes them into documented, orchestrated routines.

> "The System will become your management strategy, the means through which your Franchise Prototype produces the results you want."
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6361–6362

> "The System will transform your people problems into an opportunity by orchestrating the process by which management decisions are made while eliminating the need for such decisions wherever and whenever possible."
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6367–6370

Gerber compresses the division of labor into a single line at the Ch. 15/16 boundary:

> "The System produces the results; your people manage the system."
> — Ch. 15/16 transition, full-text.txt:7335

## Management Development as a marketing tool

The most counter-intuitive claim in the chapter is that Management Development is misclassified when treated as a management concern. Its purpose is a Prototype that keeps customers — i.e., a marketing outcome — so Management Development belongs under marketing.

> "What Is a Management System? It is a System designed into your Prototype to produce a marketing result. And the more automatic that System is, the more effective your Franchise Prototype will be."
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6371–6377

> "Management Development—the process through which you create your Management System, and teach your up-and-coming managers to use it—isn't a management tool as many people believe. It's a marketing tool.
> Its purpose is not just to create an efficient Prototype but an effective one.
> And an effective Prototype is a business that finds and keeps customers—profitably—better than any other."
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6379–6388

## Venetia case study (match / mint / coffee / newspaper)

Gerber illustrates the whole chapter with a single hotel — Venetia, a resort in a redwood grove overlooking the Pacific — that he has returned to for seventeen years. On his first visit, tired and without a reservation, he is in his room within three minutes of entering the lobby (full-text.txt:6435–6438). A fire is pre-laid on the grate with "an elegant oversized match lying diagonally across the hearth" (full-text.txt:6447–6448). At check-in the receptionist silently makes his dinner reservation (full-text.txt:6450–6451). At the restaurant he is seated ahead of people waiting (full-text.txt:6465–6467); he is asked which coffee and which newspaper he prefers — questions he forgets answering.

Back in the room post-dinner: the fire is lit, the quilt is turned down, a mint sits on each pillow, a brandy stands on the night table with a welcome card (full-text.txt:6480–6492). In the morning, his brand of coffee is perking on an automatic timer in the bathroom (full-text.txt:6501–6507); his newspaper — the *New York Times* — is on the doormat (full-text.txt:6517–6518).

On subsequent visits he is never asked his preferences again. The experience is identical each time.

> "And exactly the same scenario has occurred each and every time I've returned.
> But after that first time I was never asked my preferences again.
> I had become a part of the hotel's Management System.
> And never once has it let me down."
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6526–6533

The mechanism is what registers, not the amenities:

> "What exactly had the System provided? A match, a mint, a cup of coffee, and a newspaper! But it wasn't the match, the mint, the cup of coffee, or the newspaper that did it. It was that somebody had heard me. And they heard me every single time!"
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6538–6544

> "And it was totally automatic! Every single element was an orchestrated solution designed to produce a marketing result, an integrated component of the hotel's Management System."
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6568–6572

The 29-year-old Manager explains how it runs. Before the hotel, his only hospitality experience was two nights as a guest at a Holiday Inn in Fresno; he had been a short-order cook at a nearby restaurant (full-text.txt:6604–6611). His answer is a red binder with the initials **OM** printed on the spine — the Operations Manual — which he describes in exactly these terms:

> "'This is our Operations Manual. As you can see, it's nothing but a series of checklists.'"
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6622–6623

> "'This group of pages is yellow. Everything in the Manual is color coded. Yellow has to do with Room Setup. Blue, with Guest Support Services. For instance, when we light your fire at night, put the mints on your pillow, and so on.'"
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6626–6629

Each Room Support Person (RSP) receives eight packages of checklists in their mailbox daily — one package per room — and signs each checklist to confirm performance. Signing without doing the work is grounds for instant dismissal (full-text.txt:6631–6644). On the back of every checklist is a drawing of the room with the tasks numbered in order; the RSP checks off the drawing as they move through the room.

> "'With this drawing we can train new people almost instantly and have them producing a result identical to that of a person who's been with us for quite some time.'"
> — Ch. 15, full-text.txt:6654–6656

This is the chapter's proof-of-concept: a former short-order cook, five months in, delivering an elite hospitality experience identical to a veteran's.

## The Hierarchy of Systems

At the Ch. 15/16 transition Gerber gives the four tiers of the Hierarchy, in order:

> "And there is a Hierarchy of Systems in your business.
> This Hierarchy is composed of four distinct components:
> The first is, How We Do It Here.
> The second is, How We Recruit, Hire, and Train People to Do It Here.
> The Third is, How We Manage It Here.
> The Fourth is, How We Change It Here."
> — Ch. 15/16 transition, full-text.txt:7337–7347

The four tiers form a nested dependency. You cannot train people on "How We Do It Here" until "It" is documented. You cannot manage what you have not trained. You cannot change what you have not managed. The Operations Manual is the artifact of tier one.

## The "It" exercise

"It" is the stated purpose of the business — the single word (or short phrase) that names why the customer is there. Gerber gives Federal Express's "It" as a benchmark:

> "And the 'It' I'm referring to is the stated purpose of your business. At Federal Express it's 'When You Absolutely, Positively Have to Get It There Overnight!'"
> — full-text.txt:7349–7351

For Sarah's pie shop, Gerber names the "It" as **Caring**, then runs the exercise — turning "It" into an operational standard for every customer touchpoint:

> "'The "It" of your business, Sarah, is Caring.
> How do you express Caring when you answer the telephone?
> How do you express Caring when you take a pie out of the oven?
> How do you express Caring when you take the money from a customer?'"
> — full-text.txt:7353–7360

The answer to those questions *is* tier one of the Hierarchy — documented in the Operations Manual, taught, managed, and iterated on for the life of the business:

> "And your answer to those questions is How You Do It Here! It is the sum total of everything you've created, every distinct process for performing every little task at All About Pies. Every bit of which is documented in your Operations Manuals. Every bit of which is taught at your school. Every bit of which is managed to, and improved upon, and discussed among you and your people for as long as you're in business! That's what 'It' is. 'It' is your Best Way. 'It' is what you believe in. 'It' is why people buy from you, work for you, lend to you, trust you."
> — full-text.txt:7364–7371

The "It" exercise is how the owner converts a promise into a set of observable behaviors at phone, oven, and cash register — and, by extension, at every other touchpoint that exists in the business.

## Delegation vs. Abdication

The guardrail on Management Strategy. Once an owner has systems and managers, the temptation is to hand off ownership-level accountabilities along with daily tasks. Gerber draws the line sharply:

> "Remember Delegation rather than Abdication?
> You can't delegate your accountabilities, Sarah.
> Delegating your accountabilities is abdication."
> — Ch. 15/16 transition, full-text.txt:7292–7296

> "You, as the Shareholder, as the owner, as the COO, as the VP/Marketing, as the VP/Finance, whatever positions you take, must take full accountability for what's going on in your business.
> And to do so, you must lead the company in the direction you intend it to go.
> And that means you must set the standard."
> — Ch. 15/16 transition, full-text.txt:7298–7305

Concretely: the owner invents the rules of the game (the Management System), then hires people who want to play it — not people looking to import a different one.

> "You don't need professional managers to manage to those standards. All you need are people who wish to learn how to manage to them. People who are as personally committed to those standards as you are!
> In short, you need people who want to play your game, Sarah. Not people who believe they have a better one."
> — Ch. 15/16 transition, full-text.txt:7318–7323

Tasks are delegable; accountability is not. The System carries the load that hired judgement cannot be trusted with.

## Operations Manual starter structure

Do not invent novel structures. The book's only worked example is Venetia. Its Operations Manual has these concrete, quotable properties:

- A binder with the initials **OM** and the company logo printed on the spine (full-text.txt:6615–6616).
- Contents: "nothing but a series of checklists" (full-text.txt:6622–6623).
- Color-coded by function — at Venetia, **yellow** for Room Setup and **blue** for Guest Support Services (full-text.txt:6626–6629).
- Each checklist is scoped to one unit of work (e.g., one room per RSP, eight rooms per day) and lives in the operator's mailbox at the start of shift (full-text.txt:6631–6635).
- Each checklist has a signature line at the bottom; signing without doing the work is grounds for instant dismissal (full-text.txt:6639–6644).
- Each checklist has a diagram on the back showing the unit of work, with tasks numbered in the order they must be performed; the operator checks off the drawing as they move through it (full-text.txt:6648–6652).
- The drawing is the training artifact — it is what lets a novice produce "a result identical to that of a person who's been with us for quite some time" (full-text.txt:6654–6656).
- Supervisors run daily spot checks as a backstop (full-text.txt:6658–6659).

This is the entire starter structure: binder on spine, color-coded checklists, signature accountability, reverse-side diagrams numbered in sequence, spot-check supervision. A team designing its first Operations Manual should reproduce this shape before deviating from it.
