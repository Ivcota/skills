# People Strategy — Reference

Deep-dive on Chapter 16 of *The E-Myth Revisited* by Michael E. Gerber. Every claim below is quote-backed to `extraction-notes/full-text.txt` or the extraction notes that cite it.

---

## "You can't get your people to do anything"

Gerber opens Chapter 16 by reframing the most common question small business owners ask. The question itself — *"How do I get my people to do what I want?"* — is built on a wrong premise. The owner cannot get people to do anything. The only leverage available to the owner is to build an environment in which doing the work well becomes more important to the employee than not doing it.

> "How do I get my people to do what I want?" This is the one question I hear most often from small business owners. And the answer I invariably give them is, "You can't! You can't get your people to do anything. If you want it done, you're going to have to create an environment in which 'doing it' is more important to your people than not doing it. Where 'doing it' well becomes a way of life for them." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6696–6704)

The pivot is from coercion to environment. You don't manage behavior; you manufacture the conditions that make the behavior inevitable. That is the entire subject of the People Strategy.

## Work as a mirror

The hotel owner's central teaching — relayed through the young Manager — is that work is not outside the worker. How a person does their work reveals who they are. This reframes the sloppy employee from a performance problem into an ontological one, and it reframes the employer's job as giving people the opportunity to make work meaningful *before* they do it.

> "The work we do is a reflection of who we are. If we're sloppy at it, it's because we're sloppy inside. If we're late at it, it's because we're late inside. If we're bored by it, it's because we're bored inside, with ourselves, not with the work. The most menial work can be a piece of art when done by an artist. So the job here is not outside of ourselves, but inside of ourselves. How we do our work becomes a mirror of how we are inside." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6783–6789)

Gerber continues through the Manager's voice: work is passive without a person; it is only an idea until done; once done, it becomes a reflection of the idea behind the work and of the person doing it (full-text.txt:6791–6801). The consequence — hollow, inconsistent, inhuman transactions between most businesses and their customers — is exactly the opposite of what the hotel achieves by letting people "make a choice … not after they've done the work, but before" (full-text.txt:6818–6820).

## The game as structure

People do not want to work for exciting bosses. They want to work inside a clearly defined structure that lets them test themselves. Gerber names this structure a game.

> "people—your people—do not simply want to work for exciting people. They want to work for people who have created a clearly defined structure for acting in the world. A structure through which they can test themselves and be tested. Such a structure is called a game. And there is nothing more exciting than a well-conceived game." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6859–6865)

> "That is what the very best businesses represent to the people who create them: a game to be played in which the rules symbolize the idea you, the owner, have about the world." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6867–6869)

The degree to which people "do what you want" is the degree to which they buy into your game — and that buy-in depends on how clearly and how early you communicate the game, not on the people themselves.

> "the degree to which your people 'do what you want' is the degree to which they buy into your game. And the degree to which they buy into your game doesn't depend on them but on how well you communicate the game to them—at the outset of your relationship, not after it's begun." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6875–6880)

## Two rules of the game

Gerber lists eight rules; the first two are the ones most often quoted because they establish the non-negotiable stance of the owner toward the game. (The remaining six — winning without ending, changing tactics, constant reminding, logical grounding, fun in moderation, stealing good games — are detailed in the appendix below.)

**Rule 1 — The game comes first; the work comes second.**

> "Never figure out what you want your people to do and then try to create a game out of it. If it's to be seen as serious, the game has to come first; what your people do, second." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6923–6926)

**Rule 2 — Never create a game you won't play yourself.**

> "Never create a game for your people you're unwilling to play yourself. They'll find you out and never let you forget it." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6927–6929)

These two rules enforce sincerity. The owner does not invent a mission statement for the team; the owner lives an idea and hires people into it.

---

## Tactics vs. strategy

Rule 4 draws the critical distinction between what is allowed to change and what must not. The tactics of the game — the specific activities, campaigns, weekly rituals — should change periodically to keep the game alive. The strategy must not.

> "Change the game from time to time—the tactics, not the strategy. The strategy is its ethic, the moral underpinning of your game's logic. This must remain sacrosanct, for it is the foundation of you and your people's commitment to each other. But change is necessary. For any game can become ordinary, no matter how exhilarating it may be at the beginning." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6937–6943)

The strategy is the ethic. That phrase is load-bearing: the game's moral underpinning is what people are actually committing to when they commit to the work.

---

## Ethical boundary

Because the strategy is the ethic, the game cannot be contrived. Gerber is explicit: a fake game gets found out, and its words go hollow.

> "The words will become hollow if the game is a contrived one. The game can't be created as a device to enroll your people. It can't become cynical if it's to provide your people with what they need in order to come alive while playing it. The game has to be real. You have to mean it. The game is a measure of you. How you act in the game establishes how you will be regarded by the other players." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:6901–6912)

This is why the People Strategy can't be patched on top of a business that doesn't have one. "Without an idea worth pursuing, there can be no People Strategy at all" (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:7163–7164). The People Strategy is downstream of the owner's Primary Aim; if the Aim is missing, or if the owner isn't willing to embody it, no amount of process will hide that from the people who show up to work.

## The hiring sequence

The hotel communicates its idea through a specific, documented hiring process. Gerber lists the components in order:

> "As the Manager explained it to me, the hiring process was comprised of several distinct components:
>
> 1. A scripted presentation communicating the Boss's idea in a group meeting to all of the applicants at the same time. This presentation described not only the idea but also the business's history and experience in successfully implementing that idea, and the attributes required of the successful candidate for the position in question.
> 2. Meeting with each applicant individually to discuss his reactions to and feelings about the idea, as well as his background and experience. At this meeting, each applicant was also asked why he felt he was superbly appropriate for the role the position was to play in implementing the Boss's idea.
> 3. Notification of the successful candidate by telephone. Again, a scripted presentation.
> 4. Notification of the unsuccessful applicants, thanking each for his interest. A standard letter, signed by the interviewer.
> 5. First day of training to include the following activities for both the Boss and the new employee:
>    - Reviewing the Boss's idea
>    - Summarizing the system through which the entire business brings the idea to reality
>    - Taking the new employee on a tour of the facilities, highlighting people at work and systems at work to demonstrate the interdependence of the systems on people and the people on systems
>    - Answering clearly and fully all the employee's questions
>    - Issuing the employee his uniform and his Operations Manual
>    - Reviewing the Operations Manual, including the Strategic Objective, the Organizational Strategy, and the Position Contract of the employee's position
>    - Completing the employment papers" (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:7111–7146)

Notice the order. The idea is presented *first*, to a group, in a script. Reactions to the idea — not résumé questions — drive the individual interview. The first day of training begins by reviewing the idea again, and only then moves to uniforms, manuals, and paperwork.

---

## Why hiring is the primary medium

The hiring process is not an administrative step. It is the first and most important medium through which the idea of the business gets communicated. The medium is itself the message.

> "In short, the medium of communication became as important as the idea it was designed to communicate. And the hotel's hiring process became the first and most essential medium for communicating the Boss's idea." (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:7105–7109)

The Boss could only communicate an "orderly yet human process of pleasing customers" by running a hiring process that was itself orderly and human (full-text.txt:7101–7103). If your onboarding is chaotic, your message — whatever the handbook says — is chaos.

## Applying to a small-business owner

For a small-business owner in Sarah's position (a single-shop operator who has never thought of herself as the author of a "game"), the implication is direct. Before writing a job ad, she must be able to state her idea — the ethic of her business — clearly enough to present it in a scripted group meeting. If she can't articulate why her shop exists beyond baking pies, the People Strategy has nothing to rest on. Gerber confronts this exactly: "without an idea worth pursuing, there can be no People Strategy at all" (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:7163–7164).

Once the idea is articulated, the owner's hiring process should be redesigned so that the first thing a candidate encounters is the idea, not the tasks. The individual interview asks how the candidate reacted to the idea and why they are right for the role it creates. First-day training begins by reviewing the idea and the system that brings the idea to reality, and ends — not begins — with paperwork and uniforms. Systematizing this is not dehumanizing; Gerber insists it is the opposite: "systematizing your business need not be a dehumanizing experience, but quite the opposite … hiring people, developing people, and keeping people requires a strategy built on an understanding of people completely foreign to most businesses" (Ch. 16, full-text.txt:7151–7159).
