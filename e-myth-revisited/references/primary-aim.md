# Primary Aim — Reference

## What it is

Primary Aim is the life vision that must precede the business. Gerber opens Ch. 12 by refusing to start with the business at all: "I doubt that by now you'd be surprised to find out that I don't believe your business to be the first order of business on our agenda. You are." (Ch. 12, full-text.txt:4603–4606). The business is not your life — "though it does and can play a significantly important role in your life" (4608–4610) — and the role it plays can only be fixed once you know what life you are trying to live. Your Primary Aim is the answer to that life question, articulated clearly enough that the business can be pointed at it.

The chapter epigraph from Robert Assagioli frames the whole move: "The chief characteristic of the volitional act is the existence of a purpose to be achieved; the clear vision of an aim." (full-text.txt:4595–4596). Without that clear vision, volition collapses into drift.

Once the Aim is written, "your Primary Aim is the vision necessary to bring your business to life and your life to your business" (Ch. 12, full-text.txt:4764–4765). Gerber lists what the Aim supplies:

- "It provides you with a purpose." (4767)
- "It provides you with energy." (4769)
- "It provides you with the grist for your day-to-day mill." (4771)

It is Section 1 of the Business Development Program (full-text.txt:4585–4591) precisely because it is the criterion against which every later strategy — Strategic Objective, Organization, Management, People, Marketing, Systems — is measured.

## The core questions

Four questions, verbatim from Ch. 12, full-text.txt:4612–4615:

- "What do I value most?"
- "What kind of life do I want?"
- "What do I want my life to look like, to feel like?"
- "Who do I wish to be?"

Gerber's elaboration in the same paragraph: "Your Primary Aim is the answer to all these questions." (4617).

He frames the whole exercise as the thing that must happen "before you can determine what [the business's] role will be" (4612). The questions are not an inventory of preferences — they are life-questions that must be answered before any business-question is worth asking.

## The funeral-script exercise

Gerber walks the reader into an imagined room sufficiently large to seat "all of your friends, your family, your business associates — anyone and everyone to whom you are important and who is important to you" (4624–4626). Deep golden tapestries on the walls; subdued lighting; chairs upholstered in golden fabric; a dais at the front with candles burning at either end; on the table, center stage, "a large, shining, ornate box. And in the box is…you! Stiff as the proverbial board." (4630–4640).

From the four corners of the room comes a tape recording of your voice: "You're addressing your guests. You're telling them the story of your life. How would you like that story to go?" (4646–4650).

The exact instruction is:

> "If you were to write a script for the tape to be played for the mourners at your funeral, how would you like it to read? That's your Primary Aim." (Ch. 12, full-text.txt:4659–4662)

He restates it twice for emphasis:

- "What would you like to be able to say about your life after it's too late to do anything about it? That's your Primary Aim." (4654–4657)
- "once you've created the script, all you need to do is make it come true" — by "living your life as if it were important," "tak[ing] your life seriously," "creat[ing] it intentionally," "actively mak[ing] your life into the life you wish it to be" (4664–4673).

"Simple? Yes. Easy? No." (4675–4677). But "absolutely essential if your business is to have any meaning beyond work." (4679–4680).

The sequence Gerber specifies is write-then-live: "once you've created the script, all you need to do is make it come true" (4664–4665). The eulogy comes first; everything upstream of it is adjusted until the script can plausibly come true.

Gerber's follow-up questions (4742–4757) sharpen the script into specifics:

- "What do I wish my life to look like?"
- "How do I wish my life to be on a day-to-day basis?"
- "What would I like to be able to say I truly know in my life, about my life?"
- "How would I like to be with other people in my life — my family, my friends, my business associates, my customers, my employees, my community?"
- "How would I like people to think about me?"
- "What would I like to be doing two years from now? Ten years from now? Twenty years from now? When my life comes to a close?"
- "What specifically would I like to learn during my life — spiritually, physically, financially, technically, intellectually? About relationships?"
- "How much money will I need to do the things I wish to do? By when will I need it?"

## Turning answers into standards

The questions are not journaling prompts. Their answers become measurement devices:

> "The answers become the standards against which you can begin to measure your life's progress. In the absence of such standards, your life will drift aimlessly, without purpose, without meaning." (Ch. 12, full-text.txt:4760–4762)

Gerber makes the stakes concrete immediately before that line with a cascade of rhetorical questions (4693–4703):

- "With no clear picture of how you wish your life to be, how on earth can you begin to live it?"
- "How would you know what first step to take?"
- "How would you measure your progress?"
- "How would you know where you were?"
- "How would you know how far you had gone?"
- "How would you know how much farther you had yet to go?"

The answer he gives himself is blunt: "Without your Primary Aim, you wouldn't. Indeed, you couldn't. It would be virtually impossible." (4705–4706).

This is the hinge into Strategic Objective. The Primary Aim's life-standards — especially "How much money will I need to do the things I wish to do? By when will I need it?" (4756–4757) — are what the Strategic Objective converts into business standards in Ch. 13. Great people "compare what they've done with what they intended to do. And where there's a disparity between the two, they don't wait very long to make up the difference." (4718–4720). The Aim is what gives them something to compare against in the first place.

## The Gerber "young man" parable

After Sarah shouts "That's what's been missing in my business… Me!" (4775–4776), Gerber tells a story about a young man who had "recently turned forty" whose life "seemed to be going nowhere" (4781–4783).

College had eluded him; music had attracted him brilliantly as a boy but failed to hold him as an adult (4785–4786). He drifted — Army, Korea, Europe on a motor scooter playing saxophone and drawing religious figures in front of cathedrals for meal money (4798–4801), selling encyclopedias in San Francisco (4807), framing houses in southern California where kids twenty years younger "regarded him on the job as something of a freak" (4829–4830).

Then, via a brother-in-law, he is "suddenly set adrift" in Silicon Valley, "calling on techies who owned businesses whose names he couldn't even pronounce at first attempt" (4899–4903). The exchange Gerber records is bare:

- They ask him, "How can you help me?" He answers, "I don't know."
- They ask, "What do you know about my business?" He answers, "Nothing." (4905–4907)

But he keeps coming back "because he knew something was there" (4910).

The turn: "intuitively, he knew that he knew something that they needed… something only someone who had lived in such an unconscious, cataclysmic, totally disorganized, yet always passionate state, doing this and that, could fully appreciate." (4941–4946). "It was then that the curtain lifted… the curtain that separated him from his life." (4948–4951). He realized "that, while he didn't understand their business, neither did they!" (4953–4955). From that reborn moment came "for the very first time in his life the beginning of some purpose" (4958–4959).

Gerber closes the parable by lifting his own mask: "I know, Sarah, because the man I've been telling you about is me." (full-text.txt:5024).

The moral he draws is deliberately larger than business strategy: "It's not your business you have to fear losing. It's something much bigger than that. It's your Self." (5007–5008). "Until you lift the curtain, Sarah, until you dare to pull the mask off the world's face, until you move beyond your Comfort Zone, you will never know what it is you were missing out there." (5017–5019).

The parable is Gerber's own origin-of-Aim — a life created passively for forty years, then created actively — and the thing waiting on the other side of the curtain is not a better business but the owner's own Self.

## Ethical boundary

The guardrail comes directly from the chapter's opening: you are the first order of business, not the business (Ch. 12, full-text.txt:4603–4606; reinforced in 06-ethical-boundaries.md).

The specific failure mode Gerber names is letting the business serve an Aim you never articulated:

> "if your business is going to become a significant component of your Primary Aim, you have to let your business know what that Aim is! And how can you expect to do that, if you don't know what it is?" (Ch. 12, full-text.txt:4682–4688)

Treating the business as the first-order question, ahead of the life it is supposed to serve, is the lie this framework is built to prevent. The business is "a means rather than an end, a vehicle to enrich your life rather than one that drains the life you have" (Ch. 13, full-text.txt:5053–5054, quoted in 03-copy-patterns.md).

"Great people create their lives actively, while everyone else is created by their lives, passively waiting to see where life takes them next." (4724–4727). The Aim is what separates the two stances — "the difference between living fully and just existing… the difference between living intentionally and living by accident." (4728–4732).

Gerber reinforces this with the Castaneda quote he pulls into the chapter: "The difference between a warrior and an ordinary man is that a warrior sees everything as a challenge, while an ordinary man sees everything as either a blessing or a curse." (4734–4737). The warrior stance — seeing the business as a challenge to be directed at an articulated Aim — is the ethic Primary Aim installs.

## Sarah-style worked example

Sarah is a Technician owner — the pie-shop baker who loved making pies until making pies became the business. When Gerber walks her through the funeral-script exercise, the question that lands is "How would I like to be with other people in my life — my family, my friends, my business associates, my customers, my employees, my community?" (4746–4748), because her honest answer today is: exhausted, resentful, alone in the shop at 5 a.m.

Her realization is the one the text records verbatim: "That's what's been missing in my business… Me! How could I have been so oblivious to something so obvious?" (4775–4776). The eulogy she would want played does not say "she baked excellent pies" — it says something about caring, presence, and a life she actively created.

That eulogy, written down, becomes the standard (4760–4762) her Strategic Objective in Ch. 13 must be built to serve. Every decision about hours, hiring, and systems is now measurable against it.

Until she wrote it, the pie shop had no way to "know" what Aim it was supposed to serve (4682–4688) — so it served none, and consumed her instead. Gerber's comfort to her is not sympathy but recognition: "We are all oblivious. Join the club." (4778). The Aim is what ends the obliviousness.
