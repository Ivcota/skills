# Time Blocking (Chapter 15)

All citations: Keller & Papasan, 2013.

## 1. The principle

> "If disproportionate results come from one activity, then you must give that one activity disproportionate time." (p. 137)

Time blocking = pre-committed, protected calendar blocks for your ONE Thing. "Time blocking harnesses your energy and centers it on your most important work. It's productivity's greatest power tool" (p. 136).

"The most successful people are the most productive people" (p. 134). Productive people live on **event time** ("my work is done when it's done"), not clock time — "'Your most important work is done when it's done'" (p. 141, drawing on Robert Levine's *A Geography of Time*).

## 2. The block order — in this exact sequence (p. 139)

> "To achieve extraordinary results and experience greatness, time block these three things in the following order:
> 1. Time block your time off.
> 2. Time block your ONE Thing.
> 3. Time block your planning time."

### 1st — Time off (p. 140)
Plan vacations at the **start of the year**, in advance, as if they're appointments with your future self. "Resting is as important as working" (p. 140). Reason: "the least successful don't reserve time off, because they don't think they'll deserve it" (p. 140) — the math is inverted. Time off recharges the energy that makes the ONE Thing block productive.

### 2nd — The ONE Thing (pp. 140–142)
- Your most important appointment each day is with yourself (p. 140).
- **Block as early as possible.** "Give yourself 30 minutes to an hour to take care of morning priorities, then move to your ONE Thing" (p. 141).
- **Duration: four hours, minimum.**
  > "My recommendation is to block four hours a day. This isn't a typo. I repeat: four hours a day. Honestly, that's the minimum. If you can do more, then do it." (p. 141)
- Example calendar (Fig. 29, p. 139) shows ONE Thing block running **8 A.M. – Noon**.
- **Why four hours?** Chapter 16 does the math (p. 151): Ericsson's 10,000-hour rule ÷ 250 workdays/year ÷ 10 years = **4 hours/day**.

### 3rd — Planning time (pp. 143)
- **Weekly**: one hour to review annual and monthly goals, to make sure you're on track or course-correct.
- **Annual**: schedule late enough in the year to see trajectory, but early enough to keep a running start for next year.

## 3. Protecting the block — four tactics (pp. 147–148)

1. **Build a bunker.** Find a place out of the path of disruption. Do Not Disturb sign. Shades drawn. Vacant conference room if needed.
2. **Store provisions.** Snacks, drinks, supplies in the bunker. You should not need to leave.
3. **Sweep for mines.** Phone off. Email closed. Browser quit. Slack muted.
4. **Enlist support.** Tell the people most likely to seek you out what you're doing and when you'll be available.

### "If you erase, you must replace" (p. 147)
If an emergency forces you to drop the block, **immediately** reschedule it — ideally the same day, otherwise the next. "The minute you give up the ground you've gained it's hard to get it back."

### Don't break the chain (p. 144)
Brad Isaac / Jerry Seinfeld, July 2007. Big wall calendar. Mark a big red X every day you do your ONE Thing. "After a few days you'll have a chain. Just keep at it and the chain will grow longer every day... Your only job next is to not break the chain."

## 4. Maker's schedule, manager's schedule (pp. 142–143)

Paul Graham's 2009 essay. Paul Graham:
> "When you're operating on the maker's schedule, meetings are a disaster. A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon by breaking it into two pieces each too small to do anything hard in."

"Be a maker in the morning and a manager in the afternoon" (p. 143). At Y Combinator, all meetings are clustered at the end of the day so mornings remain maker time.

## 5. The daily shape (synthesis across Chs 15, 16, 17)

```
Morning (early)    Spiritual energy — meditation / prayer
                   Physical energy — breakfast, exercise
~8:00 A.M.         START OF ONE THING BLOCK
8:00 – Noon        ONE Thing (4-hour maker block, protected)
Noon               Break / lunch
Afternoon          "Everything else" — meetings, manager work, email
Evening            Family / friends / downtime
Night              8 hours sleep
```
(p. 170, Ch 17 restating Ch 15)

Also (p. 177): **10,000 steps** by end of day; if you haven't walked them, make hitting that step count your ONE exercise thing.

## 6. Case studies

- **Stephen King**, *On Writing* (p. 141). Mornings for new composition; afternoons for naps and letters; evenings for reading and family. Keller: *"Does he get to do this because he is Stephen King, or is he Stephen King because he does this?"*
- **Ernest Hemingway** — strict schedule, 7 A.M. start, in his bedroom (p. 148).
- **Dan Heath** — bought an old laptop, deleted browsers, went to a coffee shop to avoid distractions while writing (p. 148).
- **Paul Graham / Y Combinator** — 2009 *Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule* essay; YC adopted maker's schedule wholesale, clustering meetings at day's end (pp. 142–143).
- **Jerry Seinfeld / Brad Isaac** — the red-X calendar chain (p. 144).
- **Keller's own executive assistant** — blocked large chunks of time on his calendar; team took ~one week to adjust; result: "a huge leap in productivity" (p. 142).
- **Alexander Graham Bell** — "Concentrate all your thoughts upon the work at hand. The sun's rays do not burn until brought to a focus." (p. 135)

- "Don't break the chain." (p. 144)
- "If your time blocking were on trial, would your calendar contain enough evidence to convict you?" (p. 149)

- "Resting is as important as working." (p. 140)

## 8. Anti-patterns

- **Scheduling ONE Thing after lunch.** Willpower is lowest in the afternoon; default behavior takes over (Ch 7, p. 63).
- **Scheduling 30-minute "focus blocks."** Four hours is the minimum, not the aspiration. Sub-hour blocks don't reach deep work.
- **Leaving the block unprotected.** "Drive-bys" from colleagues will shred it (p. 142).
- **Double-booking.** Treat the block as immovable. The calendar is the contract (p. 146).
- **Working more hours instead of producing more in the hours you work** (p. 150). The 11-year British civil servant study: >11 hours/day = 67% more likely heart disease (p. 71).
- **Not time-blocking time off** — it's listed *first* for a reason (p. 140).
- **"Getting everything else done"** — "may help you sleep better at night, but it's unlikely to earn you a promotion" (p. 139).

## 9. Implementation checklist

- [ ] Block next 12 months of vacation on the calendar — today.
- [ ] Block 4 hours every morning, 8 A.M.–Noon, for the ONE Thing.
- [ ] Block 1 hour/week for planning (Sunday evening or Monday morning).
- [ ] Name the physical "bunker" location for the block.
- [ ] Tell the 3 most-likely-to-interrupt-me people the schedule.
- [ ] Sweep-for-mines checklist at block start: phone off, email closed, browser quit, Slack muted.
- [ ] Wall calendar for the Seinfeld red-X chain.
- [ ] Sign for the desk: "Until my ONE Thing is done — everything else is a distraction."
- [ ] 10,000-step target for non-block hours.
- [ ] 8 hours of sleep scheduled.

## 10. Economic framing

The 4-hour ONE Thing block isn't new time — it's reallocated time. The question is which 4 hours are you giving up. The book's answer (p. 139): "Getting 'everything else' done may help you sleep better at night, but it's unlikely to earn you a promotion." Trade the everything-else hours for the ONE Thing hours. Your manager's-schedule afternoon has room for the reactive work; your maker's-schedule morning does not.
