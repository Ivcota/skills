# The ONE Thing + The Domino Effect (Chapters 1–3)

All citations to Keller & Papasan, *The ONE Thing*, Bard Press 2013. Page numbers are PDF page numbers from the source file in `sources.md`.

## 1. The book's thesis, stated four ways

- "The ONE Thing is the best approach to getting what you want." (p. 8)
- "Extraordinary results are directly determined by how narrow you can make your focus." (p. 10)
- "Success is built sequentially. It's one thing at a time." (p. 15)
- "The ONE Thing sits at the heart of success and is the starting point for achieving extraordinary results." (p. 22)

The Ross Garber rule — the test: *"There can only be one most important thing. Many things may be important, but only one can be the most important."* (p. 18)

## 2. The origin story (Ch 1)

### The *City Slickers* scene (p. 7)
On June 7, 1991, Keller watched the film. Curly (Jack Palance) to Mitch (Billy Crystal):
> Curly: Do you know what the secret of life is?
> Mitch: No. What?
> Curly: This. [Holds up one finger.]
> Mitch: Your finger?
> Curly: One thing. Just one thing. You stick to that and everything else don't mean sh*t.
> Mitch: That's great, but what's the "one thing"?
> Curly: That's what you've got to figure out.

### Keller's coach and the 14 hires (pp. 8–9)
Keller's company was failing. Coach researched thoroughly, came back with his organizational chart on the wall and said only one thing needed to change — 14 positions needed new faces. Coach's line: *"No. Jesus needed 12, but you'll need 14."*

Keller fired himself as CEO to focus solely on recruiting those 14. Result: ~**40% year-over-year growth for almost a decade**, regional → international (p. 9).

### The shrinking coaching list (p. 9)
Keller's habit was to close coaching calls with a recap of things the coachee would do. Most got most of them done — but not the things that mattered most. Out of desperation, he kept shortening the list: "If you can do just three things this week..." → two → finally "out of desperation, I went as small as I could possibly go" and asked:

> *"What's the ONE Thing you can do this week such that by doing it everything else would be easier or unnecessary?"*

"Results went through the roof" (p. 9). The pattern: *"Where I'd had huge success, I had narrowed my concentration to one thing, and where my success varied, my focus had too."*

## 3. Go small (p. 10)

> "Going small is ignoring all the things you could do and doing what you should do. It's recognizing that not all things matter equally and finding the things that matter most."

> "You have only so much time and energy, so when you spread yourself out, you end up spread thin. You want your achievements to add up, but that actually takes subtraction, not addition."

The cost of spreading thin (p. 10): missed deadlines, disappointing results, high stress, long hours, lost sleep, poor diet, no exercise, missed moments with family/friends.

## 4. The Domino Effect (Ch 2)

### The world record (p. 12)
**Weijers Domino Productions, Leeuwarden, Netherlands, Domino Day — November 13, 2009.** 4,491,863 dominoes. A single flick released ~**94,000 joules** of energy (equivalent to an average-sized male doing 545 pushups).

### The 50%-larger physics (p. 12)
**Lorne Whitehead, *American Journal of Physics*, 1983** — a single domino can topple another **50 percent larger** than itself.

**San Francisco Exploratorium, 2001** reproduction (p. 13): 8 dominoes, each 50% larger than the prior; first 2 inches tall, eighth almost 3 feet. "Began with a gentle tick... ended with a loud SLAM."

### Geometric projection (p. 13)
- 10th domino ≈ height of Peyton Manning
- 18th ≈ Leaning Tower of Pisa
- 23rd > Eiffel Tower
- 31st ≈ 3,000 feet above Mt. Everest
- **57th ≈ earth-to-moon bridge**

### What the domino effect is about (pp. 14–15)
- "Getting extraordinary results is all about creating a domino effect in your life."
- "Extraordinary success is sequential, not simultaneous. What starts out linear becomes geometric."
- "Find the lead domino, and whack away at it until it falls."
- "Success is built sequentially. It's one thing at a time."

The domino effect applies to both the big picture (company/career) and the smallest in-day decision ("what to do next?") (p. 15).

## 5. Success Leaves Clues — four lenses (Ch 3)

Keller argues proof of the ONE Thing is visible through four different lenses. "Proof of the ONE Thing is everywhere. Look closely and you'll always find it" (p. 16).

### Lens 1 — ONE Product / ONE Service (pp. 16–17)
- **Colonel Sanders (KFC)** — one secret chicken recipe.
- **Adolph Coors Company** — 1,500% growth 1947–1967, one product, one brewery.
- **Intel** — microprocessors = vast majority of net revenue.
- **Google** — ONE Thing is search; enables ad revenue.
- **Star Wars** — the *movies* are the ONE Thing; they make $10B+ toys possible ($4.3B box office for six main films).
- **Apple** — evolution 1998–2012: Mac → iMac → iTunes → iPod → iPhone → iPad, each a "halo" for the others.

> "Extraordinarily successful companies always have one product or service they're most known for or that makes them the most money."

And the corollary (p. 18): "If today your company doesn't know what its ONE Thing is, then the company's ONE Thing is to find out."

### Lens 2 — ONE Person (pp. 17–19)
- **Walt Disney's brother Roy** — banker who got young Walt work at an art studio (animation origin).
- **Sam Walton + L. S. Robson** (father-in-law) — loaned $20,000 for first franchise; later secretly paid a landlord $20,000 to secure a critical Wal-Mart expansion lease.
- **Albert Einstein + Max Talmud** — introduced 10-year-old Einstein to math, science, philosophy over six years of weekly meals.
- **Oprah Winfrey + her father** (pivot) + **Jeffrey D. Jacobs** (lawyer/agent who pushed her to form Harpo).
- **The Beatles + George Martin** — "Fifth Beatle" producer; orchestral and keyboard arrangements.

> "No one is self-made. No one succeeds alone. No one." (p. 19)

### Lens 3 — ONE Passion + ONE Skill (pp. 19–21)
Virtuous cycle (p. 20): "Passion → disproportionate time → skill → results → enjoyment → more passion → more time."

- **Pat Matthews** — impressionist painter; turned passion into profession by painting one painting a day.
- **Angelo Amorico** — Italy's most successful tour guide; built from one passion for his country.
- **Gilbert Tuhabonye** — Burundian runner; survived 1993 Hutu massacre (9 hours buried under burning bodies, lone survivor); All-American × 6 at Abilene Christian; Austin coach; cofounder of Gazelle Foundation's "Run for the Water."

Authority quotes the book anchors to (p. 20–21):
- **Patton**: "You must be single-minded. Drive for the one thing on which you have decided."
- **Vince Lombardi**: "Success demands singleness of purpose."

### Lens 4 — ONE Life (pp. 21–22)
**Bill Gates** — one passion (computers) → one skill (programming) → one person (Paul Allen) → one letter (to Ed Roberts) → one computer (**Altair 8800**) → Microsoft (BASIC interpreter). Richest man in the world 15 straight years. **Steve Ballmer** was employee #30 and the first business manager Gates hired.

**Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** — one foundation → one focus (Global Health Program) → one target (infectious disease) → one tool (vaccines).
Bill: *"The magic tool of health intervention is vaccines, because they can be made inexpensively."* (p. 23)
Melinda's question: *"Where's the place you can have the biggest impact with the money?"*

## 6. Anti-patterns identified in the intro

- Spreading thin — "when you spread yourself out, you end up spread thin" (p. 10)
- Prioritizing everything — Ross Garber's rebuttal (p. 18)
- Treating success as simultaneous rather than sequential (p. 14)
- Adding without subtracting (p. 10)
- Lowering expectations over time, letting life get small (p. 10)
- Believing you're self-made / succeeding alone (p. 19)
- Company not knowing its ONE Thing (p. 18)

## 7. How to apply the intro in practice

1. For a personal decision: ask the Focusing Question at the smallest scale — "What's the ONE Thing I can do right now such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"
2. For a company: ask *"What's our ONE Thing?"* and, if there isn't one, *"What's the ONE Thing we can do to find out?"* (p. 18)
3. For a life: walk the four lenses — ONE product / ONE person / ONE passion+skill / ONE life — and see which is missing or unclear.
4. Scan for lead dominoes — the single act that would make downstream acts easier or unnecessary.
