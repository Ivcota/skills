# The Focusing Question (Chapters 10–12)

All citations: Keller & Papasan, 2013. This is the book's pivot tool — the single sentence that operationalizes every other framework.

## 1. The exact wording (p. 9, 93)

> **"What's the ONE Thing I can do such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"**

Scaled to a timeframe or domain, the frame becomes:

> *"What's the ONE Thing I can do [this week / right now / in my career / for my marriage] such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"*

## 2. Anatomy of the question (pp. 93–95)

The authors split it into three parts.

### Part 1 — "What's the ONE Thing I can do..."
- *One* thing — not two, not three. "You can't hedge your bet" (p. 93).
- *Can do* — "an embedded command directing you to take action that is possible. People often want to change this to 'should do,' 'could do,' or 'would do,' but those choices all miss the point... Action you 'can do' beats intention every time."

### Part 2 — "...such that by doing it..."
- "This is the bridge between just doing something and doing something for a specific purpose" (p. 94).
- "When you do this ONE Thing, something else is going to happen."

### Part 3 — "...everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"
- **The ultimate leverage test** (p. 94).
- Archimedes: "Give me a lever long enough and I could move the world."
- "It tells you when you've found the first domino... this qualifier seeks to declutter your life by asking you to put on blinders" (p. 94).

## 3. Big-picture vs. small-focus (p. 96, Fig. 15)

| Mode | Form | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| **Big-Picture** | "What's my ONE Thing?" | Strategic compass — vision for life, career, company |
| **Small-Focus** | "What's my ONE Thing *right now*?" | Daily compass — first thing in the morning; throughout the day for levered next moves |

> "It's both a map for the big picture and a compass for your smallest next move." (p. 93)

## 4. The path to Great Answers (Ch 12, pp. 106–113)

Two steps (Fig. 17, p. 106):
1. **Ask a Great Question** — *Big & Specific*.
2. **Find a Great Answer** — research + role model.

### The Great Question matrix (Fig. 18, p. 107)

Using "doubling sales" as the worked example:

| Quadrant | Question | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Small & Specific | "What can I do to increase sales by 5% this year?" | Incremental; rarely extraordinary (p. 109) |
| Small & Broad | "What can I do to increase sales?" | Brainstorming, not a goal (p. 109) |
| Big & Broad | "What can I do to double sales?" | Good start; lacks specifics (p. 109) |
| **Big & Specific** | "What can I do to **double sales in six months**?" | Target. Demands a big answer, pushes past standard tools. (p. 109) |

Then convert the Great Question into Focusing Question form (p. 110):
> "What's the ONE Thing I can do to double sales in six months such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"

### Three types of answers (p. 110–111)

- **Doable** — within your current knowledge/skills. Likely achieved.
- **Stretch** — at the far edge of your current abilities; requires research. Probable.
- **Possibility** — beyond what's already known or done. Requires new behavior.

> "High achievers... reject [doable and stretch]. Unwilling to settle for ordinary when extraordinary is possible, they've asked a Great Question and want the very best answer." (pp. 110–111)

### Benchmark + trend (pp. 111–112)

1. **Research first.** "Anytime you don't know the answer, your answer is to go find your answer." Books and the internet; find who's studied or accomplished this.
2. **Benchmark** = the current high-water mark. With a stretch approach it was your maximum; now it's your minimum (p. 112).
3. **Trend** = "the hilltop where you'll stand to see if you can spot what might come next... the next thing you can do in the same direction that the best performers are heading, or an entirely new direction" (p. 112).

> "The benchmark is today's success — the trend is tomorrow's." (Fig. 21, p. 112)
> "A new answer usually requires new behavior." (p. 112)

## 5. Making the Focusing Question a habit (Ch 11, pp. 97–105)

### The 5-step habit installation

1. **Understand and believe it.** "If you don't understand and believe, you won't take action" (p. 104).
2. **Use it.** Start each morning by asking: "What's the ONE Thing I can do today for [whatever you want] such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"
3. **Make it a habit.** "Research says this will take about 66 days" (p. 104).
4. **Leverage reminders.** "Put up a sign at work that says, 'Until my ONE Thing is done — everything else is a distraction.'" Keller designed the back cover as a trigger to place on your desk. Use notes, screen savers, calendar cues (p. 105).
5. **Recruit support.** Success support group. Get family on board. "Research shows that those around you can influence you tremendously" (p. 105).

### The cascade of life areas (p. 99–100, Fig. 16)

Apply the Focusing Question in this order (each foundation for the next):
1. Spiritual life
2. Physical health
3. Personal life
4. Key relationships
5. Job
6. Business
7. Financial life

Reframe template (p. 101): *"[Category] — what's the ONE Thing I can do [timeframe] to [goal] such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"*

Examples from the book (pp. 101–103):
- **Spiritual**: "For my spiritual life, what's the ONE Thing I can do to help others such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"
- **Physical health**: "...what's the ONE Thing I can do to achieve my diet goals..."
- **Key relationships**: "...the ONE Thing I can do to improve my marriage..."
- **Job**: "...the ONE Thing I can do to ensure I hit my goals this week..."
- **Business**: "...the ONE Thing I can do to make us more competitive..."
- **Financial**: "...the ONE Thing I can do to increase my net worth..."

## 10. Misuse — what not to do

- **Don't swap "can do" for "should / could / would do"** — those are intentions, not commitments (p. 93).
- **Don't hedge** — "You're allowed to pick one thing and one thing only" (p. 93).
- **Don't drop "such that by doing it"** — the criterion is what makes it a leverage test, not a brainstorm (p. 94).
- **Don't ask it once and move on** — "ask it again and again" (p. 93).
- **Don't over-apply it.** "You can drive yourself nuts analyzing every little aspect of everything you might do. I don't do that, and you shouldn't either" (p. 98).
- **Don't ask Small & Broad questions** — they produce average answers (p. 109).
- **Don't stop at research** — research is the starting line, not the finish (p. 111).
- **Don't settle for doable when possibility is available** (p. 110).
