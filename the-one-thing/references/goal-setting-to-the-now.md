# Goal Setting to the Now (Chapter 14)

All citations: Keller & Papasan, 2013.

## 1. The problem this solves

"Purpose without priority is powerless" (p. 126). A distant purpose (someday goal, life purpose, Big Why) does not by itself tell you what to do right now. Humans are biased toward present rewards — **hyperbolic discounting** — "the further away a reward is in the future, the smaller the immediate motivation to achieve it" (p. 127).

Single-question shortcuts also fail: asking "What's the ONE Thing I can do right now so I'm on track to achieve my someday goal?" "Doesn't work. The moment is too far from the future for you to clearly see your key priority" (p. 130).

## 2. The cascade — 7 levels (p. 128, Fig. 24)

```
1. SOMEDAY GOAL   → What's the ONE Thing I want to do SOMEDAY?
2. FIVE-YEAR GOAL → Based on my Someday Goal, what's the ONE Thing I can do
                    in the NEXT FIVE YEARS?
3. ONE-YEAR GOAL  → Based on my Five-Year Goal, what's the ONE Thing I can do
                    THIS YEAR?
4. MONTHLY GOAL   → Based on my One-Year Goal, what's the ONE Thing I can do
                    THIS MONTH?
5. WEEKLY GOAL    → Based on my Monthly Goal, what's the ONE Thing I can do
                    THIS WEEK?
6. DAILY GOAL     → Based on my Weekly Goal, what's the ONE Thing I can do
                    TODAY?
7. RIGHT NOW      → Based on my Daily Goal, what's the ONE Thing I can do
                    RIGHT NOW?
```

> "By thinking through the filter of Goal Setting to the Now, you set a future goal and then methodically drill down to what you should be doing right now. It can be a little like a Russian matryoshka doll in that your ONE Thing 'right now' is nested inside your ONE Thing today which is nested inside your ONE Thing this week..." (p. 127)

> "You're lining up your dominoes." (Fig. 25, p. 129)

## 3. Why the full cascade matters

Research shows **written goals beat unwritten** — and written + shared beat written alone.

- **Dr. Gail Matthews, Dominican University of California, 2008.** Those who wrote their goals were **39.5% more likely** to accomplish them (p. 132).
- Adding weekly progress reports sent to a friend raised completion to **76.7%** (p. 163).

Visualization works too — but of **process**, not outcome. A study of 262 students (three separate studies) showed those who visualized the *steps* they'd take — not the *reward at the end* — "performed better across the board" (p. 131).

And the cascade defeats the **planning fallacy** — the well-documented bias that people are systematically overoptimistic about what they can accomplish in a given time (p. 131). Breaking a big goal into nested ONE Things forces a realistic next step.

## 4. The etymology argument (p. 125)

The word *priority* entered English in the 14th century from Latin *prior* meaning "first" or "original." It remained **singular** until the 20th century, when we began pluralizing it ("priorities") — destroying the meaning. Keller: reclaim the singular. "There can only be ONE" (p. 132).

## 5. Present bias — the hyperbolic discounting curve (p. 127)

The classic experiment: asked to choose between $100 today vs. $200 next year, most people take the $100. Now. That's present bias. The cascade converts that bias into leverage: your *right now* answer is connected through six layers to the future payoff, so present-you and future-you share the same domino.

## 6. How to run the cascade — practical procedure

1. **Block 60–90 minutes** in a quiet place. Calendar, pen, paper.
2. **Write your Someday Goal.** Bold, ambitious, meaningful. "Live with purpose" material (Ch 13).
3. **Ask each of the seven questions in order, out loud** (p. 129). Write each answer. Don't skip levels.
4. **Each question inherits its parent.** The Year answer must point at the Five-Year. The Month answer must point at the Year. Break any level that fails this check.
5. **Stop at Right Now.** That's your current ONE Thing.
6. **Share weekly progress** with a coach or peer. The 76.7% number is real.
7. **Re-run quarterly** for the Year → Right Now levels. Re-run annually for Someday → Year.

## 7. Alice and the Cheshire Cat (p. 125)

Lewis Carroll, *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland*:
> Alice: "Would you tell me, please, which way I ought to go from here?"
> Cheshire Cat: "That depends a good deal on where you want to get to."
> Alice: "I don't much care where—"
> Cat: "Then it doesn't matter which way you go."

Keller's point: without a destination (Someday Goal), every direction is equivalent. The cascade exists to put the destination at the head of every daily choice.

## 8. Anti-patterns

- **Pluralizing "priorities"** (p. 125).
- **Skipping intermediate steps** — jumping from Someday directly to Right Now (p. 130).
- **Setting outcome visualization instead of process visualization** (p. 131).
- **Not writing it down** — 39.5% gap (p. 132).
- **Not sharing with a peer** — 76.7% vs. 39.5% gap (p. 163).
- **Present bias** — choosing what feels urgent over what's connected to Someday (p. 127).

## 10. Worked example — student

- **Someday**: Become a published novelist.
- **Five-Year**: Publish one novel.
- **One-Year**: Finish a first draft.
- **Monthly**: Complete chapters 1–3.
- **Weekly**: Finish chapter 1, 20 pages.
- **Daily**: Write 750 words.
- **Right now**: Sit down, open the document, write the next sentence.

## 11. Worked example — founder

- **Someday**: Build a company that serves 100M customers.
- **Five-Year**: Reach $50M ARR.
- **One-Year**: Reach $5M ARR.
- **Monthly**: Add $400K ARR net new.
- **Weekly**: Close 4 new enterprise deals.
- **Daily**: Have 3 first-meetings with qualified prospects.
- **Right now**: Send the follow-up to the prospect in my inbox.

Each level must make the next easier or unnecessary — the same leverage test the Focusing Question uses at a single scale.
