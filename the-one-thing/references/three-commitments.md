# The Three Commitments (Chapter 16)

All citations: Keller & Papasan, 2013.

## 1. The principle

Time blocking is necessary but not sufficient. Three commitments convert a protected calendar into extraordinary results (p. 150):

1. **Follow the Path of Mastery**
2. **Move from "E" to "P"**
3. **Live the Accountability Cycle**

> "Time blocking is essential to mastery, and mastery is essential to time blocking. They go hand in hand — when you do one, you do the other." (p. 154)

---

## 2. Commitment 1 — Follow the Path of Mastery (pp. 150–154)

**Core concept.** Mastery is a path, not a destination. "When you can see mastery as a path you go down instead of a destination you arrive at, it starts to feel accessible and attainable" (p. 150).

> "We become masters of what is behind us and apprentices for what is ahead." (p. 151)

**The math — where "4 hours a day" comes from.**
- **K. Anders Ericsson** (1993), "The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert Performance," *Psychological Review*. Source of the 10,000-hour rule. Elite violinists accumulated 10,000+ hours of practice by age 20 (pp. 151–152).
- 10,000 hours ÷ 10 years ÷ 250 workdays = **4 hours/day**.

**Authority case studies.**
- **Alex Van Halen on Eddie Van Halen**: "he'd stay in his room for hours practicing while I went out... that's why he became Eddie Van Halen" (p. 151).
- **Michelangelo**: "If the people knew how hard I had to work to gain my mastery, it wouldn't seem wonderful at all." (p. 152)
- **Jigoro Kano** (founder of judo) — asked to be buried in his **white belt**. "The journey of the successful lifelong learner was never over" (p. 152, via George Leonard's *Mastery*).

**Key insights.**
- "More than anything else, expertise tracks with hours invested" (p. 152).
- "The pursuit of mastery bears gifts" — knowledge begets knowledge, skills build on skills (p. 152).

## 3. Commitment 2 — Move from "E" to "P" (pp. 154–158)

This is the book's most distinctive model (Fig. 31, p. 155).

### "E" = Entrepreneurial ("Doing What Comes Naturally")

Your natural approach. Uses existing abilities. Hits a **Natural Ceiling of Achievement**, producing a downward cycle:

1. **Disappointment** (results stall)
2. **Resignation** ("this is as good as it gets")
3. **Greener Pastures** (abandon, start over elsewhere)
4. **The Cycle Continues** (next ceiling hits in the new field)

### "P" = Purposeful ("Doing What Comes Unnaturally")

Breaks the ceiling via a different loop:

1. **Focus** (narrow to ONE Thing)
2. **Models** (adopt what successful people use)
3. **Systems** (install repeatable processes)
4. **Breakthroughs**

> "Ask an 'E' to cut some firewood and the Entrepreneurial person would likely shoulder an axe and head straight for the woods... the Purposeful person might ask, 'Where can I get a chainsaw?'" (p. 155)

> "The path of mastering something is the combination of not only doing the best *you* can do at it, but also doing the best *it* can be done." (p. 154)

> "A different result requires doing something different." (p. 155)

### The OK Plateau

**Joshua Foer** (*Moonwalking with Einstein*) coined the term (p. 156). Typing speed is the everyday example: once you're "acceptable," you switch off deliberate practice and skill freezes forever. The OK Plateau is an E-path symptom. P-path mandates hunting for better models when yours plateau.

### In the long run, P beats E every time (p. 155, Fig. 31)

E hits its ceiling early and flattens. P breaks ceilings repeatedly via focus→models→systems→breakthrough.

## 4. Commitment 3 — Live the Accountability Cycle (pp. 158–164)

**Core concept.** "Accountability is most likely the most important of the three commitments" (p. 158). "You can be either the author of your life or the victim of it" (p. 159).

### The two cycles (Fig. 32, p. 160)

```
ACCOUNTABLE (upward)              VICTIM (downward)
Seeks Reality                     Avoids Reality
  "What's happening?"               "Asks no questions."
Acknowledges Reality              Fights Reality
  "This is the way it is."          "That's not how I see it."
Owns It                           Blames
  "If it's to be, it's up to me!"   "If everyone would just do their job!"
Finds Solution                    Personal Excuses
  "What can I do?"                  "It's not my job."
Gets On With It                   Waits & Hopes
  "O.K., let's do it!"              "If it was meant to be, it'll happen."
```

**Tale of two managers (pp. 159–161).** Same crisis, two responses. One seeks reality and owns the outcome; the other fights reality and blames the team. The first manager's team trusts her with the next challenge; the second manager's team disengages.

> "Outcomes are information you can use to frame better actions to get better outcomes." (p. 162)

### Find a coach

**Ericsson again (pp. 163–164):**
> "The single most important difference between these amateurs and the three groups of elite performers is that the future elite performers seek out teachers and coaches and engage in supervised training, whereas the amateurs rarely engage in similar types of practice."

**Matthews again (p. 163):** Written goals = 39.5% more likely. Written goals + weekly progress reports to a friend = **76.7% more likely**. Accountability partners work.

## 5. Why the three commitments interlock

A 4-hour time block compounds into extraordinary results only if:

- (a) You are deliberately improving during the block — **mastery**.
- (b) You notice when your current model has plateaued and adopt a better one — **E→P**.
- (c) You own the outcomes and course-correct — **accountability**.

Miss any one and the block becomes either noise (a), a grind toward a ceiling (b), or blame-theater (c).

## 7. Anti-patterns

- **Accepting "good enough."** The OK Plateau (p. 156).
- **Staying on the E-path after hitting the natural ceiling.** Disappointment → resignation → "greener pastures" is a cycle, not a solution (p. 155).
- **Defending the current approach** when it isn't producing results (p. 159). Defense is a tell that you've dropped into the victim cycle.
- **Playing victim**: avoids reality, fights reality, blames, excuses, waits. Each step makes the next more likely (p. 160).
- **Going it alone.** Ericsson's research is unambiguous — elites all have coaches (p. 163).
- **Treating accountability as self-punishment.** It isn't. It's seeking reality + owning it + acting on it.

---

## 8. How to install the three commitments

1. **Name your ONE Thing's mastery horizon.** "In 10 years, who's the best at this?" Write it down.
2. **Audit your E-path ceilings.** Where have you hit a ceiling in the past 2 years and *stopped*? Pick one. What model could replace yours?
3. **Run a weekly accountability loop.**
   - Monday: write the ONE Thing for the week.
   - Daily: mark the red X on the Seinfeld chain.
   - Friday: send a written progress report to a peer/coach. Include: what I did, what I didn't, what reality I'm seeking, what I'll change next week.
4. **Hire a coach.** Or build a peer-coaching pair with someone pursuing their own ONE Thing.

## 9. Keller's personal example (p. 212, about-the-author)

Gary's definition of leadership:
> "Teaching people how to think the way they need to think so they can do what they need to do when they need to do it, so they can get what they want when they want it."

That is the Path of Mastery expressed in coaching form. The three commitments operate at both self-level and team-level.
