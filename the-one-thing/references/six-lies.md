# The Six Lies Between You and Success (Part 1, Chapters 4–9)

Keller names six beliefs that masquerade as productivity advice but drive you the wrong way (p. 26). Each gets a chapter. All page citations from Keller & Papasan, 2013.

---

## Lie #1 — Everything Matters Equally (Ch 4, pp. 27–34)

**The truth.** "Equality is a lie" (p. 27). The majority of results come from a minority of actions (p. 31).

**The mechanism.** When everything feels urgent, you react instead of decide. "Pinballing through our day like a confused character in a B-horror movie, we end up running up the stairs instead of out the front door" (p. 28). To-do lists raw are **survival lists** — they "inherently lack the intent of success" (p. 29). Replace them with **success lists**, "purposefully created around extraordinary results" (p. 29).

**The tool — Pareto → Extreme Pareto.**
- Vilfredo Pareto (19th century) — 80% of Italian land owned by 20% of people.
- Joseph M. Juran cracked a GM coding machine (late 1930s), later codified "Pareto's Principle" in his *Quality Control Handbook* (pp. 30–31).
- Richard Koch, *The 80/20 Principle* — "the minority of your effort leads to the majority of your results."
- **Extreme Pareto**: take 20% of 20% of 20% recursively until **one item** remains (p. 32, Fig. 5).

**Case studies.**
- Keller's 2001 executive team brainstormed 100 ideas → narrowed to 10 → to ONE: write a book to become an elite performer. That book sold over a million copies (p. 33).
- Eric Johnson taught Keller guitar with 20 minutes/day available — practice only the minor blues scale; that scale would unlock the rest (p. 33).

**Verbatim copy.**
- "Achievers always work from a clear sense of priority." (p. 29)
- "Doing the most important thing is always the most important thing." (p. 34)
- "Go small. Go extreme. Say no." (p. 34)
- "Activity is often unrelated to productivity, and busyness rarely takes care of business." (p. 28)

**Anti-patterns.** Treating the to-do list as priority; tackling email in receive order; the "check-off game."

## Lie #2 — Multitasking (Ch 5, pp. 36–43)

**The truth.** "Multitasking is a lie" (p. 36). You cannot focus on two things at once — what we call multitasking is **rapid task-switching**, and each switch costs time and accuracy.

**The mechanism.** Computers appear to multitask by alternating on one CPU. Humans do the same via task switching, which "exacts a cost few realize they're even paying" (p. 39).

**Studies cited.**
- **Clifford Nass (Stanford), 2009** — 262 students; high multitaskers were "suckers for irrelevancy"; "Multitaskers were just lousy at everything" (p. 36).
- **Dr. David Meyer (U. Michigan)** — task-switch cost 25% for simple tasks, 100%+ for complex ones (p. 39).
- Workers interrupted every 11 minutes; ~1/3 of the day is recovery time (p. 38).
- ~**28% of an average workday** is lost to multitasking ineffectiveness (p. 41).
- Workers change windows / check email ~**37 times per hour** (p. 41).
- Matt Richtel / *NYT* "Driven to Distraction" Pulitzer (2009): distracted driving = 16% of traffic fatalities, ~500K injuries/year; phone conversation = 40% focus cut, comparable to drunk driving (pp. 41–42).

**Verbatim copy.**
- "Multitasking is merely the opportunity to screw up more than one thing at a time." (Steve Uzzell, p. 37)
- "It's not that we have too little time to do all the things we need to do, it's that we feel the need to do too many things in the time we have." (p. 38)
- "Why would we ever tolerate multitasking when we're doing our most important work?" (p. 42)

**"Monkey Mind"** (Billy Collins, p. 38) — the brain's natural tendency to swing between thoughts. ~4,000 thoughts per day; change every 14 seconds.

**Anti-patterns.** Listing multitasking as a résumé strength; tolerating it for ordinary work while demanding undivided focus from surgeons/pilots; giving loved ones "segmented attention."

## Lie #3 — A Disciplined Life (Ch 6, pp. 44–50)

**The truth.** "We don't need any more discipline than we already have. We just need to direct and manage it a little better" (p. 44).

**The mechanism.** Success is a **short race, fueled by discipline just long enough for habit to kick in and take over** (p. 44). Habits cost much less energy to maintain than to start (p. 47, Fig. 7). "The hard stuff becomes habit, and habit makes the hard stuff easy" (p. 47).

**Study cited.**
- **University College London, 2009** — habit formation averages **66 days** (range 18–254). Refutes the self-help "21 days" claim (p. 48).
- **Megan Oaten & Ken Cheng** — acquiring one positive habit creates a **halo effect**: less stress, better diet, less TV, less impulse buying (p. 49).

**Case study.** **Michael Phelps** — ADHD child who "could never focus." Coach Bob Bowman since age 11. 22 Olympic medals, 18 gold, most-decorated Olympian ever. Trained 7 days/week, 365/year from age 14 — one habit, relentlessly (pp. 45–46).

**Verbatim copy.**
- "Success is about doing the right thing, not about doing everything right." (p. 45)
- "When you do the right thing, it can liberate you from having to monitor everything." (p. 46)
- "If you are what you repeatedly do, then achievement isn't an action you take but a habit you forge into your life." (p. 50)

**Anti-patterns.** Trying to be a "disciplined person" across every domain; building multiple new habits at once; quitting before ~66 days.

## Lie #4 — Willpower Is Always on Will-Call (Ch 7, pp. 51–63)

**The truth.** "Willpower is always on will-call is a lie" (p. 52). Willpower is a limited, depletable resource with a daily battery.

**The mechanism.** "Willpower is like a fast-twitch muscle that gets tired and needs rest. It's incredibly powerful, but it has no endurance" (p. 56). The prefrontal cortex consumes 1/5 of our calories despite being 1/50 of body mass; it's "last in, first out" under fuel stress (p. 56).

**When willpower runs out, you revert to default settings.**
> "What are your default settings? When your most important work is done while your willpower wanes, default will define your level of achievement. Average is often the result." (p. 60)

**Studies cited.**
- **Walter Mischel Marshmallow Test** (Stanford Bing Nursery, late '60s–early '70s, 500+ children). "High delayers" had SAT scores ~210 points higher; "low delayers" 30% more likely to be overweight with higher drug addiction rates (pp. 54–55).
- **Baba Shiv (Stanford)** — 165 undergraduates memorizing 2-digit vs 7-digit numbers; 7-digit group nearly 2× as likely to choose chocolate cake over fruit salad (pp. 55–56).
- **2007 *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*** — 9 studies on willpower + nutrition; sugar (Kool-Aid) vs. placebo (Splenda); placebo group ~2× errors (pp. 56–57).
- **Levav, Avnaim-Pesso & Danziger parole study** — 1,112 Israeli parole hearings, 8 judges, 10 months (40% of Israel's parole decisions). Favorable-decision rate ~65% after breaks → near 0% at end of session (pp. 58–59, Fig. 8).

**The rule (p. 63).** "Do your most important work — your ONE Thing — early, before your willpower is drawn down."

**What taxes willpower** (p. 62): implementing new behaviors, filtering distractions, resisting temptations, suppressing emotion, restraining aggression, suppressing impulses, taking tests, trying to impress, coping with fear, doing something you don't enjoy, selecting long-term over short-term rewards.

**Verbatim copy.**
- "Willpower has a limited battery life but can be recharged with some downtime." (p. 53)
- "When you have your will, you get your way." (p. 53)
- "There is willpower and there is 'won't' power." (p. 61)
- "Don't spread your willpower too thin. Monitor your fuel gauge. Time your task." (p. 63)

**Fuel.** Foods that elevate blood sugar evenly — complex carbs, proteins — fuel high achievers (p. 58).

## Lie #5 — A Balanced Life (Ch 8, pp. 64–74)

**The truth.** "A balanced life is a lie" (p. 64). Balance is a myth. The truth is **counterbalancing**.

**The mechanism.** Nothing ever achieves absolute balance. "Extraordinary results require focused attention and time. Time on one thing means time away from another. This makes balance impossible" (p. 65). The magic lives at the extremes, not the middle (p. 67, Fig. 10).

**But extremes are also dangerous.** "Time waits for no one. Push something to an extreme and postponement can become permanent" (p. 69). Two cautionary stories: the schoolteacher who died before retirement with a closet full of unused fabric, and the businessman who sold his company and tried to make up lost time with his now-grown children (p. 68).

**Counterbalance — the two types** (pp. 71–72, Fig. 11, Fig. 12):
- **Between work & personal life: go LONG.** Acceptable to be extended out of balance in the work bucket.
- **Within personal life: go SHORT.** Avoid long stretches out of balance in family/health/friends/integrity.

**Patterson's five balls (pp. 71–72).** James Patterson, in *Suzanne's Diary for Nicholas*: work is rubber (bounces); family, health, friends, integrity are **glass** (shatter).

**Stats cited.**
- LexisNexis: "work-life balance" mentions in top 100 newspapers/magazines rose from 32 (1986–1996) to **1,674 in 2007 alone** (pp. 65–66, Fig. 9). The phrase coined mid-1980s when >50% of married women joined the workforce.
- 11-year British civil servant study (~7,100 people): >11 hours/day (55+ hour week) = **67% more likely heart disease** (pp. 70–71).

**The ballerina en pointe.** Looks weightless — actually constant micro-adjustments. "Counterbalancing done well gives the illusion of balance" (p. 70).

**Verbatim copy.**
- "Balance doesn't exist." (p. 64)
- "Magic happens at the extremes." (p. 67)
- "An extraordinary life is a counterbalancing act." (p. 74)

**Anti-patterns.** Chasing the middle; long out-of-balance stretches in *personal* life; compartmentalizing work and life instead of counterbalancing.

## Lie #6 — Big Is Bad (Ch 9, pp. 75–94)

**The truth.** "Big is bad is a lie" (p. 76). "It's quite possibly the worst lie of all, for if you fear big success, you'll either avoid it or sabotage your efforts to achieve it."

**The mechanism.** People associate big with *hard, complicated, time-consuming, overwhelming, intimidating* (p. 77). **Carol Dweck (Stanford)** showed that **growth mindset** outperforms **fixed mindset** by 4 decades of research (p. 91). "Mindsets can and do change."

**The rule.** "Don't fear big. Fear mediocrity. Fear waste. Fear the lack of living to your fullest." (p. 93)

**Double-down exercise (p. 93).** "If your goal is ten, ask: How can I reach 20? Set a goal so far above what you want that you'll be excited to figure out what you've got to do to make it happen."

**Case study.** **Scott Forstall (Apple iPhone team).** Forstall interviewed superstar engineers from inside Apple. Those who immediately jumped at his pitch — "make mistakes and struggle, but eventually do something we'll remember the rest of our lives" — were growth-minded. They built the iPhone (p. 91).

**Apple's 1997 "Think Different"** — Ali, Dylan, Einstein, Hitchcock, Picasso, Gandhi. "People who are crazy enough to think they can change the world are the only ones who do." (p. 93)

**Verbatim copy.**
- "Big is bad is a lie."
- "Only living big will let you experience your true life and work potential." (p. 93)
- "If courage isn't the absence of fear, but moving past it, then thinking big isn't the absence of doubts, but moving past them." (p. 93)
- "Don't let small thinking cut your life down to size. Think big, aim high, act bold." (p. 94)
- "Extraordinary results aren't built solely on extraordinary results. They're built on failure too." (p. 94)

**Anti-patterns.** Incremental thinking ("What do I do next?") — "at best the slow lane to success, at worst the off-ramp" (p. 93). Ordering from the existing menu. Fear of failure preventing bold action.

## Combined: reading the six lies as one model

Each lie exists because the *opposite* is the intuitive belief. To live the ONE Thing you unlearn the six:

1. Stop treating every item as equally important → **Extreme Pareto**.
2. Stop multitasking → **one focus at a time**.
3. Stop trying to be "disciplined" → **one selected habit, ~66 days**.
4. Stop treating willpower as infinite → **schedule ONE Thing first**.
5. Stop chasing balance → **counterbalance: long at work, short in personal**.
6. Stop fearing big → **growth mindset; double the goal**.

These six are the anti-patterns most likely to surface in any productivity or strategy conversation. Naming them is often enough to shift the frame.
