# Process — Writing a Strategy from Scratch

An end-to-end procedure for producing a strategy that survives Rumelt's audit. Each step has a worksheet prompt; complete the step only when the prompt can be answered in one sentence.

## Step 1 — Comprehend the Situation

Spend the majority of your effort here. "The most common cause of bad strategy is a weak diagnosis." ([Admired Leadership](https://admiredleadership.com/book-summaries/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Worksheet:**
- List every concern raised about the business in the last 90 days.
- For each, ask: cause or symptom?
- Cluster causes. Find the structural feature that makes most of the others downstream.

## Step 2 — Write the Diagnosis in One Sentence

"The challenge is ___."

Rules:
- One sentence, not a paragraph.
- Names a structural feature, not a symptom.
- Explicit about what it demotes.
- Uncomfortable.

If you can't compress to one sentence, loop back to Step 1.

## Step 3 — Choose the Guiding Policy

"Given that diagnosis, our approach is to ___."

Rules:
- Directional — points to a kind of approach, not a list.
- Rules things out.
- Leverages at least one source of power (focus, dynamics, chain-link, advantage, inertia, leverage, proximate objectives, design, or growth-from-capability).

## Step 4 — Name the Source(s) of Power

For the guiding policy, name which of the nine sources it exploits. If none, the policy is just a preference — redesign.

Stack 2–3 sources for durability. Beware "Growth" alone — that's a goal, not a source.

## Step 5 — Design Coherent Actions

"These are the 3–5 things we will do:"

Rules (each action):
- Directly carries out the guiding policy.
- Reinforces at least one other action.
- Feasible with existing resources in the next 12 months.
- Passes the "remove it and see what breaks" test.

If any action stands alone, it either joins the chain or gets cut.

## Step 6 — State What You Will Not Do

"We are explicitly choosing not to ___."

If nothing is given up, no choice has been made — Rumelt's universal-buy-in red flag. ([Admired Leadership](https://admiredleadership.com/book-summaries/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

## Step 7 — Audit Against the Four Hallmarks

Run through [bad-strategy-hallmarks.md](bad-strategy-hallmarks.md):
1. **Fluff?** Strip buzzwords — does non-obvious content remain?
2. **Facing the challenge?** Does one sentence name the obstacle?
3. **Goals ≠ strategy?** Strip outcome sentences — is there a plan left?
4. **Objectives coherent?** 3–5 prioritized items with causal mechanisms?

Fix any failure before shipping.

## Step 8 — Stress-Test for Competitor Response

- Who is the most dangerous competitor?
- Why can't they respond within 12 months? (Inertia, footprint, legacy, margin structure, organizational constraint.)
- What happens if they do respond? What's Plan B?

If you cannot articulate the competitor's inability to respond, you have no time window.

## Step 9 — Compress to One Page

The finished strategy fits on one page:

```
THE CHALLENGE: [one sentence]

OUR GUIDING POLICY: [one to two sentences]

WE EXPLOIT THESE SOURCES OF POWER: [2–3 named]

COHERENT ACTIONS:
1. [action — reinforces actions 2 and 4]
2. [action — reinforces actions 1 and 3]
3. [action — reinforces action 2]
4. [action — reinforces action 1]

WE WILL NOT: [one explicit sacrifice]

COMPETITOR CANNOT RESPOND BECAUSE: [one sentence]
```

If it doesn't fit on a page, it's not sharp enough. Return to Step 2.

## Step 10 — Review Cadence

Rumelt's point on dynamics and entropy: strategies decay. Reopen the diagnosis every 6–12 months. Ask:
- Is the challenge still the same?
- Has the wave we are riding crested?
- Has competitor inertia lifted?
- Has our own entropy degraded the actions?

Update, don't replace. A good strategy is a living artifact.
