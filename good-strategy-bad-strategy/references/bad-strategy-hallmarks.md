# The Four Hallmarks of Bad Strategy

> "Bad strategy is not the absence of strategy. Bad strategy can actively work against sound strategic analysis."
> — Rumelt, [McKinsey Quarterly, 2011](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy)

Bad strategy is a pathology with four recognizable signatures. Memorize them and you have a pre-built audit checklist.

## Hallmark 1 — Fluff

**Rumelt's definition:** "Fluff is a superficial statement of the obvious combined with a generous sprinkling of buzzwords that masquerade as expertise." ([BRG](https://www.brg.co.za/hallmarks-bad-strategy/))

**Diagnostic:** Strip every buzzword. If nothing non-obvious remains, it is fluff.

**Example (retail bank):** "Our fundamental strategy is one of customer-centric intermediation." Decoded: a bank takes deposits and makes loans. The sentence is a tautology wearing a suit. ([Business2Community](https://www.business2community.com/strategy/do-you-know-the-difference-between-a-good-strategy-and-a-bad-strategy-part-ii-fluff-0135466))

**Modern fluff bingo:** "leverage synergies," "world-class platform," "customer-obsessed innovation," "strategic alignment," "digital-first mindset."

**Fix:** Force every claim to pass the "competitor test": would a direct competitor disagree? If every competitor would say the same sentence, it says nothing.

## Hallmark 2 — Failure to Face the Challenge

**Rumelt:** "A strategy is a way through a difficulty, an approach to overcoming an obstacle, a response to a challenge. If the challenge is not defined, it is difficult or impossible to assess the quality of the strategy." ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy))

**Diagnostic:** Point to the sentence that names the obstacle. If you can't, the document is not a strategy.

**Example — International Harvester, 1979:** The corporate strategic plan amalgamated five divisional plans projecting market-share gains (ag equipment 16% → 20%). "The plan didn't even mention Harvester's grossly inefficient production facilities…or the fact that Harvester had the worst labor relations in US industry." A six-month strike followed; the company was dismembered. ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy))

**Why leaders avoid it:** Naming the challenge names who owns it. Political cost.

**Fix:** Require any strategy document to open with one sentence beginning "The challenge is…" — and reject the document if that sentence only restates a goal.

## Hallmark 3 — Mistaking Goals for Strategy

**Rumelt:** "Simply being ambitious is not a strategy. A good strategy does more than urge us forward toward a goal or vision. A good strategy honestly acknowledges the challenges being faced and provides an approach to overcoming them." ([BRG](https://www.brg.co.za/hallmarks-bad-strategy/))

**Diagnostic:** "Strip out every sentence that only states a desired outcome. What plan is left?"

**Example:** "Our strategy is to double revenue by 2028." That is a goal. What is the *approach*? Through which segment? Riding which wave? Exploiting which competitor's inertia?

**Rumelt:** "Often what passes for strategy is really a goal or, at best, a tactic." ([Admired Leadership](https://admiredleadership.com/book-summaries/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Fix:** Separate ends (goals), ways (strategy), and means (actions) on the page. If any row is empty, the strategy isn't real.

## Hallmark 4 — Bad Strategic Objectives

**Rumelt:** "Bad strategic objectives are those that fail to address critical issues or that are impracticable." Two forms:

### 4a. Dog's-Dinner Objectives
"A long list of things to do, often mislabeled as 'strategies' or 'objectives,' is not a strategy. It is just a list of things to do." ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy))

**Tell:** 12+ unprioritized bullets labeled "strategic pillars."
**Fix:** Force the list to 3–5 with explicit rationale for the cuts.

### 4b. Blue-Sky Objectives
"A blue-sky objective is usually a simple restatement of the desired state of affairs or of the challenge. It skips over the annoying fact that no one has a clue as to how to get there." ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy))

**Tell:** "Be the #1 platform for X" — with no mechanism.
**Fix:** For every objective, write "by [doing Y, which exploits asymmetry Z]." If you can't, it's blue-sky.

## The Three Roots of Bad Strategy

Rumelt: bad strategy has three reliable causes.

### Root 1 — Unwillingness or inability to choose
"When leaders are unwilling or unable to make choices among competing values and parties, bad strategy is the consequence. Any strategy that has universal buy-in signals the absence of choice." ([Admired Leadership](https://admiredleadership.com/book-summaries/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

### Root 2 — Template-style planning
"The siren song of template-style strategy — filling in the blanks with vision, mission, values, and strategies… a one-size-fits-all substitute for the hard work of analysis and coordinated action." ([Admired Leadership](https://admiredleadership.com/book-summaries/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

### Root 3 — "New Thought" / motivational thinking
"The belief that all you need to succeed is a positive mental attitude… Ignoring negative outcomes does not set you up for success… It crowds out critical thinking." Rumelt notes that motivational speakers are a US management-circuit staple in a way they are not in Europe. ([Admired Leadership](https://admiredleadership.com/book-summaries/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

## Audit Checklist

Run these seven questions on any document presented as "strategy":

1. **Fluff test:** Does removing every buzzword leave non-obvious claims? (If no → fluff.)
2. **Challenge test:** Is there one sentence beginning "The challenge is…"? (If no → failure to face the challenge.)
3. **Goal-vs-strategy test:** Strip every outcome sentence. Is there a plan left? (If no → goals-as-strategy.)
4. **Dog's-dinner test:** Are there more than 5 unprioritized "strategic priorities"? (If yes → bad objectives.)
5. **Blue-sky test:** Does each objective have a causal mechanism? (If no → blue-sky.)
6. **Choice test:** Is anyone unhappy with this strategy? (If no → no choice was made.)
7. **Coherence test:** Do at least two actions reinforce each other? (If no → uncoordinated initiatives.)

5+/7 pass = real strategy. Below that = rework.
