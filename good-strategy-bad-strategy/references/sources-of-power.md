# The Nine Sources of Power

Good strategies produce disproportionate results because they apply one or more structural asymmetries — **sources of power**. Coherent action without a source of power is merely busy. Rumelt identifies nine ([Sachin Rekhi](https://www.sachinrekhi.com/p/good-strategy-bad-strategy-richard-rumelt); [Jeff Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/)).

## 1. Leverage

**Definition (Rumelt):** "Using the limited resources at hand to achieve the biggest result." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Mechanism:** Find an imbalance in the situation and exploit it for a disproportionately large payoff. Leverage comes from *anticipation* (seeing what others miss), *insight* (identifying a pivot point), and *concentration* (massing on that point).

**Case — Desert Storm / Schwarzkopf's "left hook":** Rather than attack Iraqi forces frontally, Schwarzkopf misdirected Saddam's attention, then flanked with concentrated armor against the weakest sector. The asymmetry was geographic and attentional. ([WebSearch result citing Rumelt](https://www.amazon.com/Good-Strategy-Bad-Difference-Matters/dp/0307886239))

**Application drill:** Identify the one place your competitor is weakest AND your resources land heaviest. If those two don't overlap, you don't have leverage yet — redesign.

## 2. Proximate Objectives

**Definition (Rumelt):** "Choose an objective that is close enough at hand to be feasible." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/)) "The more uncertain and dynamic the situation, the more proximate a strategic objective must be." ([WebSearch restatement](https://aydoo.services/en/articles/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Mechanism:** Name a target the organization can reasonably be expected to hit — even overwhelm. Proximate objectives absorb uncertainty and unlock subsequent moves.

**Case — Kennedy's moon landing:** Not "advance space technology" (blue-sky) but "land a man on the moon and return him safely in this decade" — feasible-at-the-edge, binary, single-organization-scope. ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Application drill:** Rewrite the goal so success/failure is knowable within 12 months and executable by existing teams. If you can't, the goal is blue-sky.

## 3. Chain-Link Systems

**Definition (Rumelt):** "A system has chain-link logic when its performance is limited by its weakest link." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/)) "Quality matters when quantity is an inadequate substitute."

**Mechanism:** In chain-link systems, partial improvements yield zero return — the weakest link bottlenecks everything. But once every link is excellent, the system becomes very hard to copy (because a competitor has to fix all links simultaneously).

**Case — IKEA:** Flat-pack design, logistics, store experience, product design, and supply-chain costs all reinforce each other. A competitor cannot copy one piece in isolation. ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Application drill:** Map your system as a chain. Name the current weakest link. Unless you fix it, investing in the other links is waste.

## 4. Design

**Definition (Rumelt):** "Good strategy is design — fitting various pieces together so they work as a coherent whole." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/)) "Strategic coordination, or coherence, is not ad hoc mutual adjustment. It is coherence imposed on a system by policy and design." ([WebSearch](https://aydoo.services/en/articles/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Mechanism:** Deliberate fit across components. Each piece makes the others more valuable.

**Case — Starbucks:** Vertical integration across sourcing, roasting, retail, and experience enabled "mutual adjustment of multiple elements." ([Blas](https://blas.com/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Application drill:** For each element of your offer/operation, list what it *requires from* and *contributes to* another element. Elements with no linkages should be cut or redesigned.

## 5. Focus

**Definition (Rumelt):** "Attacking a segment of the market with a product or service that delivers more value." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Mechanism:** Concentration of effort and product fit on a chosen segment produces an advantage that a generalist cannot match.

**Case — Nvidia:** Focus on 3D GPUs for PCs instead of a diffused multimedia-chip strategy. The narrow target let them lead on the next wave. ([Penguin](https://penguinrandomhousehighereducation.com/book/?isbn=9780307886231))

**Application drill:** Name the segment, the value delivered, and what you give up to deliver it. If the "segment" is "everyone," focus is missing.

## 6. Growth

**Definition (Rumelt):** "Growing the size of the business is not a strategy — it is the result." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/)) Real, profitable growth comes from increased demand for distinctive capabilities — not from the pursuit of size itself.

**Mechanism:** Network and scale effects kick in when distinctive capability compounds.

**Case — Wal-Mart:** Rumelt: "Kmart saw Wal-Mart like Goliath saw David — smaller and less experienced in the big leagues. The network replaced the store." Walton broke the old definition of a store by treating the network of stores as the unit of competition. ([Blas](https://blas.com/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Application drill:** What distinctive capability, if it had 10× more demand, would produce the growth you want? Are you investing in it, or in growth itself?

## 7. Using Advantage

**Definition (Rumelt):** The most basic advantage involves "producing at a lower cost than your competitors, or delivering more perceived value." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Mechanism:** Press the edge where you are asymmetrically strong; avoid contests of equals.

**Application drill:** Could a competitor replicate this advantage within the quarter? If yes, it's a feature, not an advantage.

## 8. Dynamics

**Definition (Rumelt):** "Waves of change that roll through an industry." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Mechanism:** Ride a transition (regulatory, technological, demographic, cost-curve). You are not trying to move the ocean; you are catching a wave already forming.

**Case — Nvidia:** 3D graphics was a wave; Nvidia rode it. ([Penguin](https://penguinrandomhousehighereducation.com/book/?isbn=9780307886231))

**Application drill:** Name the specific wave. When did it start? When will it crest? If you cannot name the wave, you are not using dynamics.

## 9. Inertia & Entropy

**Inertia (Rumelt):** "An organization's unwillingness or inability to adapt to changing circumstances." ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Entropy (Rumelt):** "Causes organizations to become less organized and less focused over time."

**Mechanism:** Your competitor's inertia and internal entropy are sources of power *for you*. Predictable non-response is an exploitable asymmetry.

**Cases:**
- **Netflix vs. Blockbuster** — Blockbuster's retail-footprint inertia made a mail/streaming pivot structurally impossible. ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))
- **GM** — long entropy of complexity, legacy commitments, organizational drift. ([Zych](https://jlzych.com/2018/06/27/notes-from-good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Application drill:** Name the specific reason your strongest competitor cannot or will not respond within 12 months. If you can't, your strategy has no time window.

## How to Apply the Nine Sources

1. **After the kernel is drafted, map actions to sources.** For each coherent action, tag the source(s) of power it exploits.
2. **Any action that taps zero sources is suspect.** Either it's not strategic, or the source is latent and should be named.
3. **The best strategies stack 2–3 sources** (e.g., Wal-Mart stacked Growth + Design + Inertia; Nvidia stacked Focus + Dynamics + Leverage).
4. **Beware "Growth" alone.** If the only named source is growth, the strategy is goal-dressed-as-strategy.
