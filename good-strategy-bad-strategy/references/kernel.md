# The Kernel of Good Strategy

> "The kernel of a strategy contains three elements: a diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent action."
> — Richard Rumelt, quoted in [Sachin Rekhi's summary](https://www.sachinrekhi.com/p/good-strategy-bad-strategy-richard-rumelt)

The kernel is Rumelt's minimum-viable definition of a strategy. Anything lacking any of the three elements is not a strategy, regardless of how much executive time and polish went into it.

## 1. Diagnosis

**What it is:** An explanation of the nature of the challenge. It simplifies the overwhelming complexity of a situation by identifying certain aspects as critical and demoting the rest to secondary.

**Rumelt's language:**
- "Explains the nature of the most pressing challenge the organization is facing." ([Rekhi](https://www.sachinrekhi.com/p/good-strategy-bad-strategy-richard-rumelt))
- "The most common cause of bad strategy is a weak diagnosis." ([Admired Leadership](https://admiredleadership.com/book-summaries/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**How a good diagnosis feels:**
- One sentence, not a paragraph.
- Names a structural feature, not a symptom.
- Demotes other concerns explicitly.
- Makes at least one senior person uncomfortable — because naming the crux means admitting what has been avoided.

**Example (Nvidia, 1990s):** "3D graphics is the future of PC computing and our multimedia-chip strategy puts us on the wrong side of that shift." Everything downstream followed from that sentence. ([Penguin summary](https://penguinrandomhousehighereducation.com/book/?isbn=9780307886231))

**Example of missing diagnosis (International Harvester 1979):** The corporate strategic plan projected market-share gains without mentioning grossly inefficient production facilities or the worst labor relations in US industry. When the predictable strike hit, there was no strategy to fall back on — because there had been no diagnosis. ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy))

**Drill — write the diagnosis:**
1. List every challenge the organization names in its current plan.
2. For each, ask: is this a cause or a symptom?
3. Find the single structural feature that, if solved, would make most of the others tractable.
4. Write it in one sentence. If you can't, you haven't found it yet.

## 2. Guiding Policy

**What it is:** The overall approach chosen to cope with or overcome the obstacles identified in the diagnosis. It channels action in certain directions without fully defining what the action is.

**Rumelt's analogy:** "Like the guardrails on a highway, the guiding policy directs and constrains action without fully defining its content." ([search summary of Rumelt](https://aydoo.services/en/articles/good-strategy-bad-strategy/))

**Good guiding policies:**
- Are **directional** — they point to a specific kind of approach (e.g., "compete on cycle time," "own the enterprise segment," "refuse fixed-price work").
- Make **trade-offs explicit** — they rule things out.
- **Anticipate** competitor and customer response.
- Leverage a **source of power** (focus, dynamics, advantage, etc. — see [sources-of-power.md](sources-of-power.md)).

**Example (Apple, 1998):** Upon Jobs' return, the guiding policy was radical simplification — product line cut to a two-by-two grid (consumer/pro × desktop/laptop), elimination of peripherals, and re-focus on design. Every action that followed served the policy. ([Rekhi](https://www.sachinrekhi.com/p/good-strategy-bad-strategy-richard-rumelt))

**Counter-example:** "Grow revenue 30% while increasing margin and launching in three new geographies." This is a goal set, not a guiding policy — it gives no direction for how to do any of it.

## 3. Coherent Action

**What it is:** Feasible, coordinated policies, resource commitments, and actions designed to carry out the guiding policy.

**Rumelt:** "Coordination provides the most basic leverage or advantage in strategy." ([Rekhi](https://www.sachinrekhi.com/p/good-strategy-bad-strategy-richard-rumelt))

**The coherence test — each action must:**
- Directly carry out the guiding policy.
- Reinforce at least one other action (not merely sit on the same list).
- Be feasible given real resources.
- Survive the "remove it and see what breaks" test.

**Example (Nvidia):** GPU-only focus, three parallel design teams on staggered schedules, 6-month product cadence, and DirectX alignment. Each action made the others more valuable: cadence required parallel teams; parallel teams justified focus; focus enabled DirectX lead. ([Penguin summary](https://penguinrandomhousehighereducation.com/book/?isbn=9780307886231))

**Dog's-dinner counter-example:** A 14-item "strategic priorities" list where items 3 and 9 contradict each other and items 5–8 share no common thread. ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy))

## Worked Example: Audit a Kernel

Given this plan: *"Our strategy is to become the leading customer-centric platform in our category by delivering exceptional value, driving operational excellence, and investing in our people."*

- **Diagnosis?** None. No obstacle named.
- **Guiding policy?** None. "Customer-centric" and "operational excellence" are buzzwords with no direction.
- **Coherent action?** None. Three abstractions that could appear in any company's deck.

This is fluff (Hallmark 1) plus failure to face the challenge (Hallmark 2) plus mistaking goals for strategy (Hallmark 3). Send it back.
